# PROLOGUE: KOLLOCK STREET

The kind of street where everyone knew which dog barked at what time, which car belonged to whose cousin, which screen door squeaked loud enough to wake the neighbors.

Someone screamed just after seven a.m.

The sound cut through the morning stillness—high, sharp, unmistakably human. Mrs. Eleanor Hastings, seventy-one years old, had been retrieving her newspaper from the driveway when she saw it. Saw *her*. The body lay beside a home on Kollock Street—not inside, but outside, in the narrow strip of grass between the house and the property line.

Eleanor would later tell police she'd thought it was a pile of clothes at first. A trick of the early light. But then she'd seen the hair. The skin. The way the body was positioned.

She'd screamed.

The body was that of a young woman. Her clothes were torn. Her skin bore marks that told a story no one wanted to read. Strangulation. The medical examiner would confirm it later, but anyone who saw her knew. The bruises darkening her throat. The ligature marks pressed deep into flesh. The way her eyes were half-open, staring at nothing.

Sandra Kaye Davis. Twenty-two years old.

Her face was turned toward the morning sun, eyes fixed on the gold-painted sky. She looked like she might be sleeping if not for everything else—the bruises, the torn clothing, the unnatural stillness. Whoever did this hadn't even tried to hide her. She'd been left in the open, visible from the street, as if her life had mattered so little that concealment wasn't worth the effort.

The Waycross Police Department arrived within minutes. Officers who'd grown up in this town, who knew these streets, who'd never worked a murder like this. Ware County didn't get many homicides. The occasional bar fight that went too far, domestic violence that ended in tragedy, but not this. Not a young woman strangled and sexually assaulted and left beside someone's home like discarded trash.

By eight a.m., the Georgia Bureau of Investigation was on the phone. By nine, agents were on the way. Yellow crime scene tape went up around the house. Neighbors gathered at a distance, arms crossed, whispering behind hands. Who would do something like this? Did anyone see anything? Was she from around here?

Yes. Sandra was from Waycross. Twenty-two years old, a whole life ahead of her. Someone's daughter. Someone's friend. Someone who'd been alive yesterday—breathing, thinking, planning, hoping—and would never be alive again.

The heat pressed down as the sun climbed higher. Detectives photographed the scene from every angle, the camera clicks punctuating the morning quiet. Evidence markers went down—yellow numbered triangles beside torn fabric, beside disturbed grass, beside anything that might tell the story of what happened here. They collected everything. Fibers. Hair. Soil samples. And DNA—semen, the kind of biological evidence that in 1984 represented the cutting edge of forensic science, though no one yet understood how revolutionary it would become.

The body went to the medical examiner before noon. Autopsy scheduled. Cause of death obvious but needing official confirmation. Every procedure documented, every sample labeled, every chain of custody form signed. They knew this evidence might be the key to finding whoever did this.

What they didn't know was how long it would take.

The samples went to the crime lab. Reports came back weeks later: DNA present, profile inconclusive with current technology. No matches in any database, because the databases barely existed. No immediate suspects. The investigation continued through September, into October, through the fall. Interviews. Tips. Dead ends.

By Christmas 1984, the case was cooling. By Sandra's birthday in 1985—she would have turned twenty-three—it was cold.

The DNA samples went into storage. Carefully preserved, properly documented, sealed in evidence bags and labeled with dates and case numbers. Waiting for the day when science would catch up to justice. Waiting for technology that didn't exist yet. Waiting for a database that hadn't been built. Waiting for methods that hadn't been invented.

They would wait four decades.

Longer than Sandra had been alive. Longer than most marriages last. Longer than some people live. Long enough for a newborn baby to grow up, graduate college, start a career, have children of their own. Long enough for the world to change completely—the Soviet Union to fall, the internet to rise, cell phones to go from science fiction to pocket-sized supercomputers.

But on that September morning in 1984, no one knew any of that yet.

They just knew a young woman was dead.

And someone in Waycross had killed her.
