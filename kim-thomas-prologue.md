# PROLOGUE: The Call

The house was too quiet. That's what Ed Friedland would remember later—the absence of sound. No music from Kim's stereo. No baby babbling from the nursery. Just silence, thick and wrong, the moment he opened the front door.

He'd left his reading glasses on the nightstand. It was the kind of oversight that punctuated a surgeon's morning—lunch still hours away, but already he was halfway through rounds when he realized the patient charts might as well have been hieroglyphics without them. The drive home took twelve minutes from Presbyterian Hospital. Twelve minutes of July heat already climbing toward ninety, the kind of Charlotte morning that made the air feel solid.

Churchill Road was quiet the way suburban streets always were on a Friday mid-morning. Mrs. Henderson next door watering her azaleas. Someone's sprinkler ticking across a lawn three houses down. The Cherokee roses Kim had planted last spring climbing the mailbox post, white petals already browning at the edges.

The front door swung open before his key even turned.

Unlocked.

Ed stopped, hand on the knob. Kim was meticulous about locks. Compulsive, almost. Dead bolt, chain, even during the day when she was home. It was one of the small battles they'd given up fighting—his easy California childhood versus her practical urban wariness. She'd grown up in neighborhoods where you locked your doors.

"Kim?"

Nothing.

He stepped inside. The cool rush of air conditioning. The grandfather clock in the hallway marking 10:47. Still nothing.

"Kim?"

The nursery door was open at the end of the hall. He could see the edge of the crib, the mobile he'd hung last week still turning lazy circles. No movement. No sound.

His heart kicked once, hard.

Then the crying started.

Not the fussy, irritable cry of a baby who'd missed a nap. This was raw. Desperate. The sound ripped through the silence like something primal and terrified, and Ed's legs were moving before his brain caught up.

He took the hallway in four strides. The nursery door banged against the wall. Their daughter stood in the crib, hands clutching the rail, face red and tear-streaked. When she saw him she screamed louder, reaching with grasping fingers.

"Hey, hey—"

He scooped her up, her small body rigid with distress. She buried her face in his shoulder and wailed, and that's when the smell hit him. Not just the sour milk tang of spit-up or the sweet rot of a diaper that needed changing. Something else. Metallic and thick.

Blood.

The baby's nursery opened onto the master bedroom. The door stood ajar.

Ed's hand was shaking as he pushed it wider.

Kim lay on the floor beside the bed.

For one impossible moment, his brain refused to process what he was seeing. His wife. Her back to him. Arms twisted at a wrong angle, hands pulled together. Silver glint of handcuffs catching the light from the window.

Blood.

So much blood it had soaked into the rug, dark and spreading, the cream fibers turned rust-brown. A pool of it beneath her throat.

"Kim—"

He was across the room without remembering moving, the baby still screaming in his arms. He dropped to his knees beside her. Her eyes were open. Fixed on nothing. Her throat—

He turned away, stomach heaving, pressed his palm over his mouth. The baby's cries filled the room, filled his skull, and he couldn't breathe, couldn't think, couldn't make sense of the world tilting sideways.

Phone. He needed a phone.

He stumbled back into the nursery, fingers numb as he fumbled for the receiver on the dresser. Punched 911 with a shaking hand. The baby thrashed against him, tiny fists beating his chest.

"911, what's your emergency?"

"My wife—" His voice cracked. "She's—I think she's dead. Someone killed her."

"Sir, what's your location?"

He gave the address. Churchill Road. Cotswold. Southeast Charlotte. The words came automatic, clinical, the way he'd been trained to speak during codes at the hospital. Detached. Professional. Even as his world collapsed into the raw animal sound coming from his daughter's mouth.

"Is the attacker still in the house, sir?"

Ed's eyes snapped to the bedroom door. The hallway beyond. The front door he'd walked through minutes ago, standing open to the summer heat.

"I don't—I don't know. I don't think so."

"Can you check if she's breathing?"

"She's not. Her throat—" He stopped. Couldn't say it. "She's gone."

"Units are on the way, sir. Stay on the line with me."

He sank onto the nursery floor, back against the wall, baby clutched to his chest. Through the open door he could see Kim's body. The unnatural stillness. The blood spreading into patterns on the rug he'd helped her pick out at that antique store in Davidson. She'd loved that rug. Pale cream with delicate blue flowers.

The flowers were gone now. Drowned in red.

Sirens rose in the distance. Someone's dog started barking. Mrs. Henderson's voice floated through the open front door—"Ed? Is everything okay?"—and he wanted to laugh, wanted to scream, because nothing would ever be okay again.

The baby had stopped crying. She lay against him now, hiccupping small gasping breaths, exhausted. Her tiny hand clutched his shirt collar. She smelled like powder and milk and innocence, and she had no idea. Didn't know her mother was gone. Didn't know the world had just tilted off its axis.

Tires screeched outside. Voices shouting. Heavy footsteps pounding up the walkway.

"Sir? Sir, are you still there?"

"I'm here," Ed whispered.

But he wasn't. Not really. Some essential part of him had stopped the moment he pushed open that bedroom door. Stopped, and would stay frozen in that terrible silence for years to come.

The first officer burst through the front door, hand on his weapon. "Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police!"

"In here," Ed called, voice hollow. "The nursery."

They found him like that. Orthopedic surgeon. New father. Widower. Sitting on the floor with his daughter in his arms, staring at nothing.

Staring at everything.

The questions would start soon. The investigation. The suspicion. He didn't know it yet—couldn't imagine it—but in four years they would charge him with her murder. In seven years a civil jury would name the real killer. And in thirty-five years, DNA science would finally prove what everyone should have known from the beginning.

But in that moment, in the nursery with the mobile still turning overhead and his daughter's tears soaking through his shirt, Ed Friedland knew only one thing with absolute certainty:

The monster who did this had walked out their front door and into the July heat. Had left it unlocked. Had left a baby screaming in her crib and a mother bleeding on a cream rug with blue flowers.

And he was still out there.

It would be thirty-five years before anyone knew who had killed her. And when the truth came, it would reveal a monster who lived five minutes away—and had been named as a suspect from the very beginning.
