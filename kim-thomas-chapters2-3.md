# BOOK #4: THE INVESTIGATION + THE WRONG MAN
## Kim Thomas Murder, Charlotte, NC (1990-2026)

---

## CHAPTER 2: THE INVESTIGATION

### July 27, 1990 — Morning

The 911 call came in at 10:47 AM.

A man's voice, shaking: "My wife—I think she's dead. Please, oh God, please send someone."

The dispatcher tried to keep him on the line, but the sound that came through wasn't words anymore. Just the raw animal sound of someone whose world had just ended.

Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police arrived at the Churchill Road home seven minutes later. The neighborhood was quiet, middle-class Cotswold, the kind of place where people mowed their lawns on Saturday and waved to neighbors. Not the kind of place where women got murdered on a Friday morning.

The officers found Dr. Ed Friedland in the driveway, unable to go back inside. His hands were shaking. He kept saying the same thing: "The baby. Is the baby okay?"

Inside, they found Kim Thomas Friedland on the floor of the bedroom.

Thirty-two years old. Hands cuffed behind her back. Throat slashed.

The blood loss was massive. The brutality was immediate and obvious. This wasn't an accident, wasn't a burglary gone wrong. Someone had come into this house with a specific purpose: to kill Kim Thomas.

In the next room, their ten-month-old daughter was crying in her crib, unharmed. She'd been there the whole time, a few feet away, while her mother died.

---

### The Scene

The detective who caught the case was a twenty-year veteran named Bill Thompson. He'd seen plenty of murders, but home invasions in broad daylight in Cotswold were rare. This wasn't gang violence or a drug deal gone bad. This was someone's home, someone's Friday morning.

The first thing he noticed: no forced entry.

The doors were locked. The windows were intact. Whoever killed Kim Thomas had either been let inside, or had a key. That fact alone changed everything. It meant Kim knew her killer, or at least trusted them enough to open the door.

The second thing: nothing was taken.

Kim's purse was on the kitchen counter, wallet inside, credit cards untouched. Her jewelry was on the dresser. The TV, the electronics—all there. Whoever did this didn't come for money.

The handcuffs were the detail that bothered Thompson the most. They weren't police-issue—he could tell that immediately. Civilian cuffs, the kind you could buy at any adult store or online. Someone had brought them specifically for this. This was planned.

The murder weapon—a knife, likely—was gone. The killer had taken it with them, which suggested enough presence of mind to avoid leaving evidence. This wasn't a crime of passion. This was controlled violence.

And then there was the DNA.

The crime scene techs collected everything they could: blood samples, fibers from the rug beside Kim's body, trace evidence from the handcuffs, swabs from doorknobs and surfaces. In 1990, DNA testing was still relatively new, still expensive, still limited. They collected it because protocol demanded it, but no one was holding their breath for results. The technology just wasn't there yet.

---

### The Husband

In any domestic murder, the spouse is the first suspect. Statistics don't lie: women killed at home are most often killed by their husbands or boyfriends. So Ed Friedland, despite his obvious grief, despite the 911 call, became Suspect Number One.

Thompson interviewed him that same day. Ed was an orthopedic surgeon, well-respected, no criminal record. He'd left for work that morning around 7:30 AM. Kim was alive, making breakfast, playing with the baby. Everything was normal.

He'd returned around 10:30 AM—he'd forgotten some patient files—and found her.

His alibi checked out. The hospital confirmed he'd been in surgery from 8:00 to 10:00 AM. Co-workers had seen him. The timeline didn't work.

Ed volunteered for a polygraph. He passed.

When the DNA results came back weeks later, Ed's DNA was exactly where you'd expect it to be—on his wife's body, on the sheets, on her clothes. But the DNA on the rug beside her body, the DNA that suggested a struggle, that suggested the killer had been close—it wasn't his.

Still, Thompson couldn't shake the husband. No forced entry meant Kim had let someone in. Who would she let in at 8 AM on a Friday? A friend? A neighbor? Someone she knew?

Or her husband, who already had a key?

---

### August 24, 1990

A month after the murder, a patrol officer filed a routine report. It mentioned a name: Marion Gales.

The context was thin—a tip, maybe, or a neighborhood sighting. The report didn't say much, just that Gales' name had come up in connection with the Churchill Road area. It was one of dozens of leads pouring in after Kim's death. Neighbors reporting suspicious cars, strange men, anyone who looked out of place.

The report was filed. And then, like so many leads in the early chaos of an investigation, it disappeared into the stack.

No one followed up. No one ran a background check on Marion Gales. No one discovered that he lived in Grier Heights, a five-and-a-half-minute walk from Churchill Road. No one found out that sixteen years earlier, at age sixteen, Gales had shot and wounded a woman on *the exact same street* where Kim Thomas now lay dead.

The report sat in a file cabinet. And Marion Gales walked free.

---

### The Stall

By the end of 1990, the investigation had hit a wall.

The DNA evidence was collected, but the technology to analyze it fully didn't exist yet. The samples were degraded, mixed, insufficient for the crude testing methods available at the time. The lab could tell them there was an unknown male contributor on the rug beside Kim's body, but they couldn't tell them *who*.

Ed Friedland remained the primary suspect, but the evidence wasn't there. His alibi held. The polygraph held. The DNA didn't match. Without a confession or a murder weapon, the DA wouldn't touch it.

The case went cold.

Kim's family held vigils. They kept her memory alive, kept pressure on CMPD, demanded answers. Her daughter—too young to remember her mother—grew up in the shadow of a murder no one could solve.

Ed tried to rebuild his life, but the cloud never lifted. People whispered. In Cotswold, in the hospital halls, in the grocery store—*he must have done it. How else do you explain it?*

And somewhere in Charlotte, Marion Gales went about his life. In and out of jail for petty crimes, assaults, drug charges—the usual rotation for a career criminal. He knew what had happened on Churchill Road. He'd been there. He'd felt the knife in his hand, heard Kim Thomas' last breath, walked out of that house and disappeared into the July heat.

And no one came for him.

Not yet.

## CHAPTER 3: THE WRONG MAN

### 1994 — The Arrest

Four years after Kim Thomas died, Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police made an arrest.

Dr. Ed Friedland was charged with first-degree murder.

The evidence was circumstantial at best. He'd found the body. He had a key to the house. There was no forced entry. The DNA testing was inconclusive—or more accurately, the technology wasn't advanced enough to definitively rule him in or out. To investigators under pressure to solve a high-profile murder, Ed was the only suspect who made sense.

The arrest sent shockwaves through Charlotte. Kim's friends and family were divided. Some believed the police had finally gotten their man. Others knew Ed—knew the way he'd loved Kim, the way he was raising their daughter alone, the devastation that still haunted him four years later—and couldn't believe he'd done it.

Ed's nightmare had begun.

---

### The Case Against Him

The prosecution's theory was simple: domestic violence. A marriage that looked perfect from the outside but was fractured within. A fight that escalated. A moment of rage. A husband who knew how to cover his tracks.

But the evidence didn't support it.

There was no history of abuse. Friends, family, coworkers—everyone who knew Kim and Ed described them as loving, stable, happy. Kim had been a vocal women's rights activist; if Ed had been abusive, someone would have known. Kim herself would have said something.

The polygraph? Inadmissible in court, but Ed had passed it. Polygraphs aren't perfect, but they're hard to beat when you're lying about murdering your wife.

The timeline? Ed had been in surgery when Kim died. Yes, the medical examiner's estimate of time of death had a two-hour window, but it was tight. Ed would have had to leave the hospital, drive home, murder his wife, clean up, and return—all without anyone noticing.

The DNA? That was the prosecution's biggest problem. The DNA on the rug beside Kim's body didn't match Ed. The testing technology in 1994 was better than it had been in 1990, and while it still couldn't identify the unknown male contributor, it could rule people out. And it ruled out Ed Friedland.

Still, the DA pushed forward. The case had gone unsolved for four years. The community wanted answers. Ed was the only suspect they had.

---

### The Nightmare

For Ed, the arrest was surreal. One moment he was a widower trying to raise his daughter and keep Kim's memory alive. The next, he was sitting in a jail cell, charged with killing the woman he'd loved.

The media coverage was brutal. Local news ran stories with Ed's mugshot next to Kim's smiling photo. *Husband Charged in Activist's Murder.* The implication was clear: he'd done it. The police had said so.

In Cotswold, neighbors stopped making eye contact. At the hospital, colleagues whispered. Parents at his daughter's daycare looked at him differently. He was no longer Ed, the grieving widower. He was Ed, the suspected killer.

His daughter was too young to understand, but old enough to feel the shift. She asked why people were staring. Why the reporters were outside their house. Why Daddy had to go to court.

Ed's lawyer—a public defender who knew a weak case when he saw one—pushed for dismissal. The evidence was circumstantial. The DNA didn't match. The timeline didn't work. The motive didn't exist.

But the wheels of justice move slowly. For months, Ed lived in limbo—free on bond, but tethered to a murder charge that could put him in prison for life. He couldn't work. He couldn't leave the state. He could barely function.

At night, lying awake, he thought about Kim. About the morning he'd found her. About the terror he'd felt, the guilt he still carried—*I should have been there. I should have protected her.*

And now people thought *he'd* done it. That *he'd* handcuffed his wife and slit her throat and left their baby crying in the next room.

The cruelty of it was almost unbearable.

---

### The Charges Dropped

In late 1994, the DA quietly dropped the charges.

There was no dramatic courtroom moment, no vindication speech. Just a terse statement to the press: *Insufficient evidence to proceed.*

Ed was free. Technically.

But the damage was done. His reputation was destroyed. People who'd believed he was innocent still looked at him strangely. *Maybe he got away with it. Maybe the DA just couldn't prove it.*

And the people who'd believed he was guilty? They were certain now. *Of course they dropped the charges. Rich doctor, good lawyer. That's how the system works.*

Ed tried to rebuild his life. He went back to work, threw himself into raising his daughter, kept Kim's memory alive through scholarships and activism in her name. But the cloud never lifted.

And the real question—the one that haunted him every day—remained unanswered: *Who killed Kim?*

---

### The Real Killer

While Ed Friedland was being arrested, tried in the court of public opinion, and ultimately released, Marion Anthony Gales was living his life.

He was still in Charlotte. Still in and out of jail for petty crimes. Still violent, still dangerous, still the same man who'd shot a woman on Churchill Road in 1974 and murdered another on the same street in 1990.

His family suspected. Years later, they'd tell investigators they'd thought all along that Marion had done it. The timing was right—he'd been in the area. The brutality fit his pattern. And there was something about the way he'd denied it, too quickly, too defensively, when Kim's murder came up in conversation.

But suspicion isn't evidence. And in the 1990s, without DNA technology advanced enough to process degraded samples, Marion Gales was untouchable.

He watched the news coverage of Ed Friedland's arrest with detached interest. Maybe relief. Maybe amusement. The police had the wrong man, and Marion knew it. He'd walked out of that house on Churchill Road, dropped the knife in a storm drain or a dumpster, gone home, and carried on with his life.

And no one came for him.

---

### 1995 — The Rediscovery

A year after Ed's charges were dropped, a cold case detective named Sarah Nguyen was assigned to the Kim Thomas file. She was new to homicide, hungry, methodical. She started from scratch.

And she found the August 24, 1990 police report.

Marion Anthony Gales. Name mentioned in connection with Churchill Road. No follow-up.

Nguyen ran a background check. What she found made her blood run cold.

Gales had a record going back to age sixteen. Assault. Robbery. Weapons charges. And in 1974—sixteen years before Kim's murder—he'd shot and wounded a woman *on Churchill Road.* The exact same street where Kim Thomas had died.

He lived in Grier Heights. A five-and-a-half-minute walk from the Friedland house.

In July 1990—the same month Kim was murdered—Gales had allegedly beaten his sister, Annie Roseboro. A pattern of violence against women. A pattern of rage.

Nguyen brought Gales in for questioning.

He denied everything. "I've never seen Miss Thomas before, or her husband," he said, calm and flat. "I don't know anything about it."

They collected his DNA. But the technology still wasn't there. The degraded samples from the crime scene rug couldn't be compared with enough certainty to charge him. The lab could say there was an unknown male contributor. They couldn't say it was Marion Gales.

Nguyen knew he'd done it. She could feel it. But knowing and proving are two different things.

The case stayed cold.

---

### 1997 — The Civil Trial

Ed Friedland had been cleared by the criminal justice system, but he'd never been exonerated in the eyes of the public. So in 1997, he did something unusual: he filed a wrongful death lawsuit against Marion Gales.

Civil trials have a lower burden of proof than criminal trials. In criminal court, you need evidence beyond a reasonable doubt. In civil court, you only need a preponderance of the evidence—51% likely. More likely than not.

Ed's lawyer laid out the case: Gales' proximity to the crime scene. His criminal history. His violence against women. The 1974 shooting on Churchill Road. The beating of his sister in July 1990. The timeline. The fact that police had named him in a 1990 report and never followed up.

Gales' defense was thin. He denied it. He had no alibi. He had no explanation for why his name had come up in the investigation.

The jury deliberated for six hours.

They found Marion Gales liable for the death of Kim Thomas Friedland.

The judgment: $8.6 million in damages.

Ed would never see a penny—Gales had no assets to seize—but the verdict was vindication. A jury had looked at the evidence and said: *This man killed Kim Thomas.*

But it wasn't enough for criminal charges. The DA wouldn't touch it without DNA evidence that met the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt standard. And the DNA technology still wasn't there.

Marion Gales walked out of that courtroom and went back to his life.

Ed went home to his daughter and tried to explain that they'd won, but also lost. Justice had been served, but not really. The man who killed her mother was free.

And Kim's family—waiting, aching, desperate—kept waiting.

---

### The Years That Followed

Ed raised his daughter alone. She graduated high school, went to college, built a life. She grew up without a mother, with a father who'd been wrongly accused, with a murderer walking free.

The case stayed cold. Every few years, CMPD would re-test the DNA as technology improved. Each time, the results were the same: unknown male contributor. Each time, Ed was definitively excluded. Each time, Marion Gales stayed out of reach.

The technology just wasn't there yet.

But it was coming.

---

### Epilogue

Ed Friedland had been cleared. The DNA evidence, tested and re-tested, had ruled him out definitively. The charges from 1994 were a stain on the justice system, a reminder that pressure to solve a case can lead to terrible mistakes.

But while Ed's name had been cleared, his life had been shattered. His reputation never fully recovered. The whispers never stopped. And his daughter grew up knowing that the man who killed her mother was out there, free, living a five-minute walk from where her mother had died.

Marion Gales had been named in a 1990 police report. A civil jury had found him liable. His DNA had been collected in 1995. But the technology to prove what everyone suspected—that he'd walked into the Friedland home on a July morning in 1990, handcuffed Kim Thomas, and slit her throat—didn't exist yet.

So Marion Gales stayed free.

For thirty-five years.

Until the science finally caught up.
