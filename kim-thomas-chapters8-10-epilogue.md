## KIM THOMAS FRIEDLAND MURDER CASE (1990-2026)

## CHAPTER 8: THE ARREST

The phone call came on a Tuesday morning.

February 17, 2026. Detective Sarah Morrison had been with CMPD's Cold Case Unit for eight years. She'd worked the Kim Thomas file off and on since 2018, always coming back to it when newer cases went cold themselves. The file sat on her desk like an accusation—thirty-five years of failure stacked in manila folders and evidence bags.

She picked up on the second ring.

"Morrison."

"It's Mathis. Crime Lab. You're going to want to come down here."

Matthew Mathis didn't waste words. As director of the CMPD Crime Laboratory, he'd spent the last eighteen years modernizing the facility, dragging Charlotte's forensic capabilities into the twenty-first century. He'd overseen hundreds of DNA analyses. Thousands of evidence samples. But Morrison could hear something different in his voice today.

"Is this about Thomas?"

"Just get down here."

Twenty minutes later, Morrison stood in Mathis's office, staring at a computer screen filled with numbers she barely understood. Allele frequencies. Statistical probabilities. DNA markers with names like D3S1358 and vWA. 

"Explain it to me like I'm five," Morrison said.

Mathis pulled up a split-screen comparison. On the left, a DNA profile labeled "Evidence Item 47-B: Rug Sample, Thomas Crime Scene, 07/27/1990." On the right, a profile labeled "Reference Sample: Marion Anthony Gales, Collected 1995."

"Probabilistic genotyping," Mathis said, tapping the screen. "The software we acquired in 2022. It analyzes degraded or mixed DNA samples using mathematical algorithms and biological modeling. Things that were unusable ten years ago, we can now process."

"And?"

"Item 47-B. The rug found beside Kim Thomas's body. We've tested it six times over the years. Always came back inconclusive—too degraded, too contaminated, too old. But the new software can separate mixed profiles, work with skin cells, extract data from samples that look like garbage."

Morrison leaned closer. "What did it find?"

"Marion Gales." Mathis pointed at the overlapping markers. "Nineteen loci match. The probability that this DNA belongs to anyone other than Gales is approximately one in four hundred trillion."

The number hit Morrison like a physical blow. Four hundred trillion to one.

She'd read the file dozens of times. Marion Anthony Gales, sixty-three years old. In 1990, he'd lived in Grier Heights, a five-and-a-half-minute walk from Churchill Road where Kim Thomas died. At sixteen, he'd shot a woman on that same street. In July 1990—the same month as Kim's murder—he'd allegedly beaten his sister. A 1997 civil jury had found him liable for Kim's death and awarded Ed Friedland $8.6 million that Gales never paid.

But DNA evidence sufficient for criminal prosecution had always been just out of reach. The technology hadn't existed. The samples were too degraded. The science wasn't quite there yet.

Until now.

"This will hold up in court?" Morrison asked.

"I've had three independent analysts verify it. Sent samples to the state lab for confirmation. This is solid. This is prosecutable."

Morrison pulled out her phone, scrolling through contacts. "I need to call the DA's office."

"Already did. They're drafting the warrant."

By 4:00 PM that afternoon, Morrison sat in a conference room with the Mecklenburg County District Attorney, two assistant DAs, and Captain James Wheeler from the Violent Criminal Apprehension Team. The arrest warrant lay on the table between them, stark black text on white paper:

*MARION ANTHONY GALES, DOB 06/15/1962, IS HEREBY CHARGED WITH FIRST-DEGREE MURDER IN THE DEATH OF KIMBERLY THOMAS FRIEDLAND ON JULY 27, 1990...*

"Where is he?" the DA asked.

Wheeler opened a folder. "Last known address is an apartment complex off Albemarle Road. We've had eyes on him since this morning. He's home. Hasn't left."

"Is he dangerous?"

"He's sixty-three years old," Wheeler said. "But he's got a history of violence dating back to 1974. We're not taking chances. Full team, body armor, the works."

Morrison thought about Kim Thomas. Thirty-two years old, hands cuffed behind her back, throat slashed while her ten-month-old baby cried in the next room. Thirty-five years Gales had walked free. Thirty-five years he'd eaten meals and watched television and gone to bed at night while Kim's body lay cold in a cemetery plot.

"When do we move?" Morrison asked.

"Tomorrow morning. 6:00 AM."

---

February 19, 2026. Thursday. 5:47 AM.

The Violent Criminal Apprehension Team assembled in a parking lot two blocks from Gales's apartment. Six officers, body armor, sidearms. Morrison and her partner, Detective Luis Ramirez, wore vests but carried no weapons. They were there to make the arrest official, to read the charges, to close a case that had haunted Charlotte for three and a half decades.

Wheeler briefed the team in the predawn darkness. "Subject is Marion Anthony Gales, sixty-three. Wanted for first-degree murder. History of violence. We go in hard, secure the premises, bring him out. Clear?"

Nods all around.

At 5:59 AM, they moved. Two vehicles, no sirens. The apartment complex was a tired two-story building with exterior stairs and cracked concrete walkways. Gales lived in unit 2-C, second floor, east corner. A single window glowed with yellow light—someone was awake.

Wheeler raised his fist. The team stacked up outside the door. Morrison stood back, heart pounding so hard she could hear it in her ears. After all these years. After all the dead ends and false starts and nights staring at crime scene photos trying to find something everyone else had missed.

This was it.

Wheeler knocked. Heavy, authoritative. "Police! Open up!"

Fifteen seconds of silence. Then shuffling footsteps inside.

"Who is it?"

"Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police Department. Open the door, Mr. Gales."

More shuffling. The scrape of a chain lock. The door opened six inches, and Marion Anthony Gales peered out—a thin man with gray hair and a sagging face, wearing a white undershirt and sweatpants.

"What do you want?"

Wheeler didn't answer. He pushed the door open, and the team flowed inside with practiced efficiency. "Marion Anthony Gales, you are under arrest for the murder of Kimberly Thomas Friedland on July 27, 1990—"

"What? No, I didn't—"

"—you have the right to remain silent. Anything you say can and will be used against you in a court of law—"

Gales raised his hands, backing away. "This is a mistake. I already told you people—"

"—you have the right to an attorney. If you cannot afford an attorney, one will be provided for you—"

They cuffed him. Gently but firmly. Morrison watched as they led him out, this old man in handcuffs and sweatpants, shuffling down the exterior stairs while neighbors peeked through curtains. He looked small. Weak. Nothing like the monster who had taken Kim Thomas away from her family.

But DNA didn't lie.

At the bottom of the stairs, Gales turned to Morrison. "I didn't kill her," he said, voice cracking. "I swear to God, I didn't kill her."

Morrison met his eyes. She'd heard enough confessions and denials in her career to know the difference. Gales looked desperate. Terrified. But not surprised. Not the reaction of an innocent man blindsided by false accusations.

He'd been waiting for this knock on the door for thirty-five years.

"Tell it to the judge," Morrison said.

They transported Gales to the Mecklenburg County Jail in separate vehicles—Wheeler's team took the prisoner, Morrison and Ramirez followed in an unmarked sedan. At 6:47 AM, Marion Anthony Gales was officially booked: First-degree murder. No bond. Court date to be determined.

Morrison called Ed Friedland at 7:15 AM.

"Mr. Friedland? This is Detective Morrison, CMPD Cold Case Unit. We met a few years ago regarding your wife's case."

A long pause on the other end. Then: "You found something."

"Yes, sir. This morning at 6:00 AM, we arrested Marion Gales and charged him with first-degree murder in your wife's death."

Silence. Not empty silence—Morrison could hear breathing, could almost feel the weight of thirty-five years compressing into a single moment.

"Ed? Are you there?"

"I'm here." His voice sounded thick, distant. "Did he... did he confess?"

"No, sir. But we have DNA evidence. Solid, prosecutable evidence that places him at the scene. The kind of evidence we couldn't process in 1990. It took this long for the technology to catch up."

"Thirty-five years," Ed said. "My daughter is thirty-five years old this year. The same age Kim was when she died. I've waited her entire lifetime for this phone call."

Morrison didn't know what to say to that. There was no making it right, no erasing the decades of pain. Justice had come, but it had come so late.

"The DA's office will be in touch about the trial," Morrison said. "They'll need you to testify."

"Whatever they need." Ed's voice strengthened. "Whatever it takes. I want him to pay for what he did."

"He will, sir. I promise you, he will."

After the call ended, Morrison sat in her car in the jail parking lot. The sun had fully risen now, winter light harsh and cold. Somewhere inside that concrete building, Marion Gales sat in a cell, finally facing consequences for a murder he'd committed when he was twenty-eight years old. A murder he'd gotten away with for longer than most people's entire careers.

But Kim Thomas was still dead. Her daughter had still grown up without a mother. Ed Friedland had still spent years under suspicion, wrongly charged, his life torn apart while the real killer walked free.

Justice had arrived.

It was thirty-five years too late.

## CHAPTER 9: THE PRESS CONFERENCE

The CMPD headquarters building on Eastway Drive usually handled media with routine professionalism—a spokesperson would read a prepared statement, take a few questions, wrap it up in fifteen minutes. But on the morning of February 20, 2026, the parking lot filled with news vans from every major outlet in Charlotte and beyond. Satellite trucks. Camera crews. Reporters checking microphones and adjusting lights.

The Kim Thomas case had haunted this city for thirty-five years. Now, finally, there would be answers.

Inside the building's main conference room, Deputy Chief Ryan Butler reviewed his notes one final time. He'd been with CMPD for twenty-three years, had overseen dozens of major cases, but this was different. This was a case older than some of his officers. This was justice delayed so long it had become a cautionary tale.

He glanced at Matthew Mathis, the Crime Lab director, who stood quietly near the windows. Mathis looked exhausted—not from lack of sleep but from the weight of carrying this breakthrough. He'd spent years fighting for funding, for updated equipment, for the technology that could finally solve cases like this one. Now he had to explain it to a room full of journalists who wouldn't understand half the science.

"You ready?" Butler asked.

Mathis nodded. "Let's get this over with."

At 10:00 AM sharp, Butler walked to the podium. Behind him, a screen displayed a photo of Kim Thomas from 1990—young, smiling, alive. Next to it, a current booking photo of Marion Gales, gray-haired and hollow-eyed.

The room fell silent.

"Good morning. I'm Deputy Chief Ryan Butler. This morning, I'm here to announce a significant development in a case that has remained open for thirty-five years."

He paused, letting that number land. Thirty-five years. Longer than some of these reporters had been alive.

"On July 27, 1990, Kimberly Thomas Friedland was murdered in her home on Churchill Road in southeast Charlotte. She was thirty-two years old, a mother, a wife, and a women's rights activist beloved by this community. The investigation into her death has been active for more than three decades. This morning, our violent criminal apprehension team located and arrested Marion Anthony Gales, age sixty-three, at his residence. He has been charged with first-degree murder and is currently being held at the Mecklenburg County Jail without bond."

The room erupted. Questions shouted over each other. Camera flashes popped. Butler raised a hand.

"I'll take questions in a moment. First, I want to explain how we got here."

He gestured to Mathis, who stepped forward. The Crime Lab director looked uncomfortable under the lights, more at ease with microscopes than microphones.

"I'm Matthew Mathis, director of the CMPD Crime Laboratory. The breakthrough in this case came from advances in DNA analysis technology. Specifically, probabilistic genotyping—a method that allows us to analyze degraded, mixed, or trace DNA samples that were previously unusable."

A reporter from the Charlotte Observer raised her hand. "Can you explain what that means in practical terms?"

Mathis nodded. "In 1990, when Kim Thomas was killed, DNA testing required relatively large, clean samples. Blood, semen, saliva—fluids we could extract clear profiles from. But crime scenes are messy. Evidence degrades. DNA mixes together. For decades, we had DNA from the Thomas crime scene that we knew didn't belong to the victim or her husband, but we couldn't identify who it belonged to. The samples were too degraded, too contaminated."

"And now you can?" another reporter asked.

"Yes. Probabilistic genotyping uses computer algorithms and statistical modeling to analyze DNA that looks, frankly, like garbage. It separates mixed profiles. It extracts data from skin cells, touch DNA, samples that have degraded over decades. We acquired this technology in 2022 and have been systematically re-testing cold cases. The Kim Thomas case was selected for analysis in 2023. After multiple rounds of testing and verification, we definitively matched DNA from the crime scene to Marion Anthony Gales."

"How certain are you?"

"The statistical probability that the DNA belongs to anyone other than Gales is approximately one in four hundred trillion."

The room went silent for a moment. Then the questions came in a flood.

"Why wasn't Gales arrested before if he was a suspect?"

"Will he face the death penalty?"

"What about Ed Friedland—he was charged in 1994, right?"

"Does this mean other cold cases will be reopened?"

Butler stepped back to the podium. "One at a time, please."

He pointed to a reporter from Spectrum News. "The death penalty is a decision for the District Attorney's office. Given the age of the case and the defendant's age, I can't speculate on that. Next question."

"Why did it take thirty-five years?"

Butler exhaled slowly. "Because the technology didn't exist. We had evidence, we had suspects, but we couldn't conclusively tie anyone to the crime scene until now. DNA science has evolved dramatically since 1990. What's routine today was impossible then."

"What about the civil trial in 1997? A jury found Gales liable for Kim's death. Why weren't criminal charges filed then?"

"A civil verdict has a lower burden of proof—preponderance of evidence rather than beyond a reasonable doubt. We couldn't meet the criminal standard until we had DNA evidence solid enough for prosecution."

"And Ed Friedland?"

Butler's jaw tightened. "Dr. Friedland was investigated as part of standard procedure in domestic homicides. He was charged in 1994 but the charges were dropped due to insufficient evidence. Subsequent DNA testing definitively excluded him as a contributor. He is not and has never been a suspect in this department's eyes since that time."

The careful phrasing didn't erase the damage. Everyone in that room knew the truth—Ed Friedland had spent years under a cloud of suspicion, wrongly accused while the real killer walked free. That was a stain CMPD would carry forever.

"Where was Marion Gales all these years?"

"He's lived in the Charlotte area. He has a criminal history dating back to 1974, including violence against women. We interviewed him in 1995 as part of our investigation, but without DNA evidence sufficient for prosecution, we couldn't arrest him."

"He was interviewed and just... let go?"

"We didn't have the evidence," Butler said flatly. "Not until now."

A reporter from The Independent asked, "What's the family's reaction?"

Butler softened slightly. "Dr. Ed Friedland was notified this morning after the arrest. He and his daughter are understandably relieved but also devastated that it took this long. They've waited thirty-five years for answers. That's a lifetime of pain."

"Will they testify at trial?"

"That's up to the DA's office and the family. But I imagine they'll want to be there."

The questions continued for another twenty minutes. Mathis explained the science in increasing detail, drawing diagrams, losing half the room but satisfying the technical reporters. Butler deflected questions about evidence specifics—the handcuffs, the knife that was never found, the theory of how Gales had gained entry to the home. All of that would come out at trial.

Finally, Butler raised a hand. "Last question."

A young reporter from QC News spoke up. "Do you think there are other victims? Gales has a history of violence against women going back decades. Could he have killed before?"

Butler and Mathis exchanged a glance. It was the question everyone had been thinking but no one wanted to voice. Marion Gales had lived free for thirty-five years after killing Kim Thomas. He'd been in and out of jail, sure, but never for murder. Never stopped long enough that he couldn't do it again.

"We have no evidence linking Marion Gales to any other homicides," Butler said carefully. "But our Cold Case Unit is reviewing unsolved cases from the same time period. If there are connections, we'll find them."

The press conference ended at 10:52 AM. Within an hour, the story had exploded across local and national news. Charlotte Observer ran it as the front-page headline: **"DNA BREAKTHROUGH CRACKS 35-YEAR-OLD MURDER CASE."** Spectrum News went live with continuous coverage. Social media lit up with reactions—shock, relief, anger that it had taken so long.

---

Ed Friedland watched the press conference from his living room. He'd turned sixty-eight last month, retirement age for most people, though he still saw patients two days a week. Orthopedic surgery didn't leave the body easily—his hands still knew the motions of setting bones, repairing joints, putting broken things back together.

He hadn't been able to put his own life back together.

On the television, Deputy Chief Butler spoke in measured tones about DNA and evidence and justice. Ed barely heard the words. He was looking at Kim's photo on the screen behind the podium—the same photo that sat on his mantle, that he'd stared at for thirty-five years.

She'd been thirty-two. He was sixty-eight now. He'd lived more than twice as long as she had.

His phone buzzed. His daughter.

"Dad. Did you see?"

"I'm watching it now."

"Are you okay?"

Ed laughed—a bitter, hollow sound. "I don't know. I don't think I know what okay feels like anymore."

"Do you believe them? That it's really over?"

"They have his DNA," Ed said. "Four hundred trillion to one, they said. It's over. They got him."

A long silence. Then his daughter's voice, thick with tears: "Why did it take thirty-five years?"

"Because the science wasn't there yet."

"That's what they *say*. But that report from 1990—the one that mentioned Gales. If they'd followed up then—"

"They didn't." Ed cut her off gently. "We can't go back. We can only move forward."

"I was ten months old," she said. "I don't remember her at all. Every memory I have is from photos, from stories people tell me. And now this man—this *monster*—he gets to have lived his life while she's been gone mine. That's not justice, Dad. That's just... it's just cruel."

Ed couldn't argue with that. Marion Gales had attended his sister's funeral in 1998. He'd watched television, eaten Christmas dinners, seen the Twin Towers fall and Obama elected and the world turn through thirty-five years of history. Kim Thomas had seen none of it. She'd been frozen in 1990, forever thirty-two, forever murdered, while time moved on without her.

"The trial will be hard," Ed said. "They'll want us to testify. To tell the jury about her."

"I'll be there." His daughter's voice steadied. "Whatever they need, I'll be there. She deserves that much."

After they hung up, Ed sat in silence. On the television, the press conference had ended, replaced by news anchors dissecting the case, pulling up old footage from 1990, interviewing neighbors who'd known Kim. The story would dominate the news cycle for days. Weeks, maybe. Charlotte's decades-old cold case finally solved by miracle science.

But Ed didn't feel miraculous. He felt tired.

Thirty-five years he'd waited for this moment. Thirty-five years of wondering if justice would ever come, if Kim's killer would ever face consequences, if his daughter would ever know the truth about who took her mother away.

Now he knew.

Marion Anthony Gales. A violent criminal who'd lived five and a half minutes from their home. A man who'd hurt women before and would have hurt them again. A man who'd slipped into their house on a July morning and destroyed everything.

The phone rang again. Morrison, the detective.

"Mr. Friedland, I wanted to check on you. I know today is overwhelming."

"That's one word for it."

"The DA's office will reach out next week to discuss the trial. They'll need your testimony, and probably your daughter's as well."

"We'll be there," Ed said.

"I know this doesn't fix anything," Morrison said. "I know it doesn't bring her back or erase the years you spent under suspicion. But for what it's worth, we never stopped working this case. Never stopped trying."

"I know." And he did. He'd met Morrison before, seen the determination in her eyes, the way she carried the weight of unsolved cases like physical burdens. She'd done her job. They all had, eventually.

It just hadn't been enough. Not fast enough, not soon enough, not when it mattered most.

"Thank you," Ed said, because someone had to say it.

After the call ended, he walked to the mantle and picked up Kim's photo. Thirty-two years old, smiling at the camera, her whole future ahead of her. Activism and motherhood and growing old and watching their daughter graduate and get married and maybe have children of her own.

Marion Gales had taken all of that.

Now, finally, he would pay for it.

Ed set the photo back down. Tomorrow the news would move on to other stories. Next week the media circus would quiet. But the trial would come, and Ed would be there, and he would tell the world who Kim Thomas had been.

Not a victim. Not a cold case. Not a thirty-five-year-old mystery finally solved.

A woman. A mother. A wife. A person who'd mattered, who'd made the world better, who'd been loved.

That was the justice that mattered. Not the arrest, not the DNA, not the headlines.

Remembering her as she'd been, not as she'd died.

## CHAPTER 10: THE TRIAL AHEAD

March 13, 2026. Mecklenburg County Courthouse, courtroom 5130.

The room smelled like old wood and anxiety. Ed Friedland sat in the second row, hands folded in his lap, watching as Marion Anthony Gales shuffled into the courtroom in an orange jumpsuit and shackles. The man looked smaller than Ed had imagined—gray-haired, stooped, moving with the careful hesitation of someone who'd spent too much time in jail over too many years.

This was the man who'd killed Kim.

Ed had imagined this moment for thirty-five years. He'd pictured confronting Gales, screaming at him, demanding answers. Why Kim? Why that day? What had she done to deserve having her throat slashed while her baby cried in the next room?

But now, face to face with the monster, Ed felt nothing. No rage. No grief. Just a hollow, exhausted numbness.

Gales was led to the defense table, where a young public defender waited—a woman in her thirties, professional but already worn down by the futility of her task. How do you defend a man whose DNA was found at a murder scene, whose criminal history read like a warning label, who'd already been found liable in civil court twenty-nine years ago?

You do your job. You make the state prove its case. You hold them to the burden of proof.

Judge Patricia Freeman entered from chambers, and the room rose. Freeman was in her sixties, a former prosecutor known for running a tight courtroom. She'd seen every variation of human cruelty Charlotte could produce. She didn't look impressed by Marion Gales.

"Be seated. Case number 26-CR-203947, State of North Carolina versus Marion Anthony Gales. Mr. Gales, you are charged with first-degree murder in the death of Kimberly Thomas Friedland on July 27, 1990. You have the right to remain silent, you have the right to an attorney, and you have the right to a trial by jury. Do you understand these rights?"

Gales leaned toward the microphone. "Yes, ma'am."

His voice was thin, reedy. Not the voice of a monster. Just an old man.

"How do you plead?"

The public defender stood. "Not guilty, Your Honor."

No surprise there. The defense would challenge everything—the age of the evidence, the reliability of probabilistic genotyping, the chain of custody over thirty-five years. They'd argue that DNA evidence alone wasn't sufficient, that the civil trial verdict was prejudicial, that Gales's criminal history was irrelevant to this specific murder. They'd do their job, make their arguments, and lose.

Because the DNA didn't lie.

Judge Freeman reviewed the docket. "The state is requesting remand without bond. Defense?"

The public defender—her nameplate read "Karen Voss"—stood again. "Your Honor, my client is sixty-three years old, has significant health issues, and has lived in Charlotte his entire life. He's not a flight risk. We request bond be set at a reasonable amount."

"Your client is charged with first-degree murder," Freeman said dryly. "He allegedly killed a woman in her home, leaving a ten-month-old child orphaned, and evaded justice for thirty-five years. I don't find that conducive to release. Bond is denied. Trial date will be set for..." She consulted her calendar. "October 14, 2026. Seven months from now. That gives both sides time to prepare."

Gales's shoulders slumped. Seven more months in jail. Then a trial that would almost certainly end with a life sentence. He was sixty-three now. He'd die in prison.

As the guards led Gales out, he turned and scanned the courtroom. His eyes landed on Ed for just a moment—two men whose lives had been bound together by violence, separated by thirty-five years.

Ed didn't look away. He held Gales's gaze until the guards pulled him through the side door and back to his cell.

---

The prosecution team gathered in a conference room after the hearing. Assistant District Attorney Marcus Chen spread case files across the table—police reports from 1990, DNA lab results from 2026, the civil trial transcript from 1997, Marion Gales's criminal history dating back to 1974.

"The case is strong," Chen said. "DNA evidence is basically bulletproof. Four hundred trillion to one. No jury is going to ignore that."

Detective Morrison nodded. "Plus we have the civil verdict. A jury already found him liable in 1997."

"Which the defense will move to exclude," said ADA Julia Warren. "They'll argue it's prejudicial, that civil and criminal standards are different."

"Let them argue," Chen said. "Judge Freeman knows the law. She'll allow it."

"What about the defense strategy?" Morrison asked. "What's Voss going to do?"

Chen leaned back. "She'll attack the science. Probabilistic genotyping is relatively new—she'll bring in an expert to argue it's unreliable, that degraded DNA can't be trusted, that thirty-five years is too long for evidence integrity. She'll point out that we don't have a murder weapon, no eyewitnesses, no confession. She'll argue that DNA alone isn't enough for conviction."

"But it is enough," Warren said.

"In theory, yes. But juries are unpredictable. Especially with technology they don't understand." Chen tapped the DNA report. "We need to explain probabilistic genotyping in a way that makes sense to twelve random citizens. We need to walk them through how DNA works, how the science has evolved, why this evidence is rock-solid despite being thirty-five years old."

"Mathis can do that," Morrison said. "He's been testifying about DNA for two decades."

"Good. We'll also need Ed Friedland and his daughter on the stand. Humanize the victim. Make the jury see Kim Thomas as a person, not just a case file."

Morrison shifted uncomfortably. "They're willing to testify. But it's going to be hard for them. Especially the daughter—she never knew her mother. She only knows the murder."

"Which is exactly why she needs to testify," Chen said. "The jury needs to understand what Marion Gales took from that family. Not just Kim's life, but her daughter's entire childhood. Thirty-five years of growing up without a mother because this man decided to kill her."

The team spent the next two hours reviewing evidence, building timelines, identifying potential weak points the defense might exploit. The case was strong, but not perfect. Thirty-five years had passed. Witnesses had died or moved away. Physical evidence had degraded. The murder weapon had never been recovered.

But they had DNA. And in 2026, DNA was king.

---

Ed Friedland met with the prosecution team the following week. They sat in the same conference room, surrounded by the same files, and Chen walked him through what to expect.

"The defense will try to rattle you," Chen said. "They'll ask about your relationship with Kim, whether you fought, whether you had financial problems. They'll imply you're the one who killed her."

"Even though the DNA proves I didn't?"

"They have to create reasonable doubt. If they can make the jury think there's even a slim possibility someone else did it, Gales walks."

Ed's jaw tightened. "He's not walking. Not after thirty-five years."

"Then we need you to stay calm on the stand. Answer the questions truthfully, don't get defensive, and remember—you're there to tell the jury who Kim was. A women's rights activist. A devoted mother. A woman who made Charlotte a better place."

"And someone Marion Gales murdered."

"Yes. But we prove that with DNA and evidence. You prove she was worth remembering."

Ed's daughter attended a separate prep session. She sat across from Julia Warren, hands twisted in her lap, visibly uncomfortable.

"I don't remember her," she said quietly. "I was ten months old. Everything I know comes from photos and stories."

"That's okay," Warren said. "You're not there to provide memories. You're there to show the jury the cost of this crime. You grew up without a mother. You missed birthdays and graduations and every milestone because Marion Gales killed her. The jury needs to understand that."

"What will they ask me?"

"The prosecution will ask about your life, what it was like growing up without your mother, how her death affected your family. The defense will try to make it seem like you're seeking revenge, that you're biased against Gales."

"I *am* biased against him. He killed my mother."

"Which is perfectly reasonable. Just stay calm and tell the truth. That's all we need."

The months between March and October crawled by. Ed tried to focus on work, on patients who needed him, on the routine of setting bones and repairing joints. But his mind kept drifting to the trial, to the testimony he'd have to give, to the moment when he'd sit in that witness box and describe finding Kim's body while Gales watched from the defense table.

The media coverage intensified as October approached. Every major outlet in Charlotte ran features on the case—retrospectives on the 1990 murder, profiles of Kim Thomas, interviews with forensic experts explaining probabilistic genotyping. Some coverage was respectful. Some was exploitative. All of it felt like picking at a wound that had never fully healed.

Morrison called Ed in September. "The defense filed a motion to exclude the civil trial verdict. Judge Freeman denied it. They also tried to suppress the DNA evidence, claiming chain of custody issues. That was denied too."

"So it's really happening?"

"October 14. Be ready."

---

The trial would be a reckoning. Thirty-five years of pain and waiting and unanswered questions, distilled into weeks of testimony and evidence and legal argument. Marion Gales would sit at the defense table, listening as witness after witness described his violence, his history, his DNA at the crime scene.

The prosecution's case was straightforward: On July 27, 1990, Marion Gales entered the home of Ed and Kim Friedland on Churchill Road. He handcuffed Kim behind her back and slashed her throat. He left her to bleed out on the floor while her baby cried in the next room. His DNA was found on a rug beside her body. The probability of that DNA belonging to anyone else was one in four hundred trillion.

The defense would argue: DNA alone proves nothing. Gales could have been in that house for innocent reasons. The evidence is thirty-five years old, degraded, unreliable. Probabilistic genotyping is new science, not yet proven. The civil trial was a miscarriage of justice driven by grief and anger, not facts.

The jury would decide.

Ed knew what the verdict would be. The evidence was overwhelming. The science was solid. Marion Gales would be convicted and sentenced to life in prison without parole. He would die behind bars, finally punished for the crime he'd committed as a twenty-eight-year-old man.

But the conviction wouldn't bring Kim back. It wouldn't give Ed's daughter the childhood she'd lost. It wouldn't erase thirty-five years of wondering and grieving and waking up every morning knowing her killer walked free.

Justice had come.

It had just come thirty-five years too late.

On the night before the trial, Ed sat in his living room with Kim's photo in his hands. October 13, 2026. Tomorrow the trial would begin. Tomorrow he'd take the stand and tell the world about the woman he'd loved, the woman Marion Gales had taken from him.

She'd been thirty-two when she died. She would have been sixty-seven now, grandmother age, retirement age, the age when you looked back on a life well-lived.

Marion Gales had stolen all of that.

Tomorrow, finally, he would pay.

Ed set the photo down and went to bed. In seven months, there would be a verdict. In seven months, Gales would be sentenced. In seven months, this decades-long nightmare would finally end.

Kim Thomas Friedland would still be dead.

But she would be remembered.

And Marion Gales would rot in prison for what he'd done.

That wasn't justice.

But it was something.

## EPILOGUE

April 15, 2026. Four months before the trial.

Ed Friedland stood at Kim's grave, the April sun warm on his shoulders. He'd been here a thousand times over the past thirty-five years—birthdays, anniversaries, the random Tuesday afternoons when grief overwhelmed him and he needed to feel close to her again.

The headstone was simple. **KIMBERLY THOMAS FRIEDLAND. 1958-1990. BELOVED WIFE, MOTHER, ACTIVIST. FOREVER IN OUR HEARTS.**

Thirty-five years she'd been gone. Thirty-five years the grass had grown over her grave while the world moved on and her killer walked free.

Not anymore.

His daughter stood beside him, holding a bouquet of daisies—Kim's favorite. She was thirty-five years old now, the same age her mother had been when she died. Ed saw so much of Kim in her face, in the set of her jaw, the way she carried herself. She'd inherited Kim's stubbornness, her passion, her refusal to give up even when the world seemed determined to break her.

"Mom," she said softly, kneeling to place the flowers at the headstone, "they got him. After all these years, they finally got him."

Ed touched her shoulder. They stood in silence, the April breeze rustling through the cemetery trees. Somewhere in the distance, a lawnmower hummed. Life continued, indifferent to grief and justice and the small human dramas that unfolded in its wake.

"Do you think she knows?" his daughter asked.

"I don't know. I hope so."

"Does it feel like justice to you?"

Ed considered. Justice. A word he'd clung to for three and a half decades, a promise that someday Kim's killer would face consequences, that the scales would balance, that right would prevail.

But standing here in the spring sunshine with his daughter beside him and Kim's bones in the ground beneath their feet, justice felt hollow.

"It feels like the end," Ed said. "Not justice. Just... the end of waiting."

His daughter nodded. "I keep thinking about all the cold cases out there. All the families still waiting. If it took thirty-five years for us, how long for them?"

Ed had thought about that too. The CMPD had announced they were re-examining dozens of old cases using the same probabilistic genotyping technology that had finally identified Marion Gales. How many other killers were out there, aging into retirement, thinking they'd gotten away with it? How many other families were still waiting for a phone call like the one Ed had received on February 19?

"The technology gives hope," Ed said. "That's something."

"But it doesn't bring them back."

"No. It doesn't."

They stood awhile longer, then turned to leave. As they walked back toward the car, Ed's daughter paused.

"Tell me about her," she said. "Not the murder. Not the case. Just... her."

Ed smiled. It hurt, but it was a good hurt. The kind that came from remembering joy instead of loss.

"She was stubborn. God, was she stubborn. Once she believed in something, you couldn't talk her out of it. Women's rights, equality, justice—she fought for all of it. She organized protests, spoke at rallies, got arrested twice for civil disobedience."

"Really?"

"Really. She was fearless. And funny. She had this laugh that filled a room, made everyone around her feel like they were part of something good. When you were born, she cried for an hour. Just held you and cried because she was so happy."

His daughter's eyes filled with tears. "I wish I remembered her."

"She's in you," Ed said. "Every day, I see her in you. The way you stand up for yourself, the way you don't back down from what you believe. That's Kim. That's your mother."

They reached the car. Ed glanced back one more time at the grave, the simple headstone, the daisies his daughter had left.

Thirty-five years ago, Marion Gales had walked into their home and shattered everything. He'd taken Kim's life, stolen a mother from her daughter, turned Ed's world into a decades-long nightmare of grief and suspicion and unanswered questions.

But he hadn't won.

Kim Thomas Friedland was still remembered. Still loved. Still a force in the world through her daughter, through the organizations she'd built, through the people whose lives she'd touched in thirty-two short years.

Marion Gales was in jail. Soon he'd be convicted. He'd spend the rest of his life in a cell, finally punished for what he'd done.

That wasn't the ending Ed had wanted. He'd wanted Kim to live. He'd wanted his daughter to know her mother. He'd wanted Marion Gales to have been caught in 1990, before thirty-five years of freedom, before all the pain and waiting.

But this was the ending they had.

Science had given Kim justice. DNA technology—impossible in 1990, routine in 2026—had finally identified her killer. Other families would benefit from that technology. Other cold cases would be solved. Other daughters would learn the truth about who took their mothers away.

That was Kim's legacy. Not just the activism, not just the fight for women's rights, but the breakthrough that came from never giving up on her case. The detectives who kept testing evidence. The lab director who fought for better technology. The prosecutors who waited decades for science to catch up to justice.

Ed got in the car. His daughter started the engine.

"October 14," she said. "The trial starts."

"I know."

"We'll be there. Both of us."

"Yes. We will."

They drove away from the cemetery, leaving Kim's grave behind. The trial would come. The verdict would come. Marion Gales would be sentenced to life in prison, and Ed would finally have the closure he'd waited three and a half decades to receive.

But Kim would still be gone.

Justice delayed was still justice, but it would never bring her back.
