# CHAPTER 6: THE MATCH

## Seventeen Years of Silence

For seventeen years, Detective John "Billy" Belk carried the Lovers Lane murders like a second heartbeat. Every morning he opened the file. Every night he closed it unsolved.

He'd interviewed over a hundred suspects. Collected DNA samples from two dozen men. Cleared them all.

The killer's genetic profile sat in the CODIS database since 1991. A perfect blueprint. But it matched nothing. No one. 

It was like screaming into a canyon that never echoed back.

In 2007, Harris County Sheriff's Office began processing a massive backlog of old rape kits—thousands of sexual assault cases from the 1980s and 1990s, gathering dust in storage facilities while technology caught up to justice.

Lab technicians worked methodically. Swab by swab. Victim by victim. Each kit was tested, profiled, and uploaded into CODIS.

Most returned nothing. Some returned matches to known offenders already in prison.

But one—a rape kit from June 20, 1990, just two months before the Lovers Lane murders—lit up the system like a flare in the dark.

**CASE-TO-CASE MATCH.**

## The Dancer from GiGi's

The victim was a 30-year-old exotic dancer who worked at GiGi's, a topless club on Northwest Freeway. 

On that humid night in June 1990, she'd been attacked at her apartment on Terra Cotta Drive in north Harris County. The perpetrator wore a fishnet stocking mask, black gloves, and uniform-style clothing. He carried a long-barreled handgun and moved with the confidence of someone used to intimidating people.

He raped her. Then he vanished.

She'd given police a detailed description. A composite sketch was made. DNA was collected from the rape kit. But the case went cold—another sexual assault in a city full of them, filed away in the endless bureaucratic machinery of an overtaxed system.

Now, in 2007, that DNA matched the profile from Cheryl Henry's body.

Same man. Same monster.

Detective Belk stared at the report in disbelief. After seventeen years of dead ends, the case had just doubled.

The killer hadn't started with Cheryl and Andy. He'd struck before.

## The Connection

When detectives tracked down the June 1990 rape victim—now living quietly in Galveston County—they learned something that made the hairs on their necks stand up.

She'd once worked for Garland Atkinson Sr.

Andy's father.

The coincidence was too specific, too deliberate. Was this personal? Had the killer targeted Andy because of his father? Was Cheryl just collateral damage in some twisted vendetta?

Or was it a horrifying fluke—that this predator orbited the same nightclub world where both victims lived and worked?

Investigators couldn't answer that. Not yet.

What they *could* do was release a composite sketch based on the 1990 rape victim's description. They held a press conference. Announced the DNA breakthrough. Begged the public for help.

**"We know this man attacked at least two women. We know he murdered two young people. If you recognize him, call us."**

The Houston Chronicle ran the story on May 19, 2008: *"DNA may help break notorious 'Lover's Lane' murders."*

The sketch showed a white male, mid-to-late twenties in 1990, with a nondescript face—neither handsome nor ugly, the kind of man who could blend into any crowd. Dark hair. Average build. Forgettable.

Tips poured in. Detectives chased them all. Ran down dozens of leads.

Every single one evaporated.

## The Ghost in the Machine

The cruelest part wasn't the lack of clues. It was having a clue that should have been enough—and still coming up empty.

The DNA profile was perfect. Modern. Reliable. It sat in CODIS, cross-referenced against millions of samples from convicted offenders across the country.

Nothing.

Which meant the killer had either never been arrested for a serious crime in a state that collected DNA...

Or his DNA had never been uploaded into the system.

That second possibility gnawed at investigators. Because they knew something most people didn't:

**Not all arrests trigger DNA collection. Not all collected DNA gets uploaded. And even when it does, it can take years—sometimes decades—for a sample to make it into CODIS.**

There were cracks in the system. Blind spots. Administrative failures. Budget shortfalls. Incompatible databases. Jurisdictions that didn't communicate.

And somewhere in those cracks, a killer was hiding.

Belk retired in 2007, the same week he got the news about the DNA match. Seventeen years was enough. He'd fought the good fight. Now it was someone else's turn.

Detective Michael Miller inherited the case. He reviewed every page. Re-interviewed witnesses. Stared at the composite sketch until his eyes burned.

"We know there are other victims," Miller told reporters in 2008.

But knowing and proving are different things.

## The Waiting Game

Over the next eighteen years, the case cycled through the cold case unit like a ghost that wouldn't leave.

Families called every few months. Reporters wrote anniversary pieces. True crime forums dissected every detail. The Wikipedia page got longer.

But nothing moved.

Andy's father, Garland Atkinson Sr., never gave up hope. He kept his son's room the same. He told anyone who would listen: *Someone knows something.*

Cheryl's mother, Barbara Craig, prayed every night for answers. She attended victim advocacy events. She lobbied for better funding for cold case units.

Both of them died in 2024.

They never learned the truth.

They never heard the killer's name.

## The Question That Haunted Them All

Why did it take nineteen years from the 2007 DNA match to the 2026 arrest?

That question would later hang over the case like smoke. Prosecutors dodged it. Police officials changed the subject. The system had failed—but no one wanted to explain exactly how.

The truth, as best as investigators could piece it together, was this:

In 1996—just six years after the murders—Floyd William Parrott had been accused of sexual assault. DNA was collected. But the victim, another woman who worked at a strip club, declined to cooperate. She didn't want to be cross-examined in court, didn't want her life dragged through the system.

The grand jury declined to indict.

The case was dropped.

The DNA sat in an evidence locker. For decades.

It was never uploaded to CODIS. Not in 1996. Not in 2000. Not in 2007 when the June 1990 rape matched the murders.

Why?

Budget cuts. Staffing shortages. Bureaucratic oversight. A dropped case that didn't meet someone's threshold for database entry.

Pick your excuse. The result was the same.

**Floyd Parrott's DNA was invisible to the system that should have caught him.**

When the Harris County DA's Office finally—*finally*—uploaded that 1996 sample in late 2025, CODIS hit like a freight train.

**MATCH. MATCH. MATCH.**

June 1990 rape. August 1990 double murder. August 1996 sexual assault.

All the same man.

Floyd William Parrott.

And by then, he'd been gone from Texas for over a year.

# CHAPTER 7: THE PREDATOR NEXT DOOR

## The Quiet Man in Apartment 4B

In Lincoln, Nebraska, Floyd William Parrott lived the kind of life that didn't draw attention.

He was 64 years old. Balding. Soft around the middle. He drove a sensible car. Paid his rent on time. Kept to himself.

His neighbors saw him occasionally in the hallways—polite nods, brief small talk about the weather. Nothing memorable. Nothing that raised flags.

No one knew what he'd done in Houston.

No one suspected the monster sleeping in apartment 4B.

## The Long Escape

Floyd Parrott didn't flee Texas in a panic. He didn't run in the night with a duffel bag full of cash. His escape was slower, more methodical.

For over three decades after the murders, he stayed in the Houston area. Worked odd jobs. Bounced at nightclubs. Sold auto parts. Lived in New Caney, a small town northeast of Houston, where he owned property until 2020.

He wasn't hiding. He was *living*.

Because he thought he'd gotten away with it.

Parrott's confidence wasn't irrational. He'd been arrested multiple times—1988, 1990, 1995, 1996—and every time, he'd wiggled through the cracks.

Probation. Parole. Dropped charges. Plea deals.

He served three years of a twenty-year sentence for impersonating a police chief in 1995. Three years. Then walked.

When he was accused of sexual assault in 1996, the victim refused to testify. Case dismissed.

The system kept spitting him back out.

But in 2020, something shifted. Maybe he sensed the walls closing in. Maybe someone tipped him off that cold cases were being re-examined with new technology. Maybe it was just paranoia, the gnawing fear that comes from knowing you left DNA at a crime scene.

He sold his Texas property. Packed up. Moved to Lincoln, Nebraska—over 800 miles from Houston.

Far enough to breathe.

Close enough to disappear.

## The Impersonator

To understand Floyd Parrott, you have to understand his obsession with authority.

He didn't just pretend to be a cop once or twice. He did it *compulsively*, across more than a decade, with escalating audacity.

**May 1988:** Arrested for impersonating a peace officer in Harris County. Placed on probation. At the time of his arrest, he listed a work address just over one mile from the remote wooded area where Cheryl and Andy would be murdered two years later.

**December 1988:** Arrested for carrying a weapon—a blue steel revolver that police later returned to him. That same revolver matched the description given by the June 1990 rape victim.

**May 1990:** Arrested again, this time claiming to be a deputy constable for Precinct 4 and Precinct 5. Released on bond. He was *still out on bond* when he committed the June 1990 sexual assault and the August 1990 murders.

**July 1990:** Five weeks before the Lovers Lane murders, arrested on two counts of impersonating a cop. Sentenced to two years. Didn't start serving time until after the murders.

**September 1995:** The big one. Parrott posed as a *Montgomery County police chief*. Pulled over a driver on the Southwest Freeway near Chimney Rock using a car equipped with emergency lights. Extorted $100 from the terrified motorist over an expired license plate.

When police searched his home, they found:

- Police uniforms
- Multiple badges
- Emergency lights
- Law enforcement equipment

It was a shrine to control. A costume closet for predation.

He was sentenced to 20 years. Served three.

## The Psychology of a Fake Cop

Christopher Downey, the prosecutor who put Parrott away in 1995, later said:

*"Someone who impersonates a police officer and goes to the extent to solicit a bribe, when the whole time he was acting as a fake police officer, is pretty shocking... It was downright predatory."*

Parrott didn't impersonate cops to rob banks or commit sophisticated crimes.

He did it to *dominate people*.

A badge is permission. It's authority without question. It lets you stop cars. Enter homes. Demand compliance. And for a sexual predator, it's the perfect camouflage.

The June 1990 rape victim described an attacker wearing uniform-style clothing, carrying a handgun, moving with the swagger of someone used to being obeyed.

That was Parrott's brand. Control through intimidation. Authority as weapon.

And when you wear that mask long enough, you start to believe you're untouchable.

## Why Couples?

The Lovers Lane murders posed a unique psychological puzzle: Why target a *couple*?

Most sexual predators hunt alone women. Easier. Less risk. One victim to control.

But Parrott attacked Cheryl and Andy *together*. He subdued a 21-year-old man, bound him, marched him into the woods, and tied him to a tree—then returned to assault Cheryl while Andy was helpless nearby.

That required planning. Confidence. And a specific sadistic need.

**Power over multiple victims simultaneously.**

Sexual sadists don't just want to assault. They want to humiliate. Terrorize. Strip away every ounce of control their victims have.

By attacking a couple, Parrott achieved maximum trauma:

- Andy, forced to hear his girlfriend being assaulted, powerless to stop it
- Cheryl, knowing her boyfriend was tied up, likely already dead or dying
- Both victims experiencing not just physical violence, but emotional annihilation

Criminologists who study sexual sadism point to traits like:

- Need for total domination
- Pleasure derived from victim's psychological suffering
- Elaborate planning and control of crime scene
- Post-crime "staging" (arranging objects, positioning bodies)

Parrott's crime scene fit every marker.

He didn't just kill them. He *orchestrated* their deaths.

## The Andy Atkinson Connection

One question still nags investigators: *Why Andy?*

Was it random? Or was it personal?

Here's what we know:

1. The June 1990 rape victim—whose assault is DNA-linked to the murders—once worked for Andy's father, Garland Atkinson Sr.

2. Parrott worked as a bouncer at Houston nightclubs and strip clubs.

3. Cheryl worked at Rick's Cabaret. Andy occasionally worked the door at Dreams Street, his father's club.

4. Parrott's 1988 work address was one mile from the murder scene—an area undeveloped at the time, known to locals but not heavily trafficked.

Did Parrott know Garland Atkinson Sr.? Did he work for him?

Did he target Andy because of some grudge, some slight, some simmering resentment?

Or was this all just horrifying coincidence—a predator orbiting the same nightlife ecosystem where the Atkinsons and Henrys moved?

Police, in 2007, admitted: *"We didn't know if the killer might also have worked for Garland Atkinson or if it was just a crazy coincidence."*

As of 2026, they still don't know.

What they *do* know is that Parrott had a pattern: He targeted women connected to the adult entertainment industry. He used authority to intimidate. He struck in the summer of 1990 with increasing boldness.

And he never stopped.

## Hiding in Plain Sight

For 36 years, Floyd Parrott lived among us.

He shopped at grocery stores. Pumped gas. Watched TV. Complained about the weather.

He was the guy in line at the DMV. The customer at the auto parts counter. The neighbor who borrowed your jumper cables.

In Lincoln, Nebraska, no one looked twice at him. He was just another retiree, living out his golden years in quiet anonymity.

While in Houston, two families screamed into the void.

While Shane Henry attended vigils and gave interviews, begging for answers.

While Barbara Craig and Garland Atkinson Sr. grew old and sick and died, never knowing the name of the man who destroyed their children.

Parrott slept soundly. Woke up every day. Lived free.

## The Call That Changed Everything

In late 2025, someone called Houston Police.

That person—whose identity remains protected—gave investigators a name:

**Floyd William Parrott.**

We don't know who made the call. We don't know why, after 35 years, they finally spoke up.

Maybe it was someone from Parrott's past. An ex-girlfriend. A former coworker. Someone who'd always suspected but never had proof.

Maybe it was guilt. Or fear. Or the realization that silence makes you complicit.

Whatever the reason, that call set off a chain reaction.

A sergeant in HPD's cold case unit started digging. In January 2026, Detective Michael Burrow joined the team and brought "fresh eyes" to thousands of pages of old reports.

Burrow found something everyone else had missed:

A 1996 sexual assault report naming Floyd William Parrott as the suspect.

DNA had been collected. But the case was dropped. The sample never entered CODIS.

Until now.

When forensic analysts finally uploaded that 1996 DNA profile in early 2026, the system exploded.

**MATCH.**

June 1990 rape. August 1990 double murder. August 1996 sexual assault.

All Floyd William Parrott.

Detectives confirmed he wasn't in custody during any of the attacks. They confirmed his 1988 work address. They confirmed his revolver matched the rape victim's description.

They had him.

On March 25, 2026, FBI SWAT descended on a quiet apartment complex in Lincoln, Nebraska.

Floyd William Parrott was arrested without incident.

When questioned, he allegedly told investigators:

*"I never met Cheryl Henry."*

Even then—caught, cornered, DNA evidence irrefutable—he lied.

## The Monster Sleeps No More

In Lincoln, Nebraska, Floyd Parrott lived a quiet life.

No one knew what he'd done in Houston.

No one suspected the monster sleeping in apartment 4B.

But in Texas, two families were still waiting.

And one sergeant was about to find him.
