# BOOK #3 - CHAPTERS 4-5: "The Phantom Suspect" + "Cold"

## CHAPTER 4: "The Phantom Suspect"

The morning after the bodies were discovered, Detective John "Billy" Belk stood at the edge of the cul-de-sac on Enclave Parkway and watched the sun rise over a crime scene that made no sense. Twenty-six years on the force, and he'd never seen anything like it.

A young couple, brutally murdered. The man tied to a tree, throat slashed so deep his head nearly came off. The woman sexually assaulted, bound, her body partially covered with fence boards in what looked like a half-hearted attempt to hide what happened. Items from the car—golf balls, of all things—arranged like breadcrumbs pointing toward her body. A twenty-dollar bill left behind. And a cassette tape still in the deck.

"This wasn't random," Belk told his partner that morning. "This was personal. Or it was a predator who knew exactly what he was doing."

The question was: which?

---

### The Investigation Begins

Houston Police threw everything at the case. Patrol officers canvassed the area. Helicopters swept the adjacent woods. Detectives interviewed everyone who'd been at Bayou Mama's nightclub on the night of August 22, 1990. They tracked down Cheryl's coworkers at Rick's Cabaret. They spoke to Andy's friends from Gold's Gym and Dreams Street, where his father managed the door.

The physical evidence was catalogued with painstaking care. Blood inside the driver's door of Andy's white Honda Civic. Displaced floor mats. The hemp rope used to bind their hands. And most importantly: semen collected from Cheryl Henry's body during the autopsy.

In 1990, DNA profiling was still in its infancy. The Houston Police Department crime lab didn't have the technology to process it. Belk got special permission to send the sample to Baylor College of Medicine, where researchers were pioneering forensic DNA analysis.

Weeks later, the results came back: a full DNA profile. Male. Unknown subject.

The profile was entered into CODIS—the FBI's Combined DNA Index System, a national database that had only recently been established. If the killer was already in the system, or if he committed another crime and left DNA behind, there would be a match.

The search came back empty.

---

### The Tip

On September 3, 1990—less than two weeks after the murders—an anonymous tip came into the Houston Police Department.

A caller named Floyd William Parrott as a possible suspect.

The tip went into the file. Detectives noted it. But they didn't pursue it thoroughly.

Why?

Decades later, investigators would struggle to answer that question. The records from 1990 are incomplete. No one can say for certain whether Parrott was interviewed, whether his alibi was checked, or whether detectives even knew about his criminal history at the time.

What is known is this: Floyd Parrott was not DNA tested in 1990. His name did not appear on the list of two dozen suspects who would eventually be cleared through DNA comparison. He was not arrested. He was not brought in for questioning.

The tip, it seemed, fell through the cracks.

---

### Who Was Floyd William Parrott?

If detectives had dug deeper, they would have found a man with a disturbing history.

Floyd William Parrott, twenty-eight years old in August 1990, had been arrested four times between 1988 and July 1990—all for impersonating a peace officer.

In May 1988, he'd been caught pretending to be a cop in Harris County. He was placed on probation. That same year, he was arrested for carrying a weapon—a blue steel revolver that was later returned to him after his conviction.

In May 1990, just three months before the murders, Parrott was arrested again for impersonating a deputy constable for Precincts 4 and 5. He told women he was working as a security guard. He was released on bond.

Then, in July 1990—five weeks before Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson were murdered—Parrott was arrested on two counts of impersonating a cop. He was out on bond when the murders occurred.

In that May 1988 arrest, Parrott had provided a work address to authorities. That address was just over one mile from the future murder scene—a remote, undeveloped area of west Houston that locals called "Lovers Lane."

Parrott worked as a bouncer at nightclubs and strip clubs. He worked at an auto parts store. He may have worked for a wrecker company. He moved through the same world that Cheryl and Andy inhabited—the nightlife scene, the clubs, the sprawling anonymity of Houston in the early 1990s.

But in September 1990, when that anonymous tip came in, none of this connected. Not yet.

---

### The DNA That Went Nowhere

Detective Belk and his team followed every lead they could find. Over one hundred people were looked at as potential suspects. Friends of the victims. Coworkers. Ex-boyfriends. Strangers who'd been spotted in the area. At least two dozen of them agreed to provide DNA samples.

Every single one came back negative.

The killer's DNA sat in CODIS, waiting. But the killer himself—whoever he was—wasn't in the database. Either he'd never been arrested for a crime that required DNA collection, or he'd committed his crimes before DNA databases became standard.

Months passed. Then a year.

Cheryl's family marked her birthday in February 1991. Andy's family did the same. The anniversaries were unbearable. Barbara Craig, Cheryl's mother, couldn't bring herself to drive past the turnoff to Enclave Parkway. Garland Atkinson Sr., Andy's father, stopped going to Dreams Street. The club held too many memories.

Shane Henry, Cheryl's younger sister, replayed their last night together over and over in her mind. Saying goodbye at Bayou Mama's. The kiss. "I love you." The way Cheryl had been so happy, so smitten with Andy.

"I keep thinking," Shane told detectives during one of many follow-up interviews, "if I'd just asked her to stay with me that night. If I'd told her not to go."

Belk shook his head. "This wasn't your fault. This was a predator. He was waiting."

---

### The Investigation Stalls

By 1991, the case had gone cold.

Belk refused to let it die. He kept the file on his desk. He reviewed the evidence again and again, looking for something—anything—that had been missed. He tracked down new witnesses. He re-interviewed old ones. He submitted the DNA profile to CODIS every time the database expanded, hoping for a match.

Nothing.

The problem wasn't effort. The problem was that DNA technology, while revolutionary, was still limited. CODIS only worked if the perpetrator's DNA was already in the system. And Floyd William Parrott's DNA was not.

Even if it had been, there's no guarantee detectives would have connected it to the 1990 tip. Parrott's name sat in a file somewhere, noted and forgotten. His arrests for impersonating a police officer were misdemeanors—crimes that didn't require DNA collection at the time.

He was a phantom. A name without a face. A suspect without evidence.

And he was still out there.

---

### The Families' First Year

For Barbara Craig and Garland Atkinson Sr., the first year after the murders was a blur of grief and rage.

Barbara couldn't sleep. She kept Cheryl's room exactly as it had been—the drill team photos on the wall, the stuffed animals on the bed, the closet full of clothes Cheryl would never wear again. She attended every meeting with detectives, demanded updates, pushed for answers.

"Someone knows who did this," she told reporters in a television interview in early 1991. "Someone saw something. And I will not stop until they come forward."

Garland Atkinson Sr. withdrew. He couldn't talk about Andy without breaking down. The sight of Andy's car—which had been released from evidence and returned to the family—was too much. He sold it within weeks.

"We were not only father and son but best friends," he said in one of the few interviews he gave. "You see things on TV, you read things in the paper, but you never imagine something that drastic, that horrifying, happening to someone you love."

Shane Henry carried a different kind of burden. She was the last person to see Cheryl alive. The last person to kiss her, to tell her she loved her. And now she was the one who had to keep Cheryl's memory alive.

"I won't let her become a statistic," Shane vowed. "I won't let people forget her name."

---

### What Investigators Missed

Years later, when the case finally broke, investigators would look back at 1990 and ask themselves: How did we miss him?

The answer was maddeningly simple: They weren't looking.

Floyd William Parrott had been arrested four times between 1988 and July 1990. His pattern of behavior—impersonating cops, carrying weapons, targeting women—was documented. His work address placed him within a mile of the murder scene. He was out on bond when Cheryl and Andy were killed.

But none of that mattered if no one connected the dots.

The anonymous tip in September 1990 should have been a red flag. It should have triggered an interview, a background check, a request for DNA. It didn't.

Maybe detectives were overwhelmed by the sheer number of leads. Maybe the tip wasn't specific enough. Maybe Parrott's record—misdemeanors, not felonies—didn't raise alarms.

Or maybe it was something simpler: bad luck. A piece of paper in a file that no one followed up on. A name that got lost in the shuffle.

Whatever the reason, Floyd William Parrott slipped through the cracks.

And the investigation stalled.

---

By the end of 1990, Detective Belk knew the case was slipping away. The trail was growing cold. Witnesses' memories were fading. The media had moved on to other stories.

But he made himself a promise: He would not give up. He would not let Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson become another unsolved case, another file gathering dust in a storage room.

"I don't care how long it takes," he told his wife one night. "I'm going to find the son of a bitch who did this."

It would take nineteen years.

## CHAPTER 5: "Cold"

The case went cold.

Not officially—there was no press conference, no announcement that the investigation had been suspended. But by 1992, the leads had dried up. The tips stopped coming. The DNA sat in CODIS, waiting for a match that never came.

Detective John "Billy" Belk kept the file on his desk for nineteen years.

---

### Sixteen Years of Silence

The 1990s passed in a slow blur of frustration.

Belk worked other cases—Houston's homicide rate was staggering in those years—but the Lovers Lane murders never left his mind. He attended Cheryl's birthday every February. He called Barbara Craig on the anniversary of the murders every August. He checked CODIS every few months, hoping for a match.

Nothing.

The families marked time in their own ways. Barbara Craig started a scrapbook of newspaper clippings about the case. Garland Atkinson Sr. avoided the west side of Houston entirely. Shane Henry finished college, got married, started a family—but she never stopped thinking about the sister who'd been stolen from her.

"I used to dream about her," Shane said years later. "In the dreams, she was still alive. And when I woke up, I had to remember all over again that she was gone."

The Houston community remembered, too. Every few years, a newspaper would run a retrospective. "Unsolved: The Lovers Lane Murders." Tipsters would call in—mostly cranks, a few sincere—but none of the leads panned out.

The question haunted everyone who'd worked the case: *Who did this?*

---

### New Detectives, Same Dead Ends

Detectives retired. New detectives took over. The case file—now thousands of pages thick—was passed from one investigator to the next like a baton in a race no one could finish.

Each new detective brought fresh eyes. Each one reviewed the evidence, re-interviewed witnesses, submitted the DNA profile to CODIS again. And each one came to the same conclusion: Without a DNA match, the case was stuck.

By the mid-1990s, DNA technology was advancing rapidly. What had once required a blood sample the size of a quarter could now be done with a microscopic trace. Labs were processing old rape kits, cold cases, unsolved murders. CODIS was expanding, adding profiles from convicted offenders across the country.

But the Lovers Lane killer's DNA still didn't match anyone in the system.

It was as if the killer had committed the perfect crime—not because he'd been careful, but because he'd been lucky. He'd left DNA behind, but it didn't matter. The system couldn't find him.

---

### Floyd Parrott's Other Crimes

While detectives spun their wheels, Floyd William Parrott kept moving.

In September 1995, Parrott was sentenced to twenty years in prison for impersonating a public servant. This time, he'd gone too far. Posing as the Montgomery County police chief, Parrott had pulled over a driver on the Southwest Freeway near Chimney Rock. His car had emergency lights. He had police uniforms, badges, law enforcement equipment.

He extorted $100 from the driver over an expired license plate.

When police searched his home, they found a small arsenal of police gear—uniforms, badges, radios, handcuffs. Parrott wasn't just pretending to be a cop. He was obsessed with it.

Christopher Downey, the prosecutor in the case, later recalled: "Someone who impersonates a police officer and goes to the extent to solicit a bribe, when the whole time he was acting as a fake police officer, is pretty shocking... It was a pleasure to prosecute Floyd Parrott because we had reservations about his behavior—it was downright predatory."

Parrott was sentenced to twenty years. He served three.

---

### The 1996 Assault

In 1996, while awaiting trial or out on bond, Floyd Parrott was accused of another sexual assault.

The victim was a woman who worked at a strip club. She told police that Parrott had attacked her. DNA was collected from the rape kit.

But the victim declined to cooperate with the prosecution. She didn't want to subject herself to cross-examination, didn't want to relive the trauma in court. Without her testimony, the case fell apart. A grand jury declined to indict.

Parrott admitted sexual contact but claimed it was consensual.

The DNA from that assault—collected in 1996—was stored. Filed away. And then, for reasons that remain unclear, it sat untouched for nearly thirty years.

The rape kit from 1996 should have been processed. It should have been uploaded to CODIS. It should have matched the DNA from the 1990 murders.

But it wasn't. And it didn't.

Floyd Parrott walked free.

---

### The Breakthrough That Should Have Come Sooner

In 2007, seventeen years after the murders, Harris County Sheriff's Office began processing a massive backlog of old rape kits.

One of those kits was from June 20, 1990—two months before Cheryl and Andy were killed. A thirty-year-old exotic dancer had been sexually assaulted in north Harris County. The attacker wore a fishnet stocking mask, black gloves, and uniform-style clothing. He carried a long-barreled handgun.

DNA had been collected from that assault, but in 1990, the technology to process it was limited. The case went cold.

In 2007, the DNA was finally run through CODIS.

It matched.

The same man who raped the exotic dancer in June 1990 was the same man who murdered Cheryl Henry two months later.

Detective Belk got the call from the crime lab on a Tuesday afternoon. After seventeen years, they finally had a break. The killer was a serial predator. He'd struck before. And there was a victim who'd survived.

Belk tracked down the June 1990 rape victim in Galveston County. She was still traumatized, still angry, but she agreed to work with investigators. She provided details about the attack. She helped create a composite sketch of her attacker.

The sketch was released to the media. News outlets ran the story: "DNA May Help Break Notorious 'Lover's Lane' Murders."

Surely, investigators thought, someone would recognize the face. Someone would call in a tip. The killer would be found.

But no one called.

And the composite sketch—like so many others before it—led nowhere.

---

### Detectives Retire

Detective John "Billy" Belk retired in 2007, the same week he received the news about the DNA match. He'd spent nineteen years on the case—more time than Cheryl Henry had been alive.

"I told the family I'd solve this," Belk said at his retirement party. "And I didn't. That'll haunt me for the rest of my life."

But Belk didn't give up. Even in retirement, he stayed in touch with the detectives who took over the case. He attended anniversary vigils. He called Barbara Craig and Garland Atkinson Sr. every August to let them know he was still thinking about Cheryl and Andy.

"This case will clear," he told Shane Henry during one of those calls. "I don't know when. But it will."

Detective Michael Miller inherited the case from Belk. He worked it for years, following up on the June 1990 rape connection, re-interviewing witnesses, pushing for more DNA testing. But the case remained stubbornly unsolved.

By the 2010s, Miller had moved on to other assignments. The Lovers Lane murders became a "cold case"—officially still open, but no longer actively investigated.

The file sat in storage, waiting.

---

### Technology Advances, But the Killer Stays Hidden

DNA technology exploded in the 2000s and 2010s. Genetic genealogy—using DNA to trace family trees—solved decades-old cases. The Golden State Killer, caught in 2018, was the most famous example. Cold cases across the country were being cracked open.

But the Lovers Lane case remained stuck.

The problem was simple: The killer's DNA was in CODIS. It had matched to the June 1990 rape. But it didn't match anyone in the offender database. Without a name, genetic genealogy couldn't help. The case needed a suspect.

And Floyd William Parrott's DNA still wasn't in the system.

Why not?

The 1996 sexual assault case should have provided it. Parrott had admitted sexual contact. DNA had been collected. But the case was dropped when the victim declined to testify. And for reasons that remain unclear, the DNA was never uploaded to CODIS.

Investigators would later describe the 1996 DNA as "recently placed" into CODIS—nearly thirty years after it was collected. What changed? Why was it finally uploaded in 2025 or 2026? No one has provided a clear answer.

---

### Families Mark Anniversaries

Every year on August 23, the families returned to the same painful ritual.

Barbara Craig visited Cheryl's grave. She brought flowers—lilies, Cheryl's favorite. She spoke to her daughter as if she were still alive. Told her about the family, about Shane's kids, about the life Cheryl never got to live.

Garland Atkinson Sr. did the same for Andy. But as the years passed, the weight of grief became too much. He stopped attending the vigils. He stopped giving interviews. He retreated into himself.

Shane Henry became the public face of the families' fight for justice. She spoke to reporters every year. She appeared on true crime shows. She kept Cheryl's name alive.

"People ask me why I keep doing this," Shane said in a 2023 interview. "Why I keep talking about it, why I keep pushing. And the answer is simple: Because if I don't, who will?"

---

### Houston Remembers

The Lovers Lane murders became part of Houston's collective memory. Every few years, a new generation of reporters would rediscover the case and write about it. True crime forums like Websleuths kept threads active. In 2025, the Crime Junkie podcast released an episode titled "INFAMOUS: Lovers' Lane Murders."

The case haunted Houston not because it was unique—there were plenty of unsolved murders—but because it was so visceral. A young couple, in love, their lives ended in the most brutal way imaginable. And the killer was still out there.

"That could have been any of us," one commenter wrote on a Websleuths thread in 2020. "Any couple who ever parked on a quiet road. That's what makes it so terrifying."

The question haunted everyone: *Who did this?*

---

### 1996: Another Assault

And then there was the assault that should have solved everything.

In 1996, Floyd William Parrott assaulted a woman who worked at a strip club. DNA was collected. Parrott admitted sexual contact. The evidence was there.

But the case was dropped. The DNA went into storage. And Floyd Parrott walked free.

If that DNA had been processed and uploaded to CODIS in 1996, it would have matched the 1990 murders. Floyd Parrott would have been arrested. Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson's families would have had justice thirty years sooner.

But it didn't happen.

The DNA sat in storage for decades. It was finally uploaded to CODIS in 2025 or early 2026. The match was made. And suddenly, after thirty-six years, the case had a suspect.

---

### No One Connected the Dots

By 2026, Floyd William Parrott's name appeared in three separate Houston Police Department files:

1. **1990**: An anonymous tip naming him as a possible suspect in the Lovers Lane murders.
2. **1996**: A sexual assault case where DNA was collected.
3. **1988-1995**: Multiple arrests for impersonating a peace officer.

Three files. Three opportunities to connect the dots.

But no one did.

Not in 1990, when the tip came in. Not in 1996, when the DNA was collected. Not in 2007, when the June 1990 rape was matched to the murders.

It took an anonymous tip in late 2025—and a detective with "fresh eyes" reviewing thousands of pages of case files—to finally make the connection.

Floyd William Parrott had been hiding in plain sight for thirty-six years.

---

### The Families Who Didn't Live to See Justice

In 2024, Barbara Craig and Garland Atkinson Sr. both passed away.

Barbara died never knowing who killed her daughter. Garland died never knowing who killed his son. They'd spent thirty-four years waiting for answers. Thirty-four years of anniversaries, vigils, interviews, and sleepless nights.

They died without justice.

"It's heartbreaking that they don't get to have this day," Shane Henry said when Parrott was arrested in 2026. "But we know they're with them and rejoicing in their own ways."

---

### 2025: The Tip That Changed Everything

In late 2025, someone called the Houston Police Department with a tip.

They named Floyd William Parrott as a possible suspect in the Lovers Lane murders.

It was the second time Parrott's name had been given to police in connection with the case. The first time, in 1990, it had gone nowhere.

This time, it did.

A homicide sergeant began investigating. In January 2026, Detective Michael Burrow—newly assigned to the cold case unit—began reviewing the case files with fresh eyes.

And then he found it: the 1996 sexual assault report. The one naming Floyd William Parrott as the suspect. The one where DNA had been collected.

Burrow checked CODIS. The DNA from the 1996 assault had been "recently placed" into the database.

The match came back almost immediately.

The DNA from the 1996 assault matched the DNA from Cheryl Henry's autopsy. It also matched the DNA from the June 1990 exotic dancer rape.

Three crimes. Three DNA matches. Same predator.

Floyd William Parrott.

---

### The Arrest

On March 25, 2026, FBI SWAT agents arrested Floyd William Parrott at his home in Lincoln, Nebraska.

He was sixty-four years old. He'd been living quietly in Nebraska for the past two years, far from Houston, far from the crimes he'd committed.

The next day, Parrott appeared in a Nebraska courtroom. He was told he was being charged with two counts of capital murder.

Parrott denied everything. He told investigators he'd never met Cheryl Henry.

But the DNA didn't lie.

---

### The Press Conference

On March 27, 2026, Harris County District Attorney Sean Teare stood at a podium in the Criminal Justice Center and announced the arrest.

Next to him stood Houston Police Chief Noe Diaz, retired Detective Billy Belk, and Shane Henry.

"We feel really comfortable in saying that he's been preying on this community for a long time," Teare said. "If you recognize this individual and he pulled you over, if he impersonated an officer, call us. If you met him once, if you met him at a club, if you knew him at all, reach out."

Shane Henry stood at the microphone, her voice steady but her eyes wet.

"For thirty-six years, we've waited for this day," she said. "Cheryl and Andy will never be forgotten. And now, finally, we have justice."

Billy Belk, eighty years old and long retired, wiped his eyes.

"I told you this case would clear," he said. "I told you."

---

Floyd William Parrott's name sat in two separate police files—1990 and 1996. Two crimes. Two DNA matches. Same predator.

No one connected the dots.

Not yet.
