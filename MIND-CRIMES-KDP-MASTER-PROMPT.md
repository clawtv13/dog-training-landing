**PROLOGUE (800-1,000 words)**
- Start with the MOST tense moment (not necessarily chronological)
- Hook: Make reader ask "What happened here?"
- Example opening styles:
  * "The 911 call came at 2:47 AM. The operator would later say she'd never forget that voice."
  * "She was supposed to be home by 10 PM. By midnight, her mother knew something was wrong."
  * "The last person to see him alive was a gas station clerk, who remembered one detail: his hands were shaking."

**ACT 1: THE WORLD BEFORE (Chapters 1-3, ~25% - 5,000 words)**

Chapter 1: "The Normal Life" (2,000 words)
- Introduce victim as PERSON, not statistic
- A day in their life (routine, relationships, dreams)
- Show what they were like: habits, quirks, personality
- Use specific details: "She always ordered vanilla lattes with extra foam"
- Build empathy through mundane humanity
- Foreshadow subtly: "She had no way of knowing this would be her last [normal day/week]"

Chapter 2: "The Warning Signs" (1,500 words)
- Build tension with small anomalies
- Things that seemed normal at the time but now look sinister
- Multiple POVs: family noticing changes, friends last conversations
- Introduce suspect/danger (if known) or mysterious circumstances
- End with cliffhanger: something feels wrong

Chapter 3: "The Day Everything Changed" (1,500 words)
- The crime occurs (or disappearance, or last sighting)
- Minute-by-minute reconstruction (based on evidence)
- Sensory detail: what the scene looked like, sounded like, felt like
- Show immediate aftermath: discovery, 911 call, first responders
- Chapter ends at moment of highest tension: "And then, she was gone."

**ACT 2: THE INVESTIGATION (Chapters 4-7, ~50% - 10,000 words)**

Chapter 4: "The First 48 Hours" (2,500 words)
- Police response, initial investigation
- Family's growing panic
- Media attention beginning
- First leads, first suspects
- Detective POV: what they saw, what they thought
- Timeline reconstruction
- Cliffhanger: "But the detective was about to discover something that would change everything."

Chapter 5: "Following the Trail" (2,500 words)
- Evidence emerges piece by piece
- Interviews with witnesses (use dialogue from transcripts)
- Red herrings: false leads that seemed promising
- Reader = detective (present clues, let them theorize)
- Pacing: alternate fast (action) and slow (psychology)
- Character development: detective's obsession, family's grief
- End: major revelation or twist

Chapter 6: "The Dark Psychology" (2,500 words)
- Deep dive into criminal's background (if known)
- OR: psychological profile if unsolved
- Explore the "why": childhood trauma, mental illness, triggers
- Humanize without excusing
- Interviews with psychologists, criminologists
- Reflective tone: "What drives a person to cross that line?"
- Connect to broader themes: societal failures, warning signs missed

Chapter 7: "Breaking Point" (2,500 words)
- Major breakthrough in case
- OR: the moment everything fell apart (for unsolved)
- Confrontation, arrest, or devastating realization
- Multiple POVs converge: detective, family, suspect
- High tension, fast pacing
- Cliffhanger before Act 3: "The truth was finally about to come out."

**ACT 3: RESOLUTION & AFTERMATH (Chapters 8-10, ~25% - 5,000 words)**

Chapter 8: "The Trial" or "The Confession" (2,000 words)
- Courtroom drama (if applicable)
- OR: final confrontation, confession details
- Key testimony, evidence presented
- Victim's family in courtroom (emotional impact)
- Moment of truth: verdict, revelation, or realization
- Justice (or lack thereof)

Chapter 9: "The Aftermath" (1,500 words)
- Sentencing or outcome
- Impact on families (victim's and criminal's)
- What happened next: where are they now?
- Community response, changes made
- Unsolved: current status, ongoing efforts
- Longer-term trauma, healing (or lack of)

Chapter 10: "Reflections" (1,500 words)
- Bigger picture: what this case teaches us
- Systemic issues revealed
- Psychology of evil, justice, human nature
- Memorial for victim: who they were, legacy
- Call to action (if appropriate): awareness, prevention
- Respectful close that honors victim

**EPILOGUE (500-800 words)**
- Where things stand today
- Recent developments (if any)
- Final thoughts on the case
- "Some questions may never be answered, but [victim's name] will not be forgotten."

PART 3: WRITING STYLE REQUIREMENTS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

TONE & VOICE:
✅ Gripping but respectful
✅ Empathetic not sensational  
✅ Investigative feel (reader as detective)
✅ Literary quality (not tabloid)
✅ Emotional depth without manipulation

❌ NO sensationalism ("gruesome," "horrific," "blood-soaked")
❌ NO victim-blaming (ever)
❌ NO glorifying violence
❌ NO graphic details for shock value
❌ NO AI patterns ("delve into," "it's important to note")

NARRATIVE TECHNIQUES:

**1. Show, Don't Tell**
❌ "She was scared."
✅ "Her hands trembled as she reached for the phone. The dial tone felt too loud in the silent house."

**2. Sensory Details**
- Sight: specific colors, textures, facial expressions
- Sound: ambient noise, voices, silence
- Smell: distinctive scents that trigger memory
- Touch: temperature, textures
- Taste: (when relevant)

Example: "The apartment smelled of stale coffee and something metallic—blood. The only sound was the hum of the refrigerator and the distant wail of sirens."

**3. Dialogue**
- Use exact quotes from transcripts, interviews, court records
- Format: "Quote," [source said/testified/recalled].
- Make it natural, conversational
- Break up exposition with dialogue

**4. Pacing Control**
FAST scenes (short paragraphs, action, tension):
> "She ran. The footsteps behind her grew louder. Her breath came in gasps. The door—just twenty feet away. Fifteen. Ten."

SLOW scenes (longer paragraphs, reflection, psychology):
> "In the weeks that followed, the detective would replay that moment a thousand times. What had he missed? There must have been a sign, something in the suspect's eyes or voice that would have given it away. But in the chaos of the investigation, with pressure mounting from the media and the family desperate for answers, it had been easy to overlook the small details. And sometimes, he realized, those small details are everything."

**5. Foreshadowing**
Plant hints early that pay off later:
- "She noticed the car parked across the street. Later, she would remember that detail."
- "If he had known what the next hour would bring, he might have made a different choice."

**6. Cliffhangers** (end of chapters)
- Questions: "But who had made that call?"
- Revelations: "The detective was about to discover something that would change everything."
- Tension: "She opened the door. And then, she screamed."

**7. Multiple Perspectives**
Alternate between:
- Victim's timeline (reconstructed from evidence)
- Detective's investigation (first-person style)
- Family's experience (interviews, testimony)
- Suspect's background (third-person psychological)

**8. Time Manipulation**
NOT strictly chronological:
- Open with climax, flashback to setup
- Reveal timeline out of order for maximum impact
- Use chapter breaks to jump time/perspective
- Signal clearly: "Three days earlier..." "Meanwhile..."

PART 4: CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT REQUIREMENTS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

**VICTIM (Most Important)**

NOT a statistic. A PERSON.

Include:
- Physical description (height, hair, style, distinguishing features)
- Personality (introverted/extroverted, serious/funny, anxious/confident)
- Daily routine (what they did every morning, their favorite coffee shop)
- Relationships (friends, family, romantic—with specific anecdotes)
- Dreams and goals ("She was three months from her nursing degree")
- Quirks ("He always wore mismatched socks on purpose")
- Fears and struggles (humanizing vulnerabilities)
- Last known activities (minute-by-minute where possible)
- What people said about them (quotes from friends/family)

Example depth:
"Sarah Chen was 23 when she disappeared. To her friends, she was the one who always texted back immediately, who remembered everyone's coffee order, who cried during commercials. She'd wanted to be a marine biologist since she was seven and first visited the Monterey Bay Aquarium. On her bedroom wall, she kept a sticky note that said 'Dream big, work hard, stay kind'—words her grandmother had written for her before she died. Her mother would later say she could still smell Sarah's coconut shampoo in the house. Some details never fade."

**CRIMINAL/SUSPECT**

Humanize without excusing:
- Childhood background (abuse, neglect, normalcy?)
- Education, work history
- Relationships (family, romantic partners)
- Mental health history (diagnosed or evident)
- Warning signs people missed
- The "breaking point" or escalation pattern
- Psychology: why did they do it?
- Quotes from those who knew them: "He seemed so normal"

Show the person, then show the monster. The contradiction IS the story.

**DETECTIVE/INVESTIGATOR**

Give them personality:
- Name, age, experience level
- Personal stake (first case? haunted by past failure?)
- Investigation style (methodical, intuitive, aggressive)
- Moments of doubt, breakthrough insights
- Emotional toll of the case
- Quotes from interviews or press conferences

**FAMILY MEMBERS**

Not just "the grieving family":
- Individual personalities and relationships
- How each processed the tragedy differently
- Specific memories they shared
- Their advocacy or withdrawal
- Years later: healing or haunting

PART 5: SCENE SETTING REQUIREMENTS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LOCATION AS CHARACTER:

**Specific Details:**
- Street names, landmarks, local businesses
- Weather on key dates ("It had been raining for three days")
- Time of day (lighting, ambient sound)
- Season and its impact
- Local culture (small town vs city, regional flavor)

**Historical/Cultural Context:**
- What was happening in the world at that time?
- Social norms, technology available
- Economic conditions, community tensions
- Why this mattered to the case

**Sensory Immersion:**
Every scene needs:
- What you'd SEE (colors, movement, facial expressions)
- What you'd HEAR (traffic, voices, silence)
- What you'd SMELL (distinctive scents anchor memory)
- What you'd FEEL (temperature, textures)

Example:
"The diner where she was last seen was the kind of place where everyone knew everyone's order. Red vinyl booths, black-and-white checkered floor, the smell of bacon grease and burnt coffee that never quite left. The waitress, Maria, had worked there for fifteen years. She remembered that night clearly: it was raining, the kind of heavy downpour that makes you want to stay inside. Sarah sat in the corner booth, the one by the window. She ordered her usual—pancakes, scrambled eggs, orange juice. She was on her phone, texting someone. Maria remembered thinking she seemed nervous. Her leg bounced under the table. She left at 10:47 PM. They knew the exact time because the cash register stamped every receipt. She left a $5 tip on a $12 check. She always tipped well."

PART 6: RESEARCH & ACCURACY REQUIREMENTS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

EVERY fact must be verifiable from:
- Court documents and transcripts
- Police reports
- News archives (contemporary and retrospective)
- Interviews (conducted or previously published)
- Expert analysis (forensics, psychology)

CITATION style (subtle, in-text):
- "According to court testimony..."
- "Police reports later revealed..."
- "In a 2019 interview, the detective recalled..."
- "The autopsy report stated..."

ACCURACY CHECKLIST:
□ All dates verified
□ All names spelled correctly
□ Timeline confirmed
□ Locations accurate
□ Quotes attributed to sources
□ Scientific/legal terms used correctly
□ No assumptions presented as fact

WHEN UNKNOWN:
✅ "The exact timeline is unclear, but evidence suggests..."
✅ "Investigators theorize that..."
✅ "It's likely that... though this has never been confirmed."

❌ "He was thinking..." (unless source for this)
❌ "She felt terrified..." (unless witness to this)

PART 7: ETHICAL REQUIREMENTS (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

**RESPECT FOR VICTIMS:**
1. Always use their full name (not "the victim")
2. Honor their humanity (not just how they died)
3. Include their accomplishments, dreams, loved ones
4. End with their legacy, not their death

**SENSITIVITY TO FAMILIES:**
1. Acknowledge their ongoing pain
2. Don't sensationalize their grief
3. If family participated: thank them properly
4. If family objects to coverage: note this respectfully

**TREATMENT OF VIOLENCE:**
1. Describe WHAT happened, not graphic HOW (unless legally necessary)
2. Clinical when needed: "The autopsy revealed..." not "The blood-soaked..."
3. Implication > Explicit Detail
4. Reader should understand severity without trauma porn

**BALANCE:**
1. Humanize criminal to explain, not excuse
2. Avoid armchair psychology without expert input
3. Respect "innocent until proven guilty" for ongoing cases
4. Acknowledge unsolved cases remain mysteries

**DISCLAIMER (include in front matter):**
"This book is a work of narrative non-fiction based on extensive research, including court documents, police reports, news accounts, and interviews. While every effort has been made to ensure factual accuracy, some details have been reconstructed based on available evidence. This work honors the memory of [victim name] and seeks to understand [the case/the circumstances] with respect and integrity."
