## CHAPTER 8: The Sergeant Who Wouldn't Quit

**Late 2025**

The file had been sitting in the cold case archive for thirty-five years. Thirty-five summers of unanswered questions. Thirty-five Augusts when families lit candles instead of celebrating birthdays. Thirty-five years of a killer walking free.

For most of Houston, the Lovers Lane Murders had become urban legend—a cautionary tale parents told teenagers about parking in dark places. The case was "unsolved," which is police-speak for "we gave up." But one Houston Police sergeant refused to accept that word.

Late in 2025, as the holiday season approached and Houston prepared for another year of forgetting, that sergeant sat down with a decades-old file and a tip that had just come in. The tip was anonymous, like so many before it. It named a suspect: **Floyd William Parrott**.

The name meant nothing at first. The sergeant ran it through the system.

Floyd William Parrott. White male. Age 63. Last known address: Lincoln, Nebraska. 

Criminal history?

The screen lit up.

*1988: Impersonating a peace officer.*  
*1988: Carrying a weapon.*  
*1990: Impersonating a peace officer (again).*  
*1990: Impersonating a peace officer (again).*  
*1995: Impersonating a public servant. 20-year sentence.*  
*1996: Sexual assault allegation. Grand jury no-billed.*

The sergeant leaned back. This guy had spent his entire adult life pretending to be a cop. Pulling people over. Flashing fake badges. Playing dress-up in police uniforms.

But what did this have to do with Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson?

The sergeant kept digging. Cross-referenced the Parrott file with the 1990 murder case. Searched for any mention of Parrott in the thousands of pages of investigative notes.

Nothing.

Parrott had never been interviewed. Never been questioned. Never even been on the radar.

*How was that possible?*

The sergeant pulled Parrott's arrest records again. The 1988 impersonation charge—the first one—listed a work address. The sergeant opened Google Maps and typed it in.

The location dropped a pin. The sergeant zoomed out.

One mile.

Parrott's workplace in 1988 was **one mile** from the spot where Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson were murdered in 1990.

The sergeant's pulse quickened. This remote, undeveloped cul-de-sac wasn't the kind of place you stumbled upon. It was the kind of place you knew about. The kind of place you worked near. The kind of place you scoped out.

The sergeant pulled the next arrest: 1990, May. Impersonating a peace officer again. Parrott claimed to be a deputy constable and told at least one woman he worked security. Released on bond.

*He was out on bond in August 1990.*

The sergeant's hand hovered over the keyboard. Could it really be this simple? Could a killer who had evaded capture for thirty-five years have been hiding in plain sight the entire time—his name sitting in police records less than a year before the murders?

The sergeant ran another search. This time, the query was different:

**"Parrott" + "sexual assault" + "1996"**

A single hit came back: a Houston Police Department report from August 1996. A woman who worked at a strip club had reported being sexually assaulted by a man named Floyd William Parrott. She later declined to cooperate—too afraid to face cross-examination. The case never went to trial.

The sergeant's eyes locked on one line in the report:

*DNA sample collected.*

That's when the pieces clicked.

---

**December 2025**

Detective Michael Burrow was new to the Houston Police Department's cold case unit. He'd joined in January 2025, full of optimism about solving the unsolvable. His colleagues had warned him: cold cases were cold for a reason. The easy ones were already solved. What remained were the ghosts—cases so tangled, so contaminated by time and missing evidence, that justice felt impossible.

But Burrow liked puzzles. And when the sergeant handed him the Lovers Lane file with a Post-it note that read, *"Check Parrott—1996 sexual assault report,"* Burrow dove in with fresh eyes.

He started with the basics. Printed out the 1996 sexual assault report. Read it cover to cover. The victim's story was harrowing: a man posing as security had forced his way into her life, then her body. She'd been terrified. She'd reported it. She'd given DNA evidence.

And then she'd disappeared from the case.

Burrow cross-referenced the 1996 report with the 1990 murders. He pulled up the DNA profile from Cheryl Henry's sexual assault kit—the semen sample that had been collected during her autopsy and entered into CODIS back in 1991.

Then he checked the 1996 DNA sample.

The note in the file said the 1996 DNA had been "recently placed" into CODIS.

*Recently?* Burrow frowned. That case was nearly thirty years old. Why was the DNA only *recently* uploaded?

He didn't have an answer. But what he *did* have was a hunch.

Burrow opened CODIS and ran a query: **DNA profile match—Henry autopsy kit.**

The database churned. Then it blinked.

**MATCH FOUND.**

Two matches, actually.

The first was familiar: the June 20, 1990 sexual assault of an exotic dancer in north Harris County. That match had been discovered back in 2007—a huge break at the time. The same man who raped that woman had killed Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson. But the suspect had never been identified.

Until now.

Because the second match was new.

**DNA from 1996 sexual assault case: Suspect Floyd William Parrott.**

Burrow stared at the screen.

Same DNA. Three cases.

- June 1990: Rape of exotic dancer
- August 1990: Murder of Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson
- August 1996: Sexual assault allegation against Parrott

Parrott had *admitted* to sexual contact with the 1996 victim. He'd claimed it was consensual. She'd said it wasn't. The grand jury hadn't indicted.

But his DNA was in the system. And that DNA matched the Lovers Lane killer.

**Eureka.**

---

The next question was simpler but no less critical: *Could Parrott have committed these crimes?*

Burrow checked Parrott's incarceration records.

- **June 20, 1990:** Parrott was *not* in custody. He was out on bond awaiting trial for impersonating a cop.
- **August 22-23, 1990:** Parrott was *not* in custody. Still on bond.
- **August 1996:** Parrott was *not* in custody. He was between prison sentences—had served a short stint for earlier impersonation charges and was awaiting trial for a 1995 arrest where he posed as a *police chief* and extorted $100 from a driver on the Southwest Freeway.

Parrott had the opportunity. He had the criminal history. And now, thanks to CODIS, he had the DNA.

Burrow pulled up Parrott's last known address: Lincoln, Nebraska.

Time to build a case.

---

**January 2026**

Burrow wasn't working alone. Lead attorney Samantha Knecht from the Harris County District Attorney's cold case division had been on the Lovers Lane case for over a decade. When Burrow brought her the DNA match, she didn't celebrate. Not yet.

"We need it airtight," she said. "Thirty-six years is a long time. Defense will tear apart anything sloppy."

They started with the evidence:

1. **DNA match:** Three cases, one profile. Semen from Cheryl Henry's body matched DNA from Parrott's admitted sexual contact in 1996. Match probability? One in trillions.

2. **Opportunity:** Parrott was not incarcerated during any of the three incidents. He was living in Houston, working in nightclubs, and had familiarity with the area where the murders occurred.

3. **History:** Parrott's pattern of impersonating law enforcement—complete with fake badges, uniforms, and weapons—indicated he used authority to control and intimidate victims. The 1990 rape victim had described her attacker wearing uniform-style clothing and carrying a long-barreled handgun. Parrott owned a blue steel revolver matching that description.

4. **Geographic link:** Parrott's 1988 work address was one mile from the murder scene—a remote, undeveloped cul-de-sac that locals called Lovers Lane. It wasn't the kind of place you found by accident. It was the kind of place you *knew about*.

5. **Victimology:** Both Cheryl Henry and the June 1990 rape victim worked in adult entertainment. Parrott had worked as a bouncer at nightclubs and strip clubs. He had access. He had opportunity. He had motive.

It all fit.

But there was one problem: they didn't have *fresh* DNA from Parrott. The 1996 sample was stored evidence. Defense could argue contamination, chain of custody issues, lab errors from thirty years ago.

They needed a new sample.

They needed Parrott to give them his DNA without knowing it.

---

**February 2026**

Parrott had no idea he was being watched.

He lived quietly in Lincoln, Nebraska, in a modest house on a tree-lined street. He was 64 years old now—gray-haired, retired, unremarkable. His neighbors probably thought he was a nice old man. Maybe a grandfather. Maybe someone who'd lived a normal, quiet life.

They had no idea.

Houston Police coordinated with the FBI. Surveillance teams set up shop. They watched Parrott's house. Watched his routines. Watched him take out the trash.

That's when they struck.

On a cold February morning, after Parrott wheeled his trash bin to the curb and went back inside, agents moved in. They pulled the bag. Inside: coffee grounds, food wrappers, junk mail.

And a discarded soda can.

The can was logged, sealed, and sent to the lab.

Two weeks later, the results came back: **DNA match.**

Floyd William Parrott's fresh DNA matched the DNA from Cheryl Henry's autopsy. No contamination. No lab error. No doubt.

It was him.

---

**March 2026**

District Attorney Sean Teare reviewed the evidence. Thirty-six years of waiting. Thirty-six years of families asking, *"When will you catch him?"* Thirty-six years of a killer living free, probably hurting others, probably never losing a night's sleep over the two young people he'd butchered in the woods.

Teare looked at Burrow, Knecht, and the assembled team.

"File the charges."

On March 24, 2026, an arrest warrant was issued for Floyd William Parrott. Two counts of capital murder. The penalty? Death or life without parole.

Teare picked up the phone and called the FBI.

"He's in Lincoln. Let's bring him home."

## CHAPTER 9: The Trap

**March 25, 2026 — 6:00 AM — Lincoln, Nebraska**

The morning was quiet. Frost clung to the grass. The street was empty except for a few early risers walking dogs, sipping coffee, starting their day.

Inside the house on the tree-lined street, Floyd William Parrott was asleep.

Two blocks away, the FBI SWAT team was wide awake.

They'd arrived in Lincoln the night before—two unmarked SUVs, tactical gear stowed in the back, body armor under windbreakers. They'd reviewed the case file one last time. Studied the layout of Parrott's house. Discussed contingencies. Parrott had a history with weapons. He'd carried guns before. He'd impersonated cops. He knew how law enforcement operated.

Would he resist?

The team leader briefed the squad: "This guy's 64, but don't assume he's harmless. He's killed before. Treat this like a high-risk arrest. We go fast, we go loud, we overwhelm him before he can think."

At 6:15 AM, the convoy rolled out. No sirens. No lights. Just the hum of engines and the crackle of radios.

By 6:25 AM, they were in position.

---

Parrott woke to the sound of pounding.

**BOOM. BOOM. BOOM.**

"FBI! Open the door!"

He sat up, disoriented. The bedroom was dim. The clock on the nightstand read 6:30.

**BOOM. BOOM. BOOM.**

"FBI! We have a warrant! Open the door now!"

Parrott stumbled to the window and pulled back the curtain. His stomach dropped.

The street was full of vehicles. Men in tactical gear surrounded his house. Rifles. Vests. Helmets.

*What the hell?*

He didn't move. Didn't reach for a weapon. Didn't run.

What would be the point?

He walked to the front door and opened it.

Eight rifles pointed at his chest.

"Hands up! Get on the ground! Now!"

Parrott complied. He dropped to his knees, then his stomach. His hands were wrenched behind his back. Cold metal cuffs bit into his wrists.

"Floyd William Parrott, you're under arrest for the capital murder of Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson."

Parrott said nothing.

They hauled him to his feet and walked him to the SUV. Neighbors had started to emerge—faces in windows, a few brave souls stepping onto porches. The FBI didn't care. They loaded Parrott into the vehicle and drove.

By 7:00 AM, Floyd William Parrott was in a cell in Lancaster County Jail, awaiting extradition to Texas.

---

**March 26, 2026 — 10:00 AM — Houston, Texas**

The Harris County District Attorney's Office released the news.

**"Suspect Arrested, Charged in 1990 Murders."**

Within minutes, the story exploded. Local news. National news. Social media. Crime forums. Podcasts.

*The Lovers Lane Murders—solved after 36 years.*

Houston Chronicle: *"ARREST MADE IN INFAMOUS LOVERS LANE COLD CASE."*

ABC13: *"Families Finally Get Answers."*

KPRC Click2Houston: *"Crucial Tip, DNA Link Leads to Capital Murder Arrest."*

The press conference was scheduled for the next day.

---

**March 26, 2026 — 2:00 PM — Lancaster County Jail, Nebraska**

Parrott sat across from two Houston detectives. The interview room was small, sterile, and cold. A camera in the corner recorded everything.

"Mr. Parrott, do you know why you're here?" Detective Burrow asked.

Parrott stared at the table. "You told me. Murder charges."

"That's right. Two counts of capital murder. Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson. August 23, 1990. Do you remember them?"

Parrott shook his head. "No."

"No? You don't remember them?"

"I never met them."

Burrow slid a photo across the table. Cheryl Henry. Young, smiling, alive.

"Never met her?"

Parrott didn't look at the photo. "No."

Burrow slid another photo. Andy Atkinson. Bright-eyed, full of life.

"Him?"

"No."

Burrow leaned back. "Mr. Parrott, we have your DNA. It matches semen collected from Cheryl Henry's body. We also have your DNA linked to a sexual assault in June 1990—two months before Cheryl and Andy were killed. Same DNA. Same suspect. You."

Parrott said nothing.

"We know you worked near the crime scene. We know you were arrested multiple times for impersonating a cop. We know you carried weapons. We know you targeted women in the adult entertainment industry. And we know you were out on bond the night Cheryl and Andy were murdered."

Parrott's jaw tightened. "I want a lawyer."

Burrow nodded. "That's your right."

The interview was over.

---

**March 27, 2026 — 11:00 AM — Harris County Criminal Justice Center, Houston**

The press conference room was packed. Cameras lined the back wall. Reporters filled every seat. Family members sat in the front row—Shane Henry (Cheryl's sister), other relatives, and friends who had waited thirty-six years for this moment.

District Attorney Sean Teare stepped to the podium. Beside him stood Houston Police Chief Noe Diaz, Detective Michael Burrow, and retired Detective Billy Belk—the man who had spent nineteen years on this case before retiring in 2007.

Teare began.

"Yesterday, the Houston Police Department and the FBI arrested Floyd William Parrott in Lincoln, Nebraska, on two counts of capital murder. Mr. Parrott is charged with the brutal murders of Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson, who were killed on August 23, 1990, in what became known as the Lovers Lane Murders."

He paused, letting the weight of the words settle.

"This case has haunted Houston for nearly four decades. Two young people—full of life, in love, with bright futures ahead of them—were taken in the most horrific way imaginable. Their families have waited thirty-six years for answers. Today, we can finally say: we have those answers."

Teare explained the breakthrough: the anonymous tip, the DNA match, the fresh sample, the arrest.

"We also want to be very clear about something," Teare continued. "We believe Mr. Parrott has been preying on this community for a long time. He has a documented history of impersonating law enforcement, using that authority to intimidate and control others. We believe there may be additional victims."

He looked directly into the cameras.

"If you were pulled over by someone impersonating an officer in the 1980s or 1990s. If you encountered Mr. Parrott at a nightclub, a strip club, or anywhere else. If you were victimized and never came forward—please contact us. You are not alone. We want to hear from you."

The room was silent except for the click of camera shutters.

Chief Noe Diaz spoke next. "This arrest represents the tireless work of generations of investigators. From Detective Billy Belk, who never gave up on this case, to Detective Michael Burrow, who brought fresh eyes and relentless determination. I also want to thank the FBI, the Harris County DA's Office, and everyone who kept this case alive."

Then it was Billy Belk's turn. The 70-year-old retired detective stepped to the microphone. His voice was steady, but emotion flickered across his face.

"I worked this case for nineteen years. Nineteen years of chasing leads, interviewing suspects, waiting for a break. When I retired in 2007, I told the family: 'This case will clear. I promise you.' Today, that promise is kept."

He turned to Shane Henry.

"Thank you for never giving up. Thank you for keeping Cheryl and Andy's memory alive. This is for them."

Shane Henry stood. Tears streamed down her face. She approached the podium and took the microphone.

"Cheryl was my sister. She was my best friend. When she was killed, part of me died too. For thirty-six years, I've lived with the pain of not knowing who did this. Not knowing if he'd ever be caught. Not knowing if Cheryl and Andy would ever get justice."

Her voice cracked.

"My mother—Cheryl's mother—passed away in 2024. She never got to see this day. Andy's father passed away the same year. They waited their entire lives for answers and died without them. That breaks my heart."

She wiped her eyes.

"But today, I can finally say: we know who did this. We know who took Cheryl and Andy from us. And he's going to pay."

The room erupted in applause.

---

**March 28, 2026**

The response was immediate. The DA's tip line was flooded with calls. Women—now in their 50s and 60s—called to report encounters with a man who had pulled them over in the 1980s and 1990s, flashing a badge, demanding ID, sometimes asking them to step out of their cars.

One caller said: "I was 19. He pulled me over on the Southwest Freeway. He had a badge. He said my registration was expired. He made me sit in his car while he 'ran my license.' I was terrified. Then he just… let me go. I thought I was lucky. Now I wonder if I almost became a victim."

Another: "I met him at a club in 1991. He said he was a cop. He was creepy. My friends pulled me away. I never saw him again. But I never forgot his face."

The calls kept coming.

Detectives began cross-referencing reports. Were there other unsolved cases—rapes, assaults, maybe even murders—that might connect to Parrott?

The investigation was far from over.

---

**March 29, 2026**

Parrott waived extradition. Within days, he was transported from Nebraska to Harris County Jail in Houston. He was booked, processed, and placed in administrative segregation—isolated from the general population for his own safety.

The DA's office filed a motion to hold him without bail. Given the severity of the charges and Parrott's history, the judge agreed.

Floyd William Parrott would await trial in a cell.

No bond. No freedom. No escape.

## CHAPTER 10: Justice at Last

**April 1, 2026**

Houston woke up to a changed reality. For the first time in thirty-six years, the Lovers Lane Murders had a suspect. Not a theory. Not a sketch. A living, breathing human being sitting in a jail cell, awaiting trial.

The city exhaled.

News coverage was relentless. Every major outlet ran stories. Cable news picked it up. Podcasts released emergency episodes. True crime communities on Reddit, Websleuths, and Facebook erupted in discussion.

Some were relieved: *Finally. Justice.*

Others were angry: *Why did it take so long?*

Still others were terrified: *How many others did he hurt?*

Shane Henry—Cheryl's sister—gave interview after interview. She was exhausted but determined. Every time she spoke, she made sure to say Cheryl and Andy's names. She refused to let them be reduced to "victims" or "the couple." They were people. They were loved. They mattered.

"Cheryl was brilliant," Shane said on KPRC. "She was funny, she was kind, she was *alive*. Andy was the same. They had their whole lives ahead of them. And someone took that away. For thirty-six years, we didn't know who. Now we do. And we're going to make sure he never hurts anyone again."

---

**April 3, 2026 — Harris County Courthouse**

Parrott appeared via video link for his arraignment. He wore a standard orange jumpsuit. His hair was gray and unkempt. His face was expressionless.

The judge read the charges: "Floyd William Parrott, you are charged with two counts of capital murder under Section 19.03 of the Texas Penal Code. How do you plead?"

Parrott's court-appointed attorney spoke for him: "Not guilty, Your Honor."

The judge set a preliminary hearing date for May 15, 2026, and remanded Parrott to Harris County Jail without bond.

Parrott said nothing. The screen went black.

---

**April 5, 2026 — Harris County District Attorney's Office**

Behind closed doors, prosecutors were building their case. Lead attorney Samantha Knecht and DA Sean Teare assembled the evidence:

1. **DNA:** The cornerstone. Semen from Cheryl Henry matched Parrott's profile with a probability of one in trillions. The match was confirmed by two independent labs.

2. **June 1990 rape:** DNA from that case also matched Parrott. The victim—an exotic dancer who once worked for Andy Atkinson's father—had described her attacker wearing uniform-style clothing and carrying a long-barreled revolver. Parrott owned such a weapon.

3. **1996 sexual assault:** Parrott admitted sexual contact. DNA was collected. That DNA matched the 1990 cases.

4. **Criminal history:** Decades of impersonating law enforcement. Using fake authority to intimidate, control, and extort. A clear pattern of predatory behavior.

5. **Geographic links:** Parrott's workplace in 1988 was one mile from the murder scene. He worked in nightclubs, giving him access to potential victims in the adult entertainment industry.

6. **Opportunity:** Parrott was not incarcerated during any of the three DNA-linked incidents. He was free, in Houston, and had means and opportunity.

Knecht sat back. "This is solid. Defense will try to attack the DNA—argue chain of custody, contamination, lab error. But we have *fresh* DNA from 2026 trash collection. That's ironclad."

Teare nodded. "And we have three separate incidents—three victims, same DNA. That's not coincidence. That's a pattern."

He paused.

"We're seeking the death penalty."

---

**April 10, 2026**

The families gathered at a private memorial service. It wasn't for Parrott's arrest. It was for Cheryl and Andy—a chance to remember them as they were, not as crime scene photos.

Friends from high school shared stories. Cheryl's drill team coach recalled her energy and spirit. Andy's coworkers from Gold's Gym remembered his laugh.

Shane Henry stood before the small group.

"Cheryl and Andy never got to live the lives they deserved. They never got married. Never had kids. Never grew old together. Someone stole that from them. But we're here today to say: they are not forgotten. Their love is not forgotten. And now, finally, they will have justice."

She held up two photos—Cheryl and Andy, young and smiling.

"This is how I choose to remember them. Not as victims. As *people*. As my sister. As a young man full of dreams. That's who they were. That's who they'll always be."

---

**April 15, 2026**

The tip line was still ringing. By mid-April, over 200 calls had come in. Some were credible—women reporting encounters with a man matching Parrott's description. Others were less useful—conspiracy theories, false confessions, attention-seekers.

But a few calls stood out.

A woman in her 50s called from California. She said she'd worked at a Houston strip club in 1992. A man claiming to be a cop had followed her home one night. He'd knocked on her door. She'd refused to open it. He'd left. She never reported it because she didn't think anyone would believe her.

"Can you describe him?" the detective asked.

"Older white guy. Stocky. Fake badge. He had this... look. Like he thought he owned the place."

The detective pulled up Parrott's mugshots from the 1990s.

"I'm going to send you some photos. Tell me if you recognize anyone."

Two days later, the woman called back.

"That's him. Number three. That's the guy."

---

Another call came from Galveston County. A retired nurse said she'd treated a woman in 1989 who had been sexually assaulted by a man impersonating a police officer. The victim had been too traumatized to press charges. The nurse couldn't remember the victim's name—it had been thirty-seven years—but she remembered the story.

Detectives pulled 1989 sexual assault reports from Galveston County. They found three that matched the description. One victim was deceased. One couldn't be located. The third was found living in Arizona.

When detectives called, the woman—now in her 60s—broke down in tears.

"I thought I'd never talk about it again."

"We think the man who attacked you may be connected to other cases. Would you be willing to give a DNA sample?"

There was a long silence.

"If it helps catch him… yes."

The sample was collected and sent to the lab.

---

**April 20, 2026**

The DNA results came back.

**Match.**

Floyd William Parrott's DNA matched evidence from a 1989 sexual assault in Galveston County.

That made *four* cases. Four victims. Four crimes spanning seven years.

And those were just the ones with DNA.

DA Teare held a press conference.

"We now have evidence linking Floyd Parrott to *multiple* sexual assaults across the Houston area. We believe—and I want to be very clear about this—we believe there are more victims. Women who were attacked and never came forward. Women who filed reports that were never fully investigated. Women who are still out there, carrying this trauma."

He looked into the camera.

"If you were victimized by Floyd Parrott, or by someone claiming to be a police officer in the 1980s or 1990s, *please* contact us. You deserve to be heard. You deserve justice."

---

**April 25, 2026**

The legal machinery ground forward. Defense attorneys filed motions to suppress evidence, challenge DNA collection methods, and request a change of venue due to pretrial publicity.

The prosecution filed counter-motions. Discovery requests. Witness lists.

A trial date was tentatively set for September 2026.

The wheels of justice turned slowly. But they were turning.

---

**April 28, 2026**

Shane Henry sat in her living room, staring at a photo of Cheryl. Thirty-six years. Thirty-six years of grief, anger, questions, and sleepless nights.

Now, finally, there was an answer.

But it didn't feel like closure. Closure would mean Cheryl and Andy were still alive. Closure would mean her mother and Andy's father had lived to see this day.

Instead, it felt like… acknowledgment. The world was finally acknowledging what had been taken. The system was finally working.

Shane's phone buzzed. A text from Detective Burrow.

*"Trial prep starts next week. We'll need you to testify. Are you ready?"*

Shane typed back: *"I've been ready for 36 years."*

## EPILOGUE

**April 2026**

The case is still unfolding. Floyd William Parrott sits in a cell, awaiting trial. The evidence against him is overwhelming—DNA, criminal history, geographic links, victimology. Prosecutors are confident. Defense attorneys are scrambling.

But the trial will take months. Maybe a year. The justice system is slow, especially in capital cases.

Meanwhile, the families wait. Again.

They've been waiting for thirty-six years. They can wait a little longer.

---

Shane Henry stands at the spot where Cheryl's body was found. It's not a crime scene anymore. It's a corporate park—clean, modern, bustling with workers. The woods are gone. The darkness is gone.

But Shane remembers.

"This is where my sister died," she says quietly. "This is where someone decided her life didn't matter. But he was wrong. Her life *did* matter. It still matters. And now, finally, the world knows it."

---

Across town, retired Detective Billy Belk sits in his living room. On the coffee table is a worn case file—his personal copy, kept after retirement. He's carried it for seventeen years. Now, finally, he can close it.

"I promised them," he says. "I promised Cheryl's family, Andy's family, everyone who cared—I promised this case would clear. And it did."

He smiles, just a little.

"Took thirty-six years. But justice doesn't have an expiration date."

---

The questions remain. How many other victims were there? Why was Parrott never investigated earlier, despite being arrested multiple times in the years surrounding the murders? Why did it take nineteen years after the DNA breakthrough in 2007 to finally connect the dots?

Some of those questions have answers. Others may never.

But what *is* clear is this:

**Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson were young, in love, and full of dreams.**

**On August 22, 1990, someone took all of that away.**

**Thirty-six years later, science and one determined sergeant gave them justice.**

**But it will never bring them back.**
