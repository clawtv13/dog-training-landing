# BOOK #2: MIND CRIMES
## CHAPTER 2: Vanished

Good Friday, April 1, 1988. Fort Wayne, Indiana.

The afternoon sun was doing that thing it does in early spring—promising warmth but delivering just enough chill to make you zip up your jacket. Eight-year-old April Tinsley didn't mind. She had her umbrella, she had permission to play, and she had friends waiting.

April was a tornado of blonde hair and laughter. The kind of kid who sang in the church choir without self-consciousness, who made friends easily, who trusted the world because the world had given her no reason not to. Her mother, Janet, described her as "the perfect daughter" she'd always wanted. Not perfect in the pristine, untouchable way, but perfect in the messy, alive, running-barefoot-through-summer kind of way.

That afternoon, April and two friends had been ping-ponging between houses on Fort Wayne's south side, the way kids did back then. No cell phones to check in, no GPS trackers sewn into backpacks. Just "be home by dinner" and the unspoken understanding that the neighborhood was safe.

Around 3:00 PM, the trio decided to move to another friend's house. April realized she'd left her umbrella behind—just three blocks back, at the house they'd been playing at earlier. Three blocks. A five-minute walk through a neighborhood where everyone knew everyone.

"I'll go get it," April said.

Her friends shrugged. Okay. See you in a minute.

That minute never came.

---

April walked alone down West Williams Street. The houses here were modest, close together, the kind of street where people sat on porches and watched the world go by. Someone should have seen her. Maybe someone did.

What we know is this: somewhere between her friend's house and home—somewhere in those three familiar blocks—April vanished.

No scream heard. No struggle witnessed. Just a little girl with an umbrella, and then nothing.

---

Janet Tinsley first felt the cold finger of worry around 6:00 PM.

April should have been home by now. Dinner was ready. Her infant brother, Paul, was in his high chair. Michael, April's father, was washing up. The table was set for four.

But April's chair was empty.

"She's probably just playing," Michael said. "Lost track of time."

Janet wanted to believe that. But the worry was already coiling in her chest, tightening with each passing minute. She walked to the neighbor's house. Had they seen April?

No. Not since earlier.

She walked to the friend's house where April had gone to retrieve the umbrella.

"She left an hour ago," the friend's mother said. "I thought she went home."

The cold finger became a fist.

---

By 6:15 PM, Janet was running now—running through the neighborhood, calling April's name. Neighbors came out of their houses, seeing the panic in Janet's face.

"April! April Marie Tinsley!"

Nothing.

At 6:30 PM, Janet called the Fort Wayne Police Department.

"My daughter is missing."

---

Officers arrived within minutes. They fanned out through the neighborhood, flashlights cutting through the growing dusk. They knocked on doors. They checked backyards, garages, abandoned buildings. They interviewed neighbors, piecing together April's last known movements.

A composite picture emerged: April had walked alone, heading back to retrieve her umbrella. And then she'd disappeared.

No one had seen a struggle. No one had heard a cry for help. It was as if she'd simply evaporated.

But children don't evaporate. Someone had taken her.

---

By 8:00 PM, the search had grown. Neighbors joined the police, armed with flashlights and hope that was already starting to curdle into dread. They checked every alley, every dumpster, every shadow. They called April's name until their voices went hoarse.

Janet stood on her front porch, shaking. Michael held her, but his hands were trembling too. Inside, baby Paul cried, oblivious to the nightmare unfolding around him.

"Where is she?" Janet whispered. "Where is my baby?"

---

The first twenty-four hours in a missing child case are critical. Every cop knows this. Every parent prays they never have to learn it.

As night fell on Fort Wayne, the search intensified. Volunteers arrived with thermoses of coffee and grim determination. The Fort Wayne Police Department mobilized every available officer. They set up a command center, coordinated search grids, contacted neighboring jurisdictions.

A detective pulled Michael and Janet aside.

"We need to ask you some questions."

Standard procedure. Statistics are brutal: most children are harmed by someone they know. Parents, relatives, family friends. So the detective asked the terrible, necessary questions.

"Has April been upset lately?"

"Any arguments at home?"

"Can you think of anyone who might want to hurt her?"

"Do you have any enemies?"

Janet answered mechanically, her voice flat with shock. No, no, no. April was happy. April was loved. April had no reason to run away and every reason to come home.

The detective believed her. But belief doesn't find missing children.

---

Midnight came and went. April had been missing for nine hours.

Volunteers searched through the darkness, their flashlight beams bobbing like desperate prayers. They checked abandoned buildings, construction sites, drainage ditches. They walked the route April would have taken, over and over, as if repetition could reveal what they'd missed.

Nothing.

Fort Wayne is a mid-sized city in northeast Indiana—population around 170,000 in 1988. Big enough to have crime, small enough that everyone knew each other. The kind of place where people left their doors unlocked, where kids walked to school alone, where the idea of a child being abducted in broad daylight was unthinkable.

Until now.

---

Saturday, April 2, 1988. Day two.

Janet Tinsley hadn't slept. How could she? Somewhere out there, her daughter was scared, hurt, alone. Or worse. No—she couldn't think about worse. She had to believe April was alive.

The search resumed at dawn. More volunteers arrived—dozens, then hundreds. Teachers from Fairfield Elementary School, where April was a second-grader. Parents who'd seen the news and thought about their own children. Off-duty cops. Strangers who'd simply heard the name "April Tinsley" and felt compelled to help.

They formed search lines and walked through fields, forests, abandoned lots. They checked dumpsters, drainage pipes, anyplace a child's body might be hidden.

Body. The word hung in the air, unspoken but omnipresent.

---

The Fort Wayne Police Department released a statement to the media. Eight-year-old April Tinsley, blonde hair, blue eyes, last seen wearing... The description was broadcast on every local news station, printed in every newspaper.

HAVE YOU SEEN THIS GIRL?

Tips flooded in. A girl matching April's description was seen at a gas station. No—that was someone else. A suspicious van was spotted near Williams Street. Investigators chased it down. Dead end. A neighbor remembered seeing a man watching children play. Police interviewed him. Nothing.

False leads, red herrings, desperate hope grasping at shadows.

---

Janet stood in the living room, staring at April's school photo on the mantel. That smile. That bright, trusting smile.

"Come home, baby," Janet whispered. "Please come home."

The phone rang constantly. Reporters wanting a statement. Friends offering support. Police providing updates that weren't really updates. We're doing everything we can. We haven't given up hope.

But after forty-eight hours, hope was becoming a harder thing to hold onto.

---

Sunday, April 3, 1988. Day three.

Easter Sunday. Churches across Fort Wayne were packed. But many congregations had empty pews—people were out searching for April instead of celebrating the resurrection.

At Faith United Methodist Church, where April sang in the children's choir, the pastor led the congregation in prayer.

"Lord, bring April home safely. Protect her, wherever she is. Give her family strength. Guide the searchers. Let this story have a happy ending."

Amen. Amen. Please, God, amen.

But God, it seemed, wasn't listening.

---

The search had now expanded beyond Fort Wayne. Neighboring counties joined the effort. The FBI got involved, bringing additional resources and expertise. Bloodhounds tracked April's scent from her home to... nowhere. The trail went cold.

Helicopters circled overhead, scanning fields and forests. Divers checked the St. Joseph River and every pond, creek, and retention basin within a five-mile radius.

Nothing. Nothing. Nothing.

It was as if April had simply ceased to exist.

---

Janet Tinsley appeared on the evening news, her face a mask of barely controlled terror.

"If someone has April, please—please—just let her go. Bring her home. She's just a little girl. She hasn't hurt anyone. Please."

Her voice cracked. The camera lingered on her tears, broadcasting her anguish to the entire region.

Michael stood beside her, one hand on her shoulder, his own face grey with exhaustion and fear. Baby Paul was with relatives—too young to understand why mommy was crying, why strangers filled the house, why everyone kept saying his sister's name.

"April is the perfect daughter," Janet continued, her voice barely above a whisper now. "She's sweet and kind and she trusts people. She wouldn't have fought if someone told her to come with them. She would have trusted them."

That trust. That beautiful, terrible trust that children have before the world teaches them fear.

Someone had exploited that trust. Someone had looked at a little girl with an umbrella and seen an opportunity.

---

Monday, April 4, 1988. Day four.

The sun rose on another day of searching. Volunteers assembled at the Fort Wayne Police Department headquarters, receiving grid assignments and words of encouragement. Don't give up. Keep looking. She's out there.

Twenty miles northeast of Fort Wayne, in Spencerville—a tiny unincorporated community in DeKalb County—a jogger was taking his morning run along County Road 68.

It was a rural route, surrounded by farm fields and scattered houses. Quiet. Peaceful. The kind of place where nothing ever happened.

The jogger noticed something in the ditch beside the road. At first he thought it was a discarded doll or a bag of trash. He almost kept running.

But something made him stop. Made him look closer.

And then he saw.

Blonde hair. A small hand. A child.

The jogger stumbled backward, his hand over his mouth. He ran to the nearest house and pounded on the door.

"Call 911. Now. There's a body. There's a little girl."

## CHAPTER 3: The Search

The call came into the Fort Wayne Police Department at 7:42 AM.

Body found. County Road 68. Female child. Possible match for April Tinsley.

For three days, hundreds of people had searched. For three days, they'd prayed for a miracle. And now, twenty miles from where she'd vanished, April had been found.

But miracles come in only one form. Bodies come in the other.

---

Detective work is methodical. Careful. By-the-book. But when the call came in about the body in Spencerville, detectives didn't walk—they ran.

The DeKalb County Sheriff's Department had already secured the scene. Yellow tape cordoned off a section of County Road 68. The jogger who'd found April sat in a deputy's cruiser, shaking, still in shock.

The ditch was shallow, unremarkable. The kind of place you'd drive past a thousand times without noticing. But now it would be forever marked as the place where April's story ended.

Investigators approached carefully, cameras clicking, documenting everything. The body was partially concealed, one shoe missing. April's clothing was disheveled but identifiable.

One of the detectives—a veteran who'd seen more death than any human should—felt his throat tighten. He had a daughter April's age.

"It's her," he said quietly. "It's April Tinsley."

---

At the Fort Wayne Police Department, officers prepared to deliver the news no parent should ever receive.

Janet and Michael Tinsley had been awake for four days straight, sustained only by coffee, cigarettes, and the rapidly dimming hope that their daughter was still alive. When the detectives arrived at their home that morning, Janet knew before they said a word.

"No," she said. Just that. "No."

"Mrs. Tinsley—"

"NO!"

She collapsed. Michael caught her, but he was falling too, folding into himself, his strength gone. The detectives steadied them both, guided them to the couch, said the words that would echo in their minds forever.

"We found April. I'm so, so sorry."

Janet's scream could be heard three houses down.

---

The medical examiner's report would come later, with its clinical language and autopsy findings. But even without the report, investigators could see what had happened.

April had been sexually assaulted. Strangled. Her death hadn't been quick or painless. Someone had stolen her, violated her, ended her life, and discarded her like trash beside a rural road.

The cause of death was asphyxiation. Manual strangulation. Someone had wrapped their hands around an eight-year-old girl's throat and squeezed until she stopped breathing.

The autopsy would reveal more horrors—evidence of sexual assault, both before and after death. Whoever had taken April hadn't just murdered her. They'd brutalized her in ways that made even hardened detectives want to vomit.

But those details wouldn't be released to the public. The Tinsley family would be spared the full truth, though the partial truth was devastating enough.

---

By noon, the news had spread across Fort Wayne.

APRIL TINSLEY FOUND DEAD.

The volunteers who'd spent three days searching felt the news like a physical blow. They'd hoped. They'd prayed. They'd walked until their feet bled, searched until their voices gave out. And it hadn't mattered.

Some volunteers broke down crying in the street. Others just stood silent, processing the awful reality. A few drove to April's Garden—the memorial that would be built years later—and left flowers at the spot where her home had been.

At Fairfield Elementary School, counselors were called in to help students process the loss. Eight-year-olds don't understand death, not really. But they understood that their friend April wasn't coming back.

Teachers wept. Parents held their children tighter. The innocence of Fort Wayne's south side died that day along with April Tinsley.

---

The Fort Wayne Police Department held a press conference at 3:00 PM.

Police Chief [name redacted] stood at a podium, flanked by FBI agents and county sheriffs. His voice was steady, professional. But his hands gripped the podium white-knuckle tight.

"At approximately 7:30 this morning, the body of April Tinsley was discovered in DeKalb County. The Allen County Coroner has confirmed her identity. This is now a homicide investigation."

Cameras flashed. Reporters shouted questions.

"Do you have any suspects?"

"Was she sexually assaulted?"

"Do you think she was killed where she was found, or elsewhere?"

The Chief raised his hand for silence.

"We are pursuing all leads. We are asking anyone with information—anyone who saw anything unusual on Friday afternoon, anyone who noticed suspicious vehicles or individuals—to contact us immediately. No detail is too small."

He paused, his jaw tight.

"Someone in this community knows who did this. Someone saw something. And we will not rest until April's killer is brought to justice."

---

But justice is a long road. And sometimes, it's thirty years long.

---

Evidence recovered from April's body included DNA—semen found on her underwear. In 1988, DNA profiling was in its infancy. The technology existed, but forensic databases didn't. There was no CODIS, no national registry of criminal DNA. The genetic material was carefully preserved, catalogued, and stored.

Maybe someday, investigators thought. Maybe someday this will matter.

It would. But not for three decades.

---

Fort Wayne Police interviewed hundreds of people. Friends, neighbors, registered sex offenders, anyone with a history of violence against children. They compiled lists, cross-referenced alibis, followed every lead no matter how slim.

They developed a composite sketch based on witness descriptions of a man seen in the area around the time April disappeared. The sketch was distributed to every police department in Indiana, posted in every post office and convenience store.

HAVE YOU SEEN THIS MAN?

Tips flooded in. John Smith looks like that sketch. My neighbor seems suspicious. That guy at the gas station was acting weird. Every tip was investigated. Every one led nowhere.

One suspect rose to the top briefly—a 34-year-old man with a history of child molestation charges. Police questioned him extensively. But his alibi held, and the physical evidence didn't match. He was cleared.

The investigation stalled. Days became weeks. Weeks became months. The leads dried up. The tips stopped coming.

April Tinsley's murder went cold.

---

On April 8, 1988—one week after April was abducted—her memorial service was held at Faith United Methodist Church.

The church was packed. Standing room only. Hundreds of people who'd searched, who'd prayed, who'd hoped against hope that April would come home.

The casket was small. Too small. A bouquet of yellow roses—April's favorite—rested on top.

Janet Tinsley sat in the front pew, supported by family members on either side. She stared straight ahead, her face blank. Shock had given way to a numbness so deep it felt like drowning.

The pastor spoke about innocence, about faith, about senseless tragedy. He said April was with Jesus now, safe in His arms. He said her suffering was over.

But what about the suffering of those left behind? What about the parents who would never see their daughter grow up? What about the little brother who would never really remember his sister?

The choir sang "Amazing Grace." Janet's lips moved, forming the words silently. But no sound came out. How could you sing about grace in a world where children were murdered?

---

April was buried at Greenlawn Memorial Park. The gravestone was simple:

APRIL MARIE TINSLEY  
1980 - 1988  
BELOVED DAUGHTER

Eight years. That's all she got. Eight years of sunshine and laughter and church choir and playing with friends. And then darkness.

---

In the weeks following April's funeral, Fort Wayne tried to move on. People returned to work, to school, to the rhythms of ordinary life. But something had shifted. Parents no longer let their children walk alone. Doors that had once stayed unlocked were now bolted. Strangers were viewed with suspicion.

The monster who'd killed April was still out there. Walking the same streets. Breathing the same air. Maybe watching the news coverage, satisfied with the chaos he'd created.

On April 20, 1988, ninety Fort Wayne residents formed a volunteer organization called APRIL—Associated Parents Regional Independent League, also known as Abduction Prevention Reconnaissance and Information League. Their mission: help solve missing children cases, prevent future abductions, keep April's memory alive.

But volunteer organizations and community vigilance don't catch killers. Police work does. Evidence does. And in 1988, the evidence led nowhere.

---

The Fort Wayne Police Department refused to give up. Every few years, they'd reopen the case, reinterview witnesses, reexamine evidence. They sent the DNA samples to different labs, hoping new technology might reveal what old technology couldn't.

In 1990, the case made headlines again—but for the worst possible reason.

Someone had written a message on a barn door in St. Joseph Township, not far from where April's body had been found. The message was scrawled in crayon:

"I kill 8 year old April Marie Tisley did you find the other shoe haha I will kill agin"

The spelling was atrocious. The tone was mocking. And the content was unmistakable—April's killer was taunting them.

Investigators descended on the barn, dusting for fingerprints, searching for DNA, interviewing everyone within a five-mile radius. They found crayons nearby—the murder weapon of mocking confession—but nothing that identified the writer.

The message proved one thing: the killer was still in the area. Still thinking about April. Still proud of what he'd done.

And still free.

---

Years passed. The case remained open but essentially dead. Janet and Michael Tinsley tried to rebuild their lives, but how do you rebuild around a void that size? They had Paul to raise, and they tried to give him a normal childhood. But April's absence was a constant presence in their home.

On anniversaries of her death, Janet would visit the grave, lay fresh flowers, and sit for hours just... remembering. April at six, missing her front teeth. April at seven, singing off-key but with absolute confidence. April at eight, walking toward death with an umbrella in her hand.

"Why?" Janet whispered to the gravestone. "Why her? Why my baby?"

The gravestone had no answers.

---

Then, in 2004, sixteen years after April's murder, the killer spoke again.

Four homes in Fort Wayne received disturbing packages. Three were left on girls' bicycles in front yards. One was placed in a mailbox. Each package contained:

- A handwritten note on lined yellow paper
- Used condoms containing semen
- Polaroid photographs showing a nude male lower body

The note read:

"Hi Honey I Been watching you I am the same person that kinapped an Rape an kill Aproil Tinsely you are my next vitem"

The spelling was still terrible. The threat was explicit. And this time, the killer had made a critical mistake—he'd left DNA.

Police tested the semen from the condoms. It matched the DNA from April's underwear. Same perpetrator. Same monster.

But without a match in CODIS—without the killer having a prior criminal record—the DNA was useless. It could confirm the killer's identity if he was ever caught. But it couldn't identify him.

The leads went cold again.

---

In 2009, more taunting messages appeared. The FBI joined the investigation, publicly calling the case "highly solvable" because of the DNA evidence. They released a statement:

"We have DNA. We have handwriting samples. We have photographic evidence. Someone knows who this person is. And when we find him—not if, but when—the evidence will be overwhelming."

The message was clear: the net was closing. Science was advancing. The killer's days were numbered.

But days became years. And the killer remained free.

---

Janet Tinsley never stopped hoping for justice, but hope is a finite resource. By 2018—thirty years after April's murder—she'd begun to accept that she might never know her daughter's killer.

In interviews on the thirtieth anniversary of April's death, Janet's exhaustion was palpable.

"The main question I would ask is why," she told reporters. "What made her stand out? That's what I want to know. Why April?"

Why indeed. Why is a child walking home selected for death? What makes a monster see innocence and decide to destroy it?

---

But in May 2018, two weeks after the Golden State Killer was arrested using genetic genealogy, Fort Wayne Police Detective Brian Martin had an idea.

What if they could use that same technique for April's case?

He contacted Parabon NanoLabs, a Virginia company specializing in genetic genealogy. He sent them the DNA sample—preserved for thirty years, waiting for this exact moment.

And then he waited.

---

Six weeks later, on July 2, 2018, genetic genealogist CeCe Moore called Detective Martin with news that would change everything.

"We've narrowed it down to two brothers," she said. "Both living in Grabill, Indiana. One of them is your killer."

The search for April's killer, cold for thirty years, was about to become white-hot.

But that's a story for another chapter.

---

The search for April was over. But the search for her killer was just beginning.

It would take thirty years.
