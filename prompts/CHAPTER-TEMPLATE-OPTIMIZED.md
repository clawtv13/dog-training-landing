## 4. TECHNICAL REQUIREMENTS

### Show, Don't Tell - MANDATORY Approach:

**✅ DO:**
- Use sensory details (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste)
- Reveal emotion through physical reactions and body language
- Show character personality through actions and dialogue choices
- Use dialogue subtext (characters saying one thing, meaning another)
- Employ environmental storytelling (setting reflects theme/emotion)

**❌ DON'T:**
- State emotions directly ("He felt sad" → "His throat tightened")
- Use generic descriptions ("She was beautiful" → show specific details)
- Over-explain motivation ("Because he was angry..." → show the anger)
- Tell us character traits ("She was cautious" → show cautious behavior)

### Narrative Techniques to Use:

**Sensory Immersion:**
```
Instead of: "The room was creepy."
Write: "Shadows pooled in the corners. The air tasted of rust and old paper. 
Something dripped in the dark—slow, rhythmic, deliberate."
```

**Body Language for Emotion:**
```
Instead of: "She was nervous."
Write: "Her thumbnail found the edge of her phone case, picking, picking. 
She couldn't meet his eyes."
```

**Subtext in Dialogue:**
```
Instead of: "I'm angry at you."
Write: "Sure. Whatever you say." [Her jaw clenched. She turned away.]
```

**Active Voice Preference:**
- Passive voice weakens prose
- Use active constructions for immediacy
- Exception: Passive voice okay when actor is unknown or irrelevant

## 5. OPENING & CLOSING GUIDELINES

### Opening (First 100-150 words):

**Purpose:** Hook the reader immediately

**Techniques:**
- Start in the middle of action or tension
- Open with compelling sensory detail
- Begin with intriguing dialogue
- Establish POV character's voice immediately

**Avoid:**
- Weather/waking up openings (unless dramatically relevant)
- Long exposition dumps
- Info-dumping about what happened before

**Example Opening Styles:**

*Action:*  
> "The alert came at 3:47 AM. No message. No sender. Just coordinates and a countdown."

*Sensory:*  
> "The lab smelled wrong—not the usual tang of ozone and antiseptic, but something organic. Rotting."

*Dialogue:*  
> "'We need to talk about Sarah.' The words hung in the air like smoke."

### Closing (Last 50-100 words):

**Purpose:** Create forward momentum, make reader want to turn the page

**Techniques:**
- End on cliffhanger question or revelation
- Close with strong final image or line
- Leave tension unresolved (for non-final chapters)
- Create emotional resonance

**If specific ending line provided:** Lead naturally to that exact line in final sentence

## 6. QUALITY CHECKLIST

Before submitting, verify:

### Story Architecture:
- [ ] Chapter advances the plot (not filler)
- [ ] Events flow logically from previous chapter
- [ ] Sets up next chapter organically
- [ ] Character motivations are clear through actions
- [ ] Conflict/tension present throughout

### Technical Execution:
- [ ] Show-don't-tell in 90%+ of prose
- [ ] No emotion labels ("angry," "sad," "nervous")
- [ ] Active voice dominates
- [ ] Varied sentence structure (mix short punchy + longer complex)
- [ ] Sensory details in every scene

### Character Work:
- [ ] Distinct voice for POV character
- [ ] Dialogue sounds natural (read aloud test)
- [ ] Each character has clear goal/conflict in scene
- [ ] Body language reveals subtext
- [ ] No on-the-nose dialogue

### Prose Quality:
- [ ] No clichés or overused metaphors
- [ ] Strong verbs (not "walked"—strode, crept, staggered)
- [ ] Minimal adverbs (especially -ly words)
- [ ] No purple prose (overly flowery)
- [ ] Tight writing (no redundant phrases)

### Genre Fit (Crime Thriller):
- [ ] Maintains tension/unease
- [ ] Pacing appropriate for scene type
- [ ] Clues/red herrings woven naturally
- [ ] Atmosphere consistent with dark tone
- [ ] Violence/darkness shown with restraint (implication > graphic)

### Word Count:
- [ ] Within target range (±200 words acceptable)
- [ ] If over: cut unnecessary description/dialogue
- [ ] If under: deepen sensory details, expand key moments

## 7. COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

### Plot Issues:
- ❌ Characters acting inconsistently for plot convenience
- ❌ Convenient coincidences solving problems
- ❌ Info dumps disguised as dialogue ("As you know, Bob...")
- ❌ Repeating information reader already knows

### Technical Problems:
- ❌ Head-hopping (changing POV within scene)
- ❌ Filtering ("She saw," "He heard," "She felt")
- ❌ Telling emotions: "fear gripped him" → show the fear
- ❌ Generic descriptions: "nice," "weird," "interesting"

### Dialogue Errors:
- ❌ Characters speaking in exposition
- ❌ Everyone sounds the same
- ❌ On-the-nose dialogue (saying exactly what they mean)
- ❌ Overusing names in conversation
- ❌ Too many dialogue tags (use "said" or action beats instead)

### Prose Weaknesses:
- ❌ Adverb overuse: "quickly ran" → "sprinted"
- ❌ Weak verbs: "went" → specific movement verb
- ❌ Redundant phrases: "nodded his head," "shrugged her shoulders"
- ❌ Purple prose: "The cerulean orbs of his eyes..."
- ❌ Overwriting: Adding unnecessary description

## 8. SUCCESS CRITERIA

A successful chapter will:

1. **Engage immediately** - First paragraph hooks reader
2. **Maintain tension** - No sagging middle; each scene has purpose
3. **Show, not tell** - Reader experiences events, not summarized
4. **Advance plot** - Story moves forward meaningfully
5. **Deepen character** - We learn about characters through behavior
6. **Build atmosphere** - Setting supports mood and theme
7. **End compellingly** - Reader wants to continue immediately
8. **Hit word count** - Appropriate length for pacing
9. **Match tone** - Consistent with book's voice
10. **Feel professional** - Polished, publishable prose
