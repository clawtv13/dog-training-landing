# PROLOGUE: The Trees Remembered

The jogger saw the rope first.

White nylon cord, pulled tight around the trunk of a pine tree twenty feet off the trail. She almost ran past it. But something made her stop—some instinct that the rope didn't belong.

August heat pressed down on the woods like a wet towel. Even at ten in the morning, the air tasted of copper and green rot. Cicadas screamed their one-note song. The jogger wiped sweat from her eyes and squinted at the rope, following its path with her gaze.

Her stomach dropped.

Not nylon. Hemp. Old hemp rope, frayed at the edges, darkened with something that wasn't tree sap.

Behind the first tree, another pine trunk bore the same scar—a groove worn into the bark where something had pulled, hard. Her feet moved before her brain caught up, stepping off the jogging trail into the underbrush. Mosquitoes rose in clouds. Branches caught at her ankles.

That's when she saw the boards.

Cedar fence slats, arranged in a pattern too deliberate to be debris. They formed a blanket over something low to the ground, something the size of—

She ran.

* * *

The security guard had found the car the night before. White Honda Civic, parked at the dead end of Enclave Parkway where the new business park was supposed to go but hadn't yet. The area was mostly dirt lots and scattered trees, the kind of place teenagers drove to when they wanted privacy.

The car had been there for hours. Key still in the ignition. Seats reclined. Cassette tape in the deck. A purse on the passenger floorboard with a woman's ID inside.

He'd called it in at sunset. By midnight, the woods were alive with flashlight beams and helicopter rotors and the wet-nose enthusiasm of cadaver dogs straining against their leads. Family members clustered at the crime scene tape, arms crossed against the night chill, eyes searching the tree line for movement.

The dogs found Cheryl Henry first.

Twenty-two years old. Face down under those cedar slats. Hands bound behind her back with the same hemp rope the jogger would see in the morning. Three ragged gashes across her throat, deep enough that the medical examiner would later struggle with the autopsy photos.

They found Andy Atkinson fifty yards deeper into the woods.

Twenty-one. Tied to a tree in a seated position, head slumped forward. His throat cut so deeply his head had nearly separated from his body. Still fully clothed. Hands bound behind him with the same rope.

The crime scene techs worked through the night, cameras flashing white in the darkness. They photographed golf balls arranged in a pattern pointing toward Henry's body—items from the Honda's trunk, placed deliberately. They bagged a twenty-dollar bill found near the scene, noted its strange placement. They collected the rope, the blood-soaked earth, the sexual assault kit from Henry's body that would sit in evidence for years before anyone knew what to do with it.

Detective John Belk stood at the edge of the scene as dawn broke, watching the medical examiner's van pull away. He'd seen bad things before. Houston in 1990 had plenty of bad things. But this—

This was different.

The rope. The staging. The methodical cruelty of making one victim watch while the other died. This wasn't rage. This wasn't a robbery gone wrong. This was someone who'd done it on purpose, who'd thought it through, who'd enjoyed it.

"How long?" he asked the ME's assistant.

"Dead since late last night. Maybe ten, twelve hours."

While their families were filing missing persons reports. While search teams were gathering. While the Honda sat empty in the dark, keys waiting in the ignition.

Belk looked at the trees. Pine and cedar, their bark scored with rope burns. Silent witnesses to what had happened here.

He made himself a promise standing there: This case would clear. No matter how long it took.

* * *

The investigation consumed Houston's attention for weeks, then months, then years. Over a hundred potential suspects were interviewed. DNA samples were compared to the profile extracted from Cheryl Henry's body—twenty-four comparisons, all cleared. The case file grew to thousands of pages. Detective Belk carried it with him for seventeen years, until retirement finally pulled it from his hands.

In 2007, a backlog of old rape kits revealed a DNA match: the same man who'd killed Cheryl and Andy had raped another woman two months earlier, in June 1990. An exotic dancer. North Harris County. The suspect had worn a fishnet stocking mask and black gloves, carried a long-barreled handgun, dressed like a cop.

A composite sketch was released. Tips came in. The trail went cold again.

Shane Henry—Cheryl's younger sister, who'd kissed her goodbye at Bayou Mama's nightclub that August night—kept the case alive through media interviews and anniversary vigils. She posted photos of her sister's smile, Andy's easy laugh. She reminded Houston that they'd been kids, just kids, out on a date in a city that should have kept them safe.

Their mothers and fathers waited. Aged. Checked the news each year on the anniversary, hoping. Both mothers, both fathers, died before seeing justice.

The trees remembered.

The woods where Enclave Parkway ended in dirt and darkness were eventually developed. A Sysco building rose where the bodies had lain. Concrete covered the blood-soaked earth. But the story didn't fade. True crime forums kept it alive. Podcasts dissected the evidence. Detectives inherited the file, read it with fresh eyes, made their own promises.

In late 2025, someone called with a name.

Floyd William Parrott. Age sixty-four. Living in Lincoln, Nebraska, twelve hundred miles from the Houston woods where he'd left two young people bound to trees in the dark.

The DNA matched. Three cases, three decades: the June 1990 rape, the August 1990 murders, a 1996 sexual assault he'd admitted to but claimed was consensual. The same profile in all three.

He'd been arrested for impersonating a police officer multiple times in 1988, 1990, and 1995—once for pulling over a driver with fake emergency lights and extorting money. He'd worked as a bouncer at Houston nightclubs. His 1988 work address was one mile from the murder scene.

He'd been there all along. Named twice in unrelated cases. Ignored twice.

On March 25, 2026, FBI SWAT arrested him at his Nebraska home. He told investigators he'd never met Cheryl Henry.

The DNA said otherwise.

* * *

It would be thirty-six years before anyone knew who had killed them. And when the truth came, it would reveal a predator who'd been named twice, ignored twice, and allowed to walk free for decades.

## August 22, 1990 — Wednesday Evening

Cheryl Henry kissed her little sister goodbye around eleven-thirty that night and promised she wouldn't be out too late. The music at Bayou Mama's was loud enough that she had to lean in close, smiling as she said it. Shane grinned back and told them—again—to get a room already. It was the third or fourth time she'd said it that evening. Cheryl and Andy couldn't keep their hands off each other.

That's what happens when you're twenty-two and you've just fallen head over heels for someone. Everything feels urgent and electric and brand new. Like the whole world just opened up in front of you.

Cheryl wasn't usually like this. Her family knew that. She didn't get "worked up over boys," as her stepsister Cristl liked to say. But Andy Atkinson was different. There was something about him—the way he laughed, the way he looked at her like she was the only person in the room. They'd only known each other two weeks, but it felt like longer. It felt like *something*.

Shane watched them leave together, Andy's arm around Cheryl's waist, both of them laughing about something she couldn't hear. She remembers thinking they looked happy. Really, genuinely happy. The kind of happy you are when you're young and in love and the future feels limitless.

She had no way of knowing it would be the last time she'd ever see her sister alive.

---

Cheryl had driven back to Houston from Nacogdoches the week before, home for the summer between semesters at Stephen F. Austin State University. She was the oldest of seven kids, the one everyone looked up to. Popular, outgoing, full of light—that's how people described her. She had that rare quality of making everyone around her feel seen, feel important. 

She'd been on the drill team at Dulles High School and then at Northwest Academy. She was coordinated, disciplined, the kind of person who showed up and put in the work. College was going well. She had dreams—she wasn't quite sure yet what shape they'd take, but they were there, shimmering just ahead of her. A degree. A career. Maybe marriage someday, kids, a house with a yard. All the normal things twenty-two-year-olds dream about when they're not thinking too hard.

For now, she was working part-time at Rick's Cabaret on Bering to make money. It wasn't glamorous, but it paid well and the hours were flexible. She didn't mind. Most of the girls there were doing the same thing—working their way through school, saving up for something, trying to get ahead. It was just a job.

And then she'd met Andy.

---

Andy Atkinson was twenty-one, tall, with an easy smile and the kind of confidence that comes from being genuinely liked. He'd moved to Houston earlier that year from North Carolina, where he'd been attending Campbell University. But he'd wanted to be closer to his dad's side of the family, so he packed up his life and moved south. That took guts. That took someone who knew what mattered.

He landed a job at Gold's Gym on the Gulf Freeway and moved in with his grandmother in Meyerland. His dad, Garland Atkinson Sr., ran a nightclub called Dreams Street on Winrock, and Andy would help out there sometimes, working the door. It gave him a window into Houston's nightlife—the clubs, the music, the people. He liked it. He was good with people. He made friends easily.

His dad would later say they weren't just father and son—they were best friends. The kind of bond where you talk about everything, where you trust each other completely. When Andy moved to Houston, Garland felt like he'd gotten a piece of his life back.

Andy was the kind of guy who made you feel comfortable the moment you met him. He had that thing—you know, where someone walks into a room and the energy shifts just a little. People smiled more. Conversations flowed easier. He wasn't trying to be anything other than himself, and that was enough.

When he met Cheryl, something just clicked.

---

The first time Shane saw them together, she could tell. There's a frequency that people in new relationships operate on—higher, faster, brighter. Cheryl and Andy were *vibrating* with it. They couldn't stop touching. Little touches—a hand on a shoulder, fingers brushing, knees pressed together under the table. The kind of touches that say *I'm here, you're here, this is real.*

At Bayou Mama's that Thursday night, they were glued to each other. The club was packed, the music was loud, and the air was thick with smoke and humidity. Houston in late August is brutal—sticky, swampy heat that clings to your skin even after the sun goes down. But they didn't seem to care. They were too wrapped up in each other.

Shane sat with them for a while, laughing at how ridiculous they were being. She loved her sister. They said "I love you" all the time. They kissed goodbye every time they parted. That was just how they were. So when Cheryl leaned over around 11:30 and said she and Andy were heading out, Shane kissed her cheek and told her to have fun.

"Don't do anything I wouldn't do," Shane said, grinning.

"That doesn't leave me with much," Cheryl shot back, laughing.

They walked out into the thick Houston night, Andy's white Honda Civic parked somewhere nearby. Cheryl's purse was slung over her shoulder. They were holding hands.

It was just a normal Thursday night. Just two people in love, heading off to be alone together. Nothing unusual. Nothing dangerous.

Everything felt safe.

---

What Cheryl didn't know—what neither of them knew—was that there was someone watching. Someone who had made a career of watching young women like her. Someone who had been arrested five times for impersonating a police officer, who carried badges and uniforms and guns and used them to control people. Someone who had raped a woman just two months earlier in north Houston, a woman who had once worked for Andy's father. Someone who was out on bond, waiting for trial, prowling the city like he owned it.

Floyd William Parrott was twenty-eight years old that summer. He worked as a bouncer at clubs and strip joints. He knew the scene. He knew the players. He knew the spots where couples went to be alone.

He'd been arrested in July—just five weeks before—on two counts of impersonating a cop. But he was out. He was always out. The system kept letting him walk.

He had a revolver. He had rope. He had a plan.

---

Cheryl and Andy didn't know any of that. How could they? They were just two kids in love, looking for a place to be alone. Somewhere quiet. Somewhere away from the noise and the crowds. 

There was a spot they knew about—a place off Enclave Parkway in west Houston, way out where the city hadn't built yet. Just woods and dirt roads and the occasional security guard making rounds. The kids called it Lovers Lane. It was secluded, private, safe. Or at least it felt that way. They'd been there before. Lots of couples went there.

It was the kind of place where you could park your car and listen to music and talk and kiss and not worry about anyone bothering you. A place where the world shrank down to just the two of you. Where everything else—work, school, family, responsibility—faded into the background for a little while.

They felt safe there.

They shouldn't have.

---

Inside the white Honda Civic, they probably had the radio on. Maybe they were listening to a cassette tape—something they both liked, something that made them feel close. The seats were reclined. The windows were cracked. The night air drifted in, hot and heavy and alive with the sound of crickets.

Cheryl probably laughed at something Andy said. He probably kissed her. They probably talked about nothing and everything—the kind of rambling, half-finished conversations you have when you're falling in love and words aren't quite enough to capture what you're feeling.

Two weeks isn't very long. But when you're twenty-two and twenty-one, two weeks can feel like a lifetime. It can feel like the start of something real.

Maybe they talked about the future. Maybe Cheryl mentioned going back to school in the fall, and Andy said he'd visit. Maybe they made plans for next weekend. Maybe they talked about nothing important at all, just filled the space between them with words because silence felt like too much distance.

Whatever they said, it was normal. It was theirs. It was the kind of conversation that happens a thousand times a night in a thousand parked cars across the country.

Until it wasn't.

---

The attack would have come fast. Violent. Without warning. 

A figure emerging from the darkness. A gun. A voice giving commands. Andy's instinct would have been to protect Cheryl. But you can't fight a gun. You can't fight someone who's done this before, who knows exactly how much fear and force it takes to control two people at once.

Rope. Hands bound behind backs. Panic rising in their throats. The realization—slow and then all at once—that this wasn't a robbery. This was something worse.

Cheryl would have been terrified. So would Andy. But they probably tried to stay calm. Tried to comply. Tried to believe that if they just did what he said, they'd be okay.

People always think that. They always think if they cooperate, if they don't fight back, the person with the gun will let them go. Most of the time, they're right. Most of the time, the person with the gun just wants money or a car or to feel powerful for a minute. Most of the time, you survive.

But not this time.

---

Somewhere in those woods, tied to a tree with his hands behind his back, Andy Atkinson died first. His throat was slashed so deeply that his head was nearly severed. The medical examiner would later note the force required to make wounds like that. The rage. The brutality.

He died knowing what was happening to Cheryl. Or maybe he didn't. Maybe he was already gone by then. Maybe that was a mercy.

Cheryl fought. The evidence would show that. Blood in the car. Displaced floor mats. Signs of a struggle. She fought as hard as she could. But she was twenty-two years old and a hundred and twenty pounds, and the man attacking her was bigger, stronger, armed, and had done this before.

She was sexually assaulted. Her hands were bound. Her throat was cut three times—ragged, violent slashes. Her body was left face-down in the dirt, partially covered with wooden fence slats in what police would later describe as "an attempt to conceal the body."

She died alone in the dark, a hundred and fifty yards from the boy she'd been falling in love with.

---

The next day, Cheryl didn't show up for work. Her family filed a missing person's report at 5:00 p.m. 

That evening, a security guard making his rounds noticed a white Honda Civic parked at the end of the cul-de-sac on Enclave Parkway. It had been there for hours. He walked up, looked inside, saw a purse on the passenger-side floorboard. He opened it. Inside was Cheryl Henry's ID.

By midnight, police had found her body.

By morning, they had found Andy's.

---

Shane Henry would never forget kissing her sister goodbye that night. She would never forget the way Cheryl smiled, the way Andy had his arm around her waist, the way they looked at each other like the world was full of light.

Garland Atkinson Sr. would never forget the phone call that told him his son—his best friend—was dead. He couldn't bring himself to go to the morgue. He couldn't look. "You never imagine something that drastic, that horrifying, happening to someone you love," he would later say.

The families would spend the next thirty-six years waiting for justice. Cheryl's mother, Barbara, would die in 2024 without ever seeing an arrest. So would Andy's father, Garland. They waited their whole lives and never got answers.

Shane kept her sister's memory alive. She gave interviews. She attended press conferences. She refused to let Cheryl become just another unsolved case, just another statistic, just another dead girl whose name people forgot.

Because Cheryl wasn't just a victim. She was a daughter. A sister. A friend. A young woman with dreams and a future and a life that mattered.

And Andy wasn't just a body tied to a tree. He was someone's son. Someone's best friend. A young man who moved halfway across the country to be closer to family, who worked hard, who made people smile.

They were people. Real people. They deserved better than what happened to them.

## CHAPTER 2: THE LOVERS LANE

The woods off Enclave Parkway weren't much to look at in the daylight—just scrub pine and cedar breaking through sandy Texas soil, the kind of half-wild space that developers hadn't gotten around to clearing yet. But at night, when Houston's humidity hung thick as a blanket and the city lights faded to a distant glow, the place transformed into something else entirely.

Every teenager in west Houston knew about it.

The cul-de-sac at the end of Enclave Round had become, by unspoken agreement, one of those places where young couples went when they wanted privacy. No streetlights. No houses. Just a dead-end circle of asphalt surrounded by trees, tucked away in what would someday be a business park but was, in the summer of 1990, gloriously undeveloped. 

They called it Lovers Lane.

It wasn't an official name—you wouldn't find it on any map—but the label had stuck the way these things do. Word passed from friend to friend, older sibling to younger, until it became part of the local geography of teenage romance. The spot had a reputation: secluded enough to feel private, close enough to civilization to feel safe. Parents might not approve, but they'd probably done something similar in their own youth, at their own version of Lovers Lane.

The location had its advantages. Far enough from the main roads that headlights wouldn't sweep past every few minutes. Close enough to neighborhoods that help was theoretically nearby if needed. The surrounding woods created a natural buffer, a sense of being somewhere separate from the ordinary world. Couples parked there to talk, to kiss, to escape the watchful eyes of family and friends. It was a place that belonged to young love, to the delicious feeling of being alone together.

Nobody thought much about the danger. This was Houston, sure, and Houston had its rough edges, but this wasn't the rough part. This was a transitional zone between suburbs and future development, populated mainly by pine trees and the occasional security guard making rounds through empty lots. The teenagers who parked there felt safe enough. After all, everyone came here. If it were dangerous, people would know.

On the night of August 22, 1990, the air was thick and hot even after sunset, the kind of oppressive heat that makes clothes stick to skin and turns cars into mobile saunas until the AC kicks in. The week had been muggy even by Houston standards—temperatures in the nineties during the day, barely cooling off at night. 

For Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson, the evening had started at Bayou Mama's, a nightclub on Westheimer where the music was loud and the drinks were cold. Cheryl's younger sister Shane had joined them, watching with bemused affection as the couple orbited each other with the kind of obvious infatuation that makes everyone else in the room disappear. They'd known each other only about two weeks, but it was that intoxicating early stage where everything feels electric.

Shane had good-naturedly teased them—"Get a room already!"—before saying goodnight around 11:30 PM. She kissed her sister goodbye, told her she loved her. It was what they always did, routine as breathing. The last time Shane would ever see Cheryl alive.

After Shane left, Cheryl and Andy stayed a while longer before deciding to leave. They climbed into Andy's white Honda Civic, the key turning in the ignition, the cassette deck clicking as Andy pushed in a tape. The music started, something from a mix tape, the soundtrack to a summer night.

The drive to Enclave Parkway wasn't long. West through the city, past strip malls and gas stations still open, past neighborhoods going dark for the night. They would have known where they were going—at 22 and 21, both knew Houston's social geography. Lovers Lane was the kind of place you heard about, the kind of place that sounded romantic in a slightly rebellious way.

The Honda's headlights swept the empty street as they turned onto Enclave Round, following the curve to where the pavement ended. No other cars. No signs of life. The woods pressed in on three sides, dark and dense. They parked facing the trees, the engine ticking as it cooled.

Andy reached for the controls and reclined the seats. The tape kept playing. Through the windshield, the night was absolute—no moon visible through the cloud cover, no ambient light penetrating this far. The only illumination came from inside the car itself, the faint glow of the dashboard, maybe the orange ember of a cigarette.

They talked. They kissed. Two people wrapped up in each other, oblivious to everything outside their small bubble of intimacy. The windows fogged slightly from their breath and body heat. Time passed unmeasured, irrelevant.

They never heard him coming.

Later, investigators would piece together fragments of what happened next, reading the scene like a text written in violence. But in the moment, there was only sudden terror—a figure emerging from the darkness, a weapon, commands barked with the authority of someone used to being obeyed.

Parrott had been hiding in the woods, or perhaps he'd arrived after them, parking elsewhere and approaching on foot. He may have been wearing something that resembled a uniform—he'd done that before, playing cop, using the authority of a badge to control people. He had a gun. He had rope.

The ambush was swift and overwhelming. One moment, two people in love. The next, two people in mortal danger.

Andy was pulled from the car first, hands yanked behind his back, rope biting into his wrists. Cheryl's screams—if she screamed—went nowhere, absorbed by the thick night air and the trees. Parrolt was methodical, experienced. This wasn't improvisation. He'd thought about this, prepared for this.

The gun kept them compliant. The rope kept them bound. And the darkness kept them hidden.

He forced Andy to walk, barefoot or stumbling, away from the car and into the trees. Fifty yards. A hundred. Deeper into the woods where the ground was uneven and littered with fallen branches. Far enough that someone passing by the parked car wouldn't immediately see what lay beyond.

There was a pine tree, thick enough to serve his purpose. Andy was positioned against it, back to the trunk, hands still bound behind him. Another length of rope went around his neck and around the tree, holding him in place, making movement impossible.

Andy was twenty-one years old. He'd moved to Houston that year to be closer to his father's side of the family, working at Gold's Gym, sometimes helping out at his dad's nightclub. He was falling for a girl he'd met just two weeks ago. He had plans.

None of that mattered now.

Back at the car, Cheryl's terror must have been absolute. She could hear what was happening to Andy, or she couldn't hear anything, which might have been worse. She was bound, helpless. The man with the gun had total control.

What happened next, the specific sequence of violation and violence, is documented in forensic reports and autopsy files. It doesn't need to be described in detail here. What matters is this: Cheryl Henry was assaulted with a cruelty that speaks to the darkest capacities of human beings. She was raped. She was bound. She was dragged or forced to walk through the woods in the opposite direction from where Andy had been taken, away from the car, away from the road, away from any hope of help.

The attack on her body was both sexual and homicidal, an assertion of absolute power. When it was over, or perhaps while it was still ongoing, she was positioned against another tree, hands bound behind her back, rope around her neck securing her to the trunk.

And then the knife.

The medical examiner would later describe three ragged gashes across her throat. The cuts were deep, brutal, inflicted with force. She would have bled out quickly, consciousness fading as shock set in, the pine tree at her back the last thing she felt.

Andy, bound to his own tree somewhere in the dark, may have heard her die. Or perhaps he was already dead himself, his throat cut with such violence that his head was nearly severed from his body. The killer used the same method on both of them—total, savage butchery disguised as efficiency.

After they were dead, he lingered.

This wasn't a panicked flight. He took time to arrange things. Golf balls from Andy's car were placed in the woods, positioned in a way that seemed deliberate, as if marking a trail or drawing attention. A twenty-dollar bill was left nearby—investigators would later note this detail with confusion. Why leave money? Was it symbolic? Payment for something? An accident?

Cheryl's body was covered, partially, with cedar fence slats—wooden boards that had no business being in those woods, that had been brought or found and placed over her as if concealing her mattered, as if some gesture toward propriety could coexist with what he'd just done.

Then he left.

The Honda sat in the cul-de-sac, seats still reclined, key in the ignition, cassette tape silent now. Cheryl's purse was on the passenger-side floor. A rubber floor mat was displaced, shoved aside during the struggle. Blood on the inside of the driver's door near the handle—not much, but enough.

The woods were silent again. The bodies lay waiting to be found, bound to trees like some grotesque parody of a ritual, the rope holding them upright even in death.

By the time the sun came up on August 23, 1990, Floyd William Parrott was somewhere else, living his life, carrying the secret of what he'd done. The DNA he'd left behind—semen collected during Cheryl's autopsy—would sit in evidence storage, a genetic fingerprint waiting for technology to catch up.

It would take thirty-six years.

## CHAPTER 3: THE DISCOVERY

Dawn came slowly on August 23, 1990, the sun rising into air already thick with humidity. The morning joggers and dog walkers who might normally pass through the area saw nothing unusual—Enclave Parkway wasn't exactly a destination, just another undeveloped stretch of land in west Houston's sprawling landscape.

But Cheryl Henry didn't show up for work that day.

By afternoon, her family knew something was deeply wrong. Cheryl was responsible, reliable. She didn't just disappear. Her mother Barbara and sister Shane began calling around, checking with friends, trying to piece together the previous night. The last confirmed sighting: Bayou Mama's nightclub, around 11:30 PM, Cheryl and Andy leaving together in Andy's white Honda.

At 5:00 PM, the family filed a missing persons report with Houston Police.

While they waited and worried, a security guard was making his routine rounds through the business park area near Enclave Round. His job was straightforward: check the empty lots, make sure nobody was dumping trash or vandalizing property, keep an eye out for anything unusual. Most nights were uneventful.

This evening was different.

At the end of the cul-de-sac, tucked against the tree line, was a white Honda Civic. It had been there for hours—since the night before at least—which struck the guard as odd. Kids sometimes parked there briefly, but they didn't abandon cars. He approached on foot.

The vehicle was locked but intact. No signs of damage. Peering through the windows, he could see the interior: seats reclined, a purse on the passenger-side floor. He noted the license plate and called it in. When police ran the plates, they came back to Andrew Atkinson. When they looked in the purse, they found Cheryl Henry's ID.

The families' worst fears crystallized into terrible certainty.

Houston Police arrived in force as daylight faded. They cordoned off the area, brought in cadaver dogs, called for helicopter support with spotlights. Officers on horseback picked their way through the scrubby woods. The search was urgent now—if the couple had been carjacked or hurt, they might still be nearby, might still need help.

Family members gathered at the scene, held back by yellow tape, watching the flashlight beams sweep through the darkness, hearing the dogs bark, the helicopter rotors thumping overhead. Shane Henry stood with her mother and stepfather, praying for the best, bracing for the worst.

The woods weren't large, but they were dense enough to hide secrets. Pine trees grew close together, undergrowth thick at ground level. The searchers moved methodically, calling out to each other, marking grid sections as clear.

It was one of the officers with a flashlight who found her first.

Cheryl's body was approximately 150 yards from the car, deeper in the woods than anyone would casually wander. She was face down, partially concealed under wooden boards—cedar fence slats that someone had placed over her, a halfhearted attempt at covering what could never truly be hidden. Her hands were bound behind her back with coarse rope. Her clothes were gone. Three ragged wounds gaped across her throat.

The officer who discovered her stopped, processed what he was seeing, and immediately radioed for homicide detectives. This wasn't a missing persons case anymore.

The news rippled through the search team: body found, female, deceased. On the perimeter, the families felt the shift in energy even before anyone said the words. You could see it in the way the police moved, hear it in the change in radio chatter. Shane Henry's legs nearly gave out.

But there were two missing people. Where was Andy?

The search intensified, more urgent now, though everyone knew that if Cheryl was dead, Andy almost certainly was too. The dogs ranged farther. More officers pushed into the brush.

It took until morning—August 24—to find him.

Andy Atkinson's body was approximately 50 to 200 yards from Cheryl's, in a different section of woods, as if the killer had deliberately separated them. He was tied to a pine tree in a seated position, back against the trunk, hands bound behind him with the same type of hemp rope. Another rope circled his neck and the tree, holding him upright, suspended between sitting and kneeling. His clothes were intact—jeans, shirt, shoes.

## CHAPTER 4: "The Phantom Suspect"

The morning after the bodies were discovered, Detective John "Billy" Belk stood at the edge of the cul-de-sac on Enclave Parkway and watched the sun rise over a crime scene that made no sense. Twenty-six years on the force, and he'd never seen anything like it.

Items from the car—golf balls, of all things—arranged like breadcrumbs pointing toward her body. A twenty-dollar bill left behind. And a cassette tape still in the deck.

The question was: which?

### The Investigation Begins

Houston Police threw everything at the case. Patrol officers canvassed the area. Helicopters swept the adjacent woods. In 1990, DNA profiling was still in its infancy. The Houston Police Department crime lab didn't have the technology to process it. Belk got special permission to send the sample to Baylor College of Medicine, where researchers were pioneering forensic DNA analysis.

Weeks later, the results came back: a full DNA profile. Male. Unknown subject.

The profile was entered into CODIS—the FBI's Combined DNA Index System, a national database that had only recently been established. If the killer was already in the system, or if he committed another crime and left DNA behind, there would be a match.

The search came back empty.

### The Tip

On September 3, 1990—less than two weeks after the murders—an anonymous tip came into the Houston Police Department.

A caller named Floyd William Parrott as a possible suspect.

What is known is this: Floyd Parrott was not DNA tested in 1990. His name did not appear on the list of two dozen suspects who would eventually be cleared through DNA comparison. He was not arrested. He was not brought in for questioning.

The tip, it seemed, fell through the cracks.

### Who Was Floyd William Parrott?

If detectives had dug deeper, they would have found a man with a disturbing history.

Floyd William Parrott, twenty-eight years old in August 1990, had been arrested four times between 1988 and July 1990—all for impersonating a peace officer.

In May 1990, just three months before the murders, Parrott was arrested again for impersonating a deputy constable for Precincts 4 and 5. He told women he was working as a security guard. He was released on bond.

Then, in July 1990—five weeks before Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson were murdered—Parrott was arrested on two counts of impersonating a cop. He was out on bond when the murders occurred.

Parrott said nothing.

"We know you worked near the crime scene. We know you were arrested multiple times for impersonating a cop. We know you carried weapons. We know you targeted women in the adult entertainment industry. And we know you were out on bond the night Cheryl and Andy were murdered."

Parrott's jaw tightened. "I want a lawyer."

Burrow nodded. "That's your right."

The interview was over.

"Thank you for never giving up. Thank you for keeping Cheryl and Andy's memory alive. This is for them."

Shane Henry stood. Tears streamed down her face. She approached the podium and took the microphone.

"Cheryl was my sister. She was my best friend. When she was killed, part of me died too. For thirty-six years, I've lived with the pain of not knowing who did this. Not knowing if he'd ever be caught. Not knowing if Cheryl and Andy would ever get justice."

Her voice cracked.

"My mother—Cheryl's mother—passed away in 2024. She never got to see this day. Andy's father passed away the same year. They waited their entire lives for answers and died without them. That breaks my heart."

"But today, I can finally say: we know who did this. We know who took Cheryl and Andy from us. And he's going to pay."

The room erupted in applause.

Floyd William Parrott would await trial in a cell.

No bond. No freedom. No escape.

## CHAPTER 10: Justice at Last

**April 1, 2026**

Houston woke up to a changed reality. For the first time in thirty-six years, the Lovers Lane Murders had a suspect. Not a theory. Not a sketch. A living, breathing human being sitting in a jail cell, awaiting trial.

The city exhaled.

News coverage was relentless. Every major outlet ran stories. Cable news picked it up. Podcasts released emergency episodes. True crime communities on Reddit, Websleuths, and Facebook erupted in discussion.

Some were relieved: *Finally. Justice.*

Others were angry: *Why did it take so long?*

Still others were terrified: *How many others did he hurt?*

Shane Henry—Cheryl's sister—gave interview after interview. She was exhausted but determined. Every time she spoke, she made sure to say Cheryl and Andy's names. She refused to let them be reduced to "victims" or "the couple." They were people. They were loved. They mattered.

"Cheryl was brilliant," Shane said on KPRC. "She was funny, she was kind, she was *alive*. Andy was the same. They had their whole lives ahead of them. And someone took that away. For thirty-six years, we didn't know who. Now we do. And we're going to make sure he never hurts anyone again."

Parrott appeared via video link for his arraignment. He wore a standard orange jumpsuit. His hair was gray and unkempt. His face was expressionless.

Parrott's court-appointed attorney spoke for him: "Not guilty, Your Honor."

Parrott said nothing. The screen went black.

**April 5, 2026 — Harris County District Attorney's Office**

Behind closed doors, prosecutors were building their case. Lead attorney Samantha Knecht and DA Sean Teare assembled the evidence:

1. **DNA:** The cornerstone. Semen from Cheryl Henry matched Parrott's profile with a probability of one in trillions. The match was confirmed by two independent labs.

2. **June 1990 rape:** DNA from that case also matched Parrott. The victim—an exotic dancer who once worked for Andy Atkinson's father—had described her attacker wearing uniform-style clothing and carrying a long-barreled revolver. Parrott owned such a weapon.

3. **1996 sexual assault:** Parrott admitted sexual contact. DNA was collected. That DNA matched the 1990 cases.

4. **Criminal history:** Decades of impersonating law enforcement. Using fake authority to intimidate, control, and extort. A clear pattern of predatory behavior.

5. **Geographic links:** Parrott's workplace in 1988 was one mile from the murder scene. He worked in nightclubs, giving him access to potential victims in the adult entertainment industry.

6. **Opportunity:** Parrott was not incarcerated during any of the three DNA-linked incidents. He was free, in Houston, and had means and opportunity.

Knecht sat back. "This is solid. Defense will try to attack the DNA—argue chain of custody, contamination, lab error. But we have *fresh* DNA from 2026 trash collection. That's ironclad."

Teare nodded. "And we have three separate incidents—three victims, same DNA. That's not coincidence. That's a pattern."

He paused.

"We're seeking the death penalty."

## EPILOGUE

**April 2026**

The case is still unfolding. Floyd William Parrott sits in a cell, awaiting trial. The evidence against him is overwhelming—DNA, criminal history, geographic links, victimology. Prosecutors are confident. Defense attorneys are scrambling.

But the trial will take months. Maybe a year. The justice system is slow, especially in capital cases.

Meanwhile, the families wait. Again.

They've been waiting for thirty-six years. They can wait a little longer.

---

Shane Henry stands at the spot where Cheryl's body was found. It's not a crime scene anymore. It's a corporate park—clean, modern, bustling with workers. The woods are gone. The darkness is gone.

But Shane remembers.

"This is where my sister died," she says quietly. "This is where someone decided her life didn't matter. But he was wrong. Her life *did* matter. It still matters. And now, finally, the world knows it."

---

Across town, retired Detective Billy Belk sits in his living room. On the coffee table is a worn case file—his personal copy, kept after retirement. He's carried it for seventeen years. Now, finally, he can close it.

"I promised them," he says. "I promised Cheryl's family, Andy's family, everyone who cared—I promised this case would clear. And it did."

He smiles, just a little.

"Took thirty-six years. But justice doesn't have an expiration date."

---

Some of those questions have answers. Others may never.

But what *is* clear is this:

**Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson were young, in love, and full of dreams.**

**On August 22, 1990, someone took all of that away.**

**Thirty-six years later, science and one determined sergeant gave them justice.**

**But it will never bring them back.**
