# CHAPTER 1: A Normal Friday

## April 1, 1988 — Fort Wayne, Indiana

April Marie Tinsley woke up to sunshine streaming through her bedroom window. Eight years old, gap-toothed smile, dark brown hair that her mother would later describe as "always a little messy, no matter how many times I brushed it." She had a favorite stuffed rabbit named Mr. Hops that she'd had since she was three, worn thin from being dragged everywhere—to the grocery store, to church, to sleepovers at her grandmother's house.

It was Good Friday. Schools let out early.

April didn't care much about the religious significance of the day. What mattered was the extra hour of freedom, the promise of the weekend stretching ahead like an open field. She was a third-grader at Fairfield Elementary School, a cheerful brick building where she sang in the children's choir and had just learned how to do long division, though she still had to count on her fingers sometimes when no one was looking.

Her teacher, Mrs. Patterson, had handed out pastel-colored eggs filled with candy that morning. April had gotten a yellow one. Yellow was her favorite color. Not sunshine yellow—more like butter yellow, the soft kind that didn't hurt your eyes. Her umbrella was yellow too, decorated with cartoon characters she'd picked out herself at the Dollar Store on South Calhoun Street. She'd saved her allowance for three weeks to buy it.

At home, the Tinsley house on West Williams Street smelled like Friday: her mother's tuna casserole baking in the oven, the faint scent of laundry detergent from the clothes hanging on the line in the backyard. The neighborhood was the kind where everyone knew everyone. Chain-link fences divided small yards. Kids rode bikes in the street without helmets. Doors stayed unlocked.

April's baby brother, Paul, was napping when she got home just after noon. She tiptoed past his room—he was only an infant, and waking him meant her mother would be busy for the next hour, which meant no permission to go outside.

"Mom, can I go to Katie's?" April asked, already pulling on her sneakers by the front door.

Janet Tinsley looked up from folding laundry. Her daughter was vibrating with that specific energy eight-year-olds have on Friday afternoons when the weather is perfect and school is finally, blessedly over.

"Which Katie? Katie Bishop or Katie Hobbs?"

"Katie Bishop. And Melissa's there too. We're gonna play Barbies."

Janet knew Katie Bishop's house. Three blocks. Williams Street to Lafayette, then over to Hoagland. April had walked it dozens of times.

"Be home by five," Janet said. "And take your umbrella. Weather said rain this afternoon."

April grabbed the yellow umbrella from the hook by the door, even though the sky was clear. Her mother was always worried about rain. Or strangers. Or cars going too fast. Or a hundred other things that seemed silly when you were eight and the whole world felt safe.

"Love you!" April called, already halfway out the door.

"Love you too, baby."

The screen door slammed shut.

---

## 3:00 PM

April loved Katie Bishop's house because Katie's mom let them have as many juice boxes as they wanted, and because Katie had the Barbie Dream House—the big one with the elevator that actually worked. April only had three Barbies at home, all hand-me-downs from her cousin, but Katie had at least ten, plus Ken, plus the pink convertible.

They played for over an hour. Melissa Hobbs showed up around 2:30, and the three of them sprawled across Katie's bedroom floor, narrating elaborate soap operas where Barbie was a veterinarian and Ken was a race car driver and they lived in a house with an elevator because obviously that's what all grown-ups had.

Then Katie's mom called up the stairs: "Girls, we're going to Melissa's house for a bit. You coming, April?"

April looked around. She'd left her umbrella downstairs. The yellow one with the cartoons.

"I'll catch up," she said. "I just gotta grab my umbrella."

The three of them spilled out of Katie's house together, sunlight and laughter and the invincibility of childhood. Melissa's house was four more blocks in the opposite direction. April would go back for the umbrella, then cut through the alley to Melissa's. Easy.

"Don't take forever!" Katie called over her shoulder.

April grinned. "I won't!"

She watched her friends disappear around the corner, their voices fading into the afternoon hum of Fort Wayne: lawnmowers buzzing, someone's radio playing from an open window, a dog barking three yards over.

April turned back toward Katie's front door. Her umbrella was just inside, propped against the wall in the entryway where she'd left it. It would take thirty seconds to grab it.

But then she remembered: Katie's mom had locked the door when they left. April had heard the deadbolt click.

No big deal. She'd just go home, grab her own umbrella (she had a backup, a plain blue one, not as cool but it worked), and then head to Melissa's. Home was three blocks. She'd done it a hundred times. She could walk it with her eyes closed.

April started down the sidewalk. Williams Street was quiet. Most people were still at work. A few cars passed, but no one she recognized. The spring air smelled like cut grass and someone grilling burgers even though it was only three in the afternoon.

She kicked a pebble along the sidewalk as she walked, counting her steps. One-two-three-four. She liked even numbers. Eight was her favorite because that's how old she was. She wondered if nine would be her favorite next year, or if eight would always be the best.

There was a lot April wondered about. She wondered if she'd be taller than Melissa by fifth grade. She wondered if Mr. Hops would eventually fall apart completely, and if her mom could sew him back together one more time. She wondered what it would be like to be a grown-up, to have a job, to drive a car, to not have to ask permission to go to your friend's house.

She wondered if there would be tuna casserole left when she got home, or if her dad would have eaten it all by the time he got back from work.

April didn't wonder if she was safe. That thought didn't occur to her. Why would it? This was her neighborhood. These were her streets. She knew every crack in the sidewalk, every house with a mean dog, every yard where you could cut through to save time.

She was three blocks from home.

Three blocks from her mother folding laundry.

Three blocks from her baby brother asleep in his crib.

Three blocks from the tuna casserole in the oven.

Three blocks from everything normal and boring and safe.

## What April Loved

Before we go further, you should know a few things about April Marie Tinsley. Not April the victim. Not April the headline. Just April.

She loved singing. Her voice wasn't particularly good—a little off-key, a little too loud—but she sang anyway, belting out hymns in the children's choir at Faith United Methodist Church every Sunday. Her favorite was "Jesus Loves Me" because it was short and she could remember all the words.

She loved animals. There was a stray cat in the neighborhood, an orange tabby with a torn ear, and April would sneak it bits of her lunch—bologna sandwiches, cheese crackers, once an entire hard-boiled egg. Her mother said they couldn't afford a pet, but April was working on her. Persistence, she'd learned, was key.

She loved her family. Her dad, Michael, worked long hours, but when he was home, he'd toss her up in the air until she shrieked with laughter. Her mom, Janet, was strict about bedtime and vegetables, but she also read April stories every night, doing all the voices, making the monsters sound scary and the princesses sound brave.

And Paul. Baby Paul, who couldn't do much yet except cry and sleep and grab April's finger with his tiny fist. April had been an only child for seven years, and she'd liked it that way, but now she couldn't imagine life without him. She was going to teach him everything when he got older: how to ride a bike, how to climb the big tree in the backyard, how to sneak cookies from the jar on the top shelf.

She loved her friends. Katie and Melissa, but also Amanda and Sarah and Tommy from down the street. They played tag until the streetlights came on, built forts out of cardboard boxes, traded stickers and friendship bracelets and secrets.

She loved her neighborhood. The corner store where Mr. Kowalski would give her a free piece of bubble gum if she helped him sweep the front step. The library on Fairfield Avenue where Mrs. Chen knew her by name and always saved the new *Baby-Sitters Club* books for her. The park with the good swings, the ones that went high enough to make your stomach flip.

She loved normal things. Macaroni and cheese. Saturday morning cartoons. The smell of her mother's perfume. The sound of rain on the roof. The feeling of clean sheets. The way the sun looked when it came through the trees in the backyard, all gold and dappled.

April Marie Tinsley loved her life.

She had no reason not to.

---

## 3:15 PM

April was halfway home when she saw the car.

It was nothing remarkable. Just a car, slowing down as it approached her. She kept walking. Cars slowed down all the time. Maybe someone was looking for an address. Maybe someone was lost.

The car stopped.

April kept walking. Her mother had taught her the rules: Don't talk to strangers. Don't take candy from anyone you don't know. If someone asks for help, find an adult—it's not your job.

But April also lived in a world where neighbors helped neighbors, where adults were generally trustworthy, where nothing bad had ever happened on Williams Street.

She didn't run.

She should have run.

## What Came Next

There are details about what happened next—details recovered from a confession given thirty years later, details assembled from forensic evidence, details too horrific to include in this chapter because this chapter is about April when she was still April, not yet a case file, not yet a cold case, not yet a cautionary tale.

What matters is this: April Marie Tinsley, eight years old, third grade, gap-toothed smile, lover of yellow umbrellas and stray cats and *Baby-Sitters Club* books, disappeared.

By 6:00 PM, when she hadn't come home, her mother called the police.

By midnight, the entire neighborhood was searching.

By morning, hope was fading.

Three days later, a jogger found her body in a ditch twenty miles away.

---

## Before the End

But that's not this chapter. This chapter is about the before. This chapter is about the normal Friday, the good Friday, the Friday that should have been unremarkable and safe and boring.

This chapter is about an eight-year-old girl who loved her life and had no idea it was about to end.

This chapter is about the moment before everything changed.

---

April started walking. Three blocks to Katie's house. She'd done it a hundred times before.

This would be the last.
