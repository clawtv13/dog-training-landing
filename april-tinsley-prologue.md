# PROLOGUE: The Girl in the Ditch

The farmer saw the umbrella first.

A child's umbrella, bright yellow with cartoon characters, lying in the tall grass beside County Line Road. He almost didn't stop. His truck rolled past another fifty yards before his foot found the brake. Something about that umbrella—the wrongness of it—pulled at him. No kid would leave such a thing behind. Not willingly.

He put the truck in park and walked back.

The morning was gray, that particular shade of Indiana April where winter hadn't quite loosened its grip. Clouds pressed low over the fields, and mist hung in the ditches like breath. County Road 68 was the kind of road you took when you wanted to avoid people, when you had work to do and no time for traffic. The kind of road where you could drive for miles and see nothing but corn stubble and fence posts.

The umbrella lay ten feet from the road's edge, half-hidden in last year's grass. As he got closer, he saw it wasn't alone.

A shoe. Small. Pink. The kind a little girl would wear.

His throat went dry.

He took another step, and that's when he saw her.

She lay in the ditch, partially concealed by the grass, her small body still and pale against the brown earth. She wore a red windbreaker—bright against the dull gray morning—and one bare foot stuck out from beneath her. The other foot still had its mate, that second pink shoe, tied carefully with its white laces.

The farmer didn't go closer. Didn't need to. He could see enough from where he stood—the wrong angle of her neck, the unnatural stillness. He backed away slowly, as if sudden movement might shatter something, then turned and ran for his truck.

His hands shook as he reached for the CB radio.

---

By the time the police arrived, word had already begun to spread through the small communities that dotted DeKalb County. Spencerville. Grabill. The rural townships where everyone knew everyone else, where children still played outside without supervision because nothing bad ever happened here.

Fort Wayne Police had been searching for her since Friday. Three days. Seventy-two hours that stretched like years for her mother, who had reported her missing when she didn't come home for dinner. The little girl had only been walking to a friend's house three blocks away. Just three blocks, to retrieve the umbrella she'd left behind.

Now they'd found the umbrella. And they'd found her.

Eight years old. April Marie Tinsley. Second grade at Fairfield Elementary. Brown hair. Brown eyes. Four feet tall and sixty pounds. She liked to sing in the church choir. She had a baby brother at home who was too young to understand why his sister wasn't coming back.

The crime scene unit worked through the morning, methodical and silent. They photographed the umbrella, the shoe, the place where the grass was flattened. They measured distances. They collected evidence—fibers, soil samples, and something else, something that would sit in a police locker for thirty years waiting for technology to catch up with justice.

The jogger who'd found her—a local farmer checking his fence line—would never quite shake the image. Decades later, he'd still see that yellow umbrella in his dreams. The cartoon characters grinning up at the gray sky. The wrongness of something so cheerful lying beside something so terrible.

---

In Fort Wayne, April's mother sat in her living room surrounded by police officers and family members, waiting for news she already knew was coming. A mother knows. She'd known Friday night when April didn't come home. She'd known when the search stretched through Saturday and Sunday. She'd known when she saw the detectives' faces as they climbed the steps to her front door that Monday morning.

But knowing doesn't make it real. Doesn't make it bearable.

The detective who told her tried to be gentle. Used words like "found" and "passed" instead of words like "murdered" and "dead." But the words didn't matter. The only word that mattered was "April," and her daughter's name would never sound the same again.

---

The investigation moved fast at first. Tips poured in. Witnesses who'd seen a blue pickup truck. A composite sketch of a man spotted near the abduction site. DNA evidence collected from April's body—cutting-edge forensic science for 1988, though the technology couldn't yet tell them who it belonged to.

They had five suspects within weeks. Tested them all. None matched.

The trail went cold.

Then, two years later, someone found a message scrawled in crayon on a barn not far from where April's body had been discovered:

*I kill 8 year old April Marie Tisley did you find the other shoe haha I will kill agin*

The misspellings. The mocking laughter. The promise of more death. The message hung over Fort Wayne like smoke, choking the hope that this had been a one-time thing, a tragic accident of opportunity and horror.

No more children died. But the city's innocence did.

Parents who'd let their children walk to school alone started driving them. Yards that had been full of kids playing after dinner emptied. The neighborhood where April had lived—a place where people used to leave their doors unlocked—became a grid of watchful eyes and locked windows.

Sixteen years passed. The case files gathered dust. April's mother marked each birthday her daughter would never have. The detective who'd worked the case retired, taking the failure with him like a stone in his pocket.

Then, in 2004, the messages started again.

Four houses in Fort Wayne. Four families. Four little girls who came home to find notes tucked into plastic bags on their bicycles:

*Hi Honey I Been watching you I am the same person that kinapped an Rape an kill Aproil Tinsely you are my next vitem*

The notes came with Polaroid photographs. Used condoms. The DNA matched the 1988 evidence. The killer was still there. Still watching. Still wanting them to know.

But who? The police had the DNA. They had the handwriting. They had everything except a name.

---

On a July morning in 2018—thirty years and three months after April Tinsley disappeared—two Fort Wayne detectives pulled into the Grabill Mobile Home Park. Lot #4. They'd been watching the trailer for a week, waiting for the right moment. Waiting to be sure.

The man who answered the door was fifty-nine years old. Gray hair. Glasses. The kind of neighbor who kept to himself. The kind of man you'd pass in the grocery store and never think twice about.

"Do you know why we're here?" the detective asked.

The man in the doorway stood silent for a moment, his face unreadable. Then, quietly, he spoke two words:

"April Tinsley."

He knew. He'd been waiting thirty years for them to knock on his door. And when they asked him what happened, he told them everything. The abduction. The rape. The strangulation. What he'd done to her body afterward. Where he'd left her. Why he'd taunted them.

He told them all of it without hesitation, without tears, without any indication that he understood the decades of grief he'd caused or the childhood he'd stolen. He recited the horror like a man reading a grocery list, clinical and detached.

It would be thirty years before anyone knew who had taken April Tinsley.

And when the truth finally came, it would prove that monsters don't always look like monsters.

Sometimes they're your neighbor.
