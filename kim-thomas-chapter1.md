# CHAPTER 1: The Activist

Kim Thomas Friedland's alarm didn't go off on July 27, 1990. It didn't need to.

Her ten-month-old daughter had developed an internal clock more reliable than any digital display—up at 5:47 AM, give or take three minutes. Kim heard the first rustling from the nursery down the hall and smiled into her pillow before opening her eyes. Friday morning. Ed had an early surgery. She'd have the whole day alone with the baby.

Some women would have dreaded that. Kim cherished it.

She padded down the carpeted hallway of their Cotswold home, the morning light just beginning to filter through the Carolina pines outside. Their daughter stood in her crib, gripping the rail with chubby fists, face splitting into a gummy grin the moment she saw her mother. Kim's heart did what it always did in these moments—melted completely.

"Good morning, sunshine," she whispered, lifting the warm weight of her daughter against her chest. The baby smelled like sleep and baby powder and something indefinably sweet that Kim had tried and failed to capture in words when writing in her journal. "Ready to take on the patriarchy today?"

The baby gurgled. Close enough to yes.

## The Woman Before the Victim

At thirty-two years old, Kim Thomas Friedland was exactly where she wanted to be in life, which was rare enough to feel like a small miracle. She'd married her college sweetheart—Ed, solid and steady, a brilliant orthopedic surgeon with hands that could rebuild shattered bones and gentle enough to rock a colicky baby at three in the morning without complaint. They'd bought this house in Cotswold two years ago, a neighborhood of tree-lined streets and brick ranchers where people still waved to each other and kids rode bikes until the streetlights came on.

And she had her work. God, she had her work.

"Women's rights activist" was how the newspapers would describe her later, in that reductive way that obituaries flatten lives into resumé bullets. But Kim's activism wasn't some abstract political stance or weekend hobby. It was the architecture of her daily life, the lens through which she saw everything.

She organized. She agitated. She showed up.

Tuesday nights, she ran a support group for women leaving abusive relationships, held in the basement of a Methodist church on Central Avenue. Thursdays, she volunteered at Planned Parenthood, where she'd counsel terrified teenagers and hold their hands and never, ever judge. Weekends, she knocked on doors for local candidates who actually gave a damn about childcare and equal pay and domestic violence funding.

And in between all of it, she wrote. Letters to the editor. Op-eds for the *Charlotte Observer*. Furious, passionate screeds that her friends said made them want to march through the streets with signs. Ed teased her about her "manifesto phase," but he said it with pride. He framed one of her published pieces and hung it in his office at the hospital.

"My wife," he'd tell colleagues, "is going to change the world."

Kim didn't need to change the whole world. She'd settle for changing Charlotte.

## Friday Morning, July 27, 1990

The morning routine unfolded like choreography they'd perfected over ten months of sleepless trial and error.

Kim changed the baby's diaper while Ed showered. Ed made coffee while Kim nursed. They traded off bites of toast over the breakfast table while their daughter demolished a bowl of mashed banana with more enthusiasm than accuracy. Ed glanced at the clock—6:47 AM—and swore softly.

"Early case?" Kim asked.

"ACL reconstruction. Twenty-two-year-old college athlete." Ed gulped the last of his coffee and kissed the top of his daughter's head, then Kim's lips. "I'll be home for lunch if it goes fast. You good?"

"We're great." Kim wiped banana from the baby's eyebrow. "We're going to overthrow capitalism and demand universal childcare. Maybe hit the park if it's not too hot."

Ed grinned. "Try not to get arrested."

"No promises."

He grabbed his keys from the hook by the door, his medical bag from the hall closet. Paused. Came back and kissed Kim again, longer this time, his hand cupping her cheek.

"Love you," he said.

"Love you more."

"Impossible."

It was their thing. Stupid and sweet and theirs.

The door closed. Kim heard his car start in the driveway, the rumble of the engine fading as he backed out and headed toward the hospital. She looked at her daughter, banana-smeared and beaming, and felt that wash of contentment that still surprised her sometimes. She'd fought so hard for women to have choices, to not be defined solely by motherhood, to have careers and autonomy and voices.

And she'd chosen this. This sticky-handed, exhausting, transcendent chaos. She'd chosen it while also choosing her activism, her writing, her organizing. She'd chosen both. That was the whole goddamn point.

"Okay, kiddo," she said, lifting her daughter from the high chair. "Bath time. Then Mama's got some calls to make about the City Council hearing next week. You can chew on your blocks and look adorable. Team effort."

The baby grabbed a fistful of Kim's hair and yanked.

Kim laughed.

## The Neighborhood

Cotswold in 1990 was the kind of neighborhood where people didn't lock their doors during the day. Where women pushed strollers in packs down the sidewalks, chatting about whose kids had which summer cold. Where the biggest crime was usually someone letting their dog poop on a neighbor's lawn without cleaning it up.

Kim felt safe here. That was part of why they'd bought the house.

She had grown up in a tougher part of Charlotte, where her mother worked two jobs and her father was mostly absent and she learned early that the world was not designed for women like her mom—women who scraped and sacrificed and still couldn't get ahead. That was where Kim's fire had come from. The bone-deep knowledge that the system was rigged and someone needed to fight to unrig it.

But she wanted something different for her daughter. She wanted her to grow up in a place where she could ride her bike to a friend's house and play in sprinklers and not have to learn too young that the world was divided into people with power and people without it.

Kim would teach her daughter to fight. But first, she wanted her to feel safe.

Churchill Road was quiet that Friday morning. Most of the neighbors were already at work or running errands. Kim moved through her morning—cleaning up breakfast, starting a load of laundry, settling the baby on a blanket in the living room with her toys. She had a phone call scheduled for 9:30 AM with a woman from the League of Women Voters about coordinating a voter registration drive. She had notes to review, talking points to prep.

She made another cup of coffee. Picked up a ringing toy her daughter had flung across the room. Checked the clock. 8:43 AM.

Everything normal. Everything fine.

Everything about to shatter.

## The Activist's Legacy

Later—much, much later—people would ask what Kim had been working on in those final weeks of her life. Like maybe her activism had gotten her killed. Like maybe she'd pissed off the wrong man with her letters about domestic violence funding or her columns about reproductive rights.

The truth was simpler and more terrible.

Kim Thomas Friedland wasn't killed because she was an activist. She was killed because she was a woman, alone, in her home, and a predator decided she was prey.

That predator had a name: Marion Anthony Gales. He was twenty-eight years old. He lived in Grier Heights, a five-and-a-half-minute walk from Churchill Road. He had a criminal record that started when he was sixteen—when he shot and wounded a woman on Churchill Road, the very same street where Kim would die sixteen years later.

He had a pattern. Violence against women. Again and again and again.

And on July 27, 1990, that pattern would intersect with Kim Thomas Friedland's life in the worst possible way.

But she didn't know that yet.

At 8:43 AM, Kim was just a mother, playing with her baby. An activist who believed in a better world. A wife who loved her husband. A woman with plans for the afternoon and the weekend and the rest of her life.

At 8:43 AM, she was still just Kim.

## What We Lost

If Kim Thomas Friedland had lived, what would she have done?

Her friends think about this sometimes. She'd have raised her daughter, obviously—taught her to be fierce and kind and unafraid. She'd have kept organizing, kept writing, kept showing up at City Council meetings and candidate forums and grassroots fundraisers.

She'd have been there in the '90s when the Violence Against Women Act passed—probably would have organized a celebration, invited everyone she knew, given a speech about how it was a start but not enough, never enough until women could walk through the world without fear.

She'd have been there for her daughter's first day of kindergarten, her high school graduation, her wedding maybe. She'd have been a grandmother, possibly. She'd have aged into one of those women who terrify politicians and inspire college students and never, ever shut up about injustice.

She would have kept going. That's what Kim did. She kept going.

Instead, she got ten months of motherhood. Thirty-two years of life. One Friday morning in July that started like every other and ended like none ever should.

## The Final Hours

We don't know exactly when Marion Gales entered the house on Churchill Road.

We don't know if he knocked on the door and Kim answered, trusting and unsuspecting. We don't know if he forced his way in or talked his way in or simply walked through an unlocked door because this was Cotswold in 1990 and people didn't lock their doors during the day.

We don't know if Kim fought. God, we hope she fought.

We know she was handcuffed behind her back. We know her throat was slashed. We know she died on the floor of her own home while her baby girl slept or played somewhere nearby, unaware that the world was ending.

We know Marion Gales' DNA was on the rug beside her body—a fact that wouldn't be confirmed until February 2026, thirty-five years and seven months after he killed her. We know he walked out of that house and back to his own place, five and a half minutes on foot, and cleaned himself up and went about his day.

We know that at 10:30 AM, Dr. Ed Friedland would come home and find her.

By then, Kim Thomas had been dead for hours.

And the man who killed her had already disappeared into the neighborhood—back to his house, five minutes away on foot.
