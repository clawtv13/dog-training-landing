# BOOK #4 - CHAPTERS 4-5: KIM THOMAS

---

## CHAPTER 4: THE NEIGHBOR

The detective had seen the file a hundred times. Maybe more. The Kim Thomas murder had consumed his department for five years—a case that should've been solved in weeks, not years. A young mother handcuffed and slaughtered in her own home. A ten-month-old baby left crying in a crib. The kind of case that haunts you.

But in 1995, cold cases didn't have their own unit. They were what you did when the hot cases went cold, when the leads dried up and the phones stopped ringing. The Kim Thomas file sat in a metal cabinet in the basement of the Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police Department headquarters, gathering dust alongside dozens of other unsolved murders.

Detective Carl Morrison pulled the box down on a Tuesday afternoon. He'd been assigned to review the case—again—after Dr. Ed Friedland's lawyer made another call demanding answers. Five years since Kim's death. Four years of investigation that had gone exactly nowhere. One year since they'd charged her husband with murder, only to drop the charges when the evidence wouldn't hold.

Ed Friedland had become the case's ghost story. Charged but never tried. Cleared but never vindicated. The orthopedic surgeon who found his wife dead and spent the next year of his life under a cloud of suspicion so dark it cost him patients, friends, and any hope of moving forward.

Morrison didn't believe Ed did it. Never had. The DNA didn't match. The timeline didn't work. The motive didn't exist. But in 1990, when a woman was murdered in her home, you looked at the husband first. Sometimes you looked at him last, too, because there was nobody else to look at.

The file was thick. Witness statements, crime scene photos, forensic reports, interview transcripts. Morrison had read it all before. Multiple times. But cold cases weren't solved by reading the same reports over and over. They were solved by finding the thing everyone else had missed.

He started at the beginning. July 27, 1990. A Friday morning that started ordinary and ended in blood.

Ed Friedland left for work around 7:30 AM. Kim was home with their daughter, a baby who wouldn't remember her mother. The Cotswold neighborhood was quiet—middle-class homes, tree-lined streets, the kind of place where people knew their neighbors and felt safe leaving their doors unlocked.

No forced entry. That detail had haunted the investigation from day one. Whoever killed Kim Thomas didn't break in. They knocked. She opened the door. Either she knew them or they gave her a reason to trust them.

The handcuffs changed everything. Not police-issue. Purchased somewhere, brought to the scene with intent. This wasn't a crime of passion. It was planned. Controlled. The killer wanted her helpless.

Her throat had been slashed. A single, brutal cut. The weapon was never found. The killer took it with them, along with any hope of easy answers.

Morrison flipped through the crime scene photos, forcing himself to look. After five years, the images had lost some of their horror, but not all of it. Kim on the floor. Blood pooling on the rug. The crib visible in the background, a reminder of what her daughter had lost.

The DNA evidence was there but useless. In 1990, DNA testing required large samples and perfect conditions. The blood was Kim's. The touch DNA on the handcuffs was degraded. The skin cells on the rug beside her body were too mixed, too contaminated to process. They had the killer's DNA. They just couldn't do anything with it.

Morrison moved to the witness statements. Neighbors had heard nothing. Seen nothing. Kim's activism work had made her visible in the community—women's rights rallies, Democratic organizing, speeches at local events—but it hadn't made her enemies. Not the kind who would handcuff her and cut her throat.

Then he found it.

August 24, 1990. Nearly a month after the murder. A police report he'd never seen before, buried in the middle of the file.

Officer Jenkins had filed it after a routine canvass of the neighborhood. The report mentioned a name: Marion Anthony Gales. The context was thin—a neighbor had mentioned seeing Gales in the area around the time of the murder. Jenkins had noted it, filed it, and apparently nobody had followed up.

Morrison felt his pulse quicken. Marion Gales. The name meant nothing to him.

He ran the name through the system.

The screen filled with records. Assault. Robbery. Weapons charges. Drug offenses. Marion Anthony Gales had a criminal history stretching back to 1974, when he was sixteen years old.

Morrison kept reading. Then he stopped.

1974. Marion Gales, age sixteen, convicted of shooting a woman on Churchill Road.

Churchill Road. The same street where Kim Thomas had lived. The same street where she'd been murdered sixteen years later.

Morrison's hands went still on the keyboard. This couldn't be coincidence.

He pulled up Gales' address from 1990. Grier Heights. A neighborhood southeast of Cotswold. He opened Google Maps and typed in the addresses.

5.5 minutes. Walking distance from Marion Gales' apartment to Kim Thomas' front door was 5.5 minutes.

Morrison printed everything. Criminal records, addresses, case files. Then he started making calls.

---

Marion Gales' criminal history read like a roadmap to violence.

At sixteen, he'd shot a woman in broad daylight on Churchill Road. The victim survived but never fully recovered. Gales served time in juvenile detention and was released at eighteen with a record that should've been a warning.

Throughout his twenties, the arrests piled up. Bar fights. Armed robbery. Possession with intent to distribute. Every few years, Gales would disappear into the prison system for a year or two, then emerge and start the cycle again.

But it was the pattern that made Morrison's stomach turn. Violence against women. Over and over.

In 1985, a woman told police that Marion Gales had raped her. She described the attack in detail—how he'd forced his way into her apartment, overpowered her, threatened to kill her if she screamed. The detective who took her statement believed her. But the woman never pressed charges. She was terrified. Gales had told her he knew where she lived, where she worked, where her mother lived. She moved out of Charlotte two weeks later.

No charges filed. No arrest. Just another report in a system that couldn't protect her.

Then came July 1990. The same month Kim Thomas was murdered.

Morrison found the domestic violence report buried in Gales' file. July 14, 1990. Thirteen days before Kim's murder.

Annie Roseboro, Marion's sister, called 911 from a neighbor's house. She told the responding officer that her brother had beaten her during an argument about money. He'd hit her in the face, knocked her down, kicked her while she was on the floor. She had bruises on her ribs, a black eye, a split lip.

Did she want to press charges?

No. She was afraid. Marion had told her he'd kill her if she sent him back to jail.

The officer filed the report and left. Annie Roseboro refused medical treatment. Marion Gales faced no consequences.

Two weeks later, Kim Thomas was handcuffed and murdered in her home.

Morrison built a timeline on the whiteboard in the conference room. The pattern was unmistakable.

1974: Gales shoots woman on Churchill Road. Age 16.

1975-1989: In and out of jail. Multiple violent offenses.

1985: Rape allegation. Woman too afraid to press charges.

July 14, 1990: Beats his sister Annie.

July 27, 1990: Kim Thomas murdered.

August 24, 1990: Neighbor mentions seeing Gales in area. Report filed, not acted on.

Morrison stared at the board. Marion Gales was twenty-eight years old in 1990. He lived a five-minute walk from the victim. He had a history of violence against women. He had shot someone on the same street sixteen years earlier. He was violent enough to beat his own sister two weeks before the murder.

And nobody had talked to him.

---

The interview room at CMPD headquarters was designed to be uncomfortable. Hard chairs. Fluorescent lights. No windows. A table bolted to the floor. Marion Gales sat across from Morrison, looking bored.

He was thirty-three now, five years older than when Kim Thomas died. Prison had aged him—tattoos, scars, a hardness around the eyes that came from too many fights in too many cells. He'd been picked up on a parole violation three days earlier and was waiting to be transferred back to the prison system when Morrison requested the interview.

"You know why you're here?" Morrison asked.

Gales shrugged. "You tell me."

"Kim Thomas. July 27, 1990. Ring a bell?"

"Nope."

Morrison slid a photo across the table. Kim's driver's license photo, taken a year before her death. Smiling, alive, unaware of what was coming.

Gales looked at it without expression. "Never seen her."

"She lived on Churchill Road. Cotswold. You know the street?"

Something flickered across Gales' face. "Maybe. I know a lot of streets."

"You shot a woman on Churchill Road in 1974. You were sixteen."

Gales leaned back in his chair. "That was a long time ago."

"Twenty years. You remember it?"

"I served my time."

Morrison opened the file and pulled out the map he'd printed. Two addresses circled in red. "This is where Kim Thomas lived. This is where you lived in 1990. Five and a half minute walk. You're telling me you never saw her?"

"I'm telling you I don't know her."

"You were in the neighborhood on July 27, 1990."

"Says who?"

"A neighbor saw you."

Gales smiled. "Then arrest me."

Morrison wanted to. Everything in him wanted to slap cuffs on this man and drag him to a cell. But wanting wasn't enough. He had proximity. History. A neighbor's vague recollection. None of it was enough.

"We're going to need a DNA sample," Morrison said.

"Why?"

"Because we have DNA from the crime scene. If you didn't do it, the test will prove it."

Gales considered this. Then he shrugged. "Fine. Take your sample. I got nothing to hide."

Morrison called in the crime scene tech. Gales opened his mouth, let them swab his cheek, signed the consent form without reading it. He wasn't worried. And that made Morrison's skin crawl.

"I've never seen Miss Thomas before, or her husband," Gales said as Morrison walked him back to holding. "I don't know anything about it."

Morrison watched him go. Marion Gales walked like a man who knew he was untouchable. Because in 1995, he was.

---

The DNA results came back six weeks later.

Morrison opened the report with hands that shook slightly. This was it. The break they needed. Marion Gales' DNA compared against the unknown male profile from the crime scene. If they matched, they had their killer. If they didn't, Morrison was back to square one.

He read the first line. Then the second. Then he put the report down and closed his eyes.

*Insufficient sample quality for comparison.*

The DNA from the crime scene—the skin cells on the rug, the touch DNA on the handcuffs—was too degraded. Mixed with Kim's DNA, contaminated by five years of storage, broken down by time and temperature and the limitations of 1995 forensic technology.

The crime lab director called an hour later. "I'm sorry, Carl. We tried everything. The technology just isn't there yet."

"What do I tell the family?"

"Tell them we're keeping the evidence. Tell them that DNA technology is improving every year. Tell them that someday, maybe, we'll be able to test it."

Morrison hung up and walked to the conference room where his timeline still covered the whiteboard. Marion Gales' name at the center of it all.

They had their suspect. They knew he did it. The proximity, the history, the pattern—it all pointed to him. A violent man with a history of assaulting women, who lived a five-minute walk from the victim, who had committed violence on that same street before.

But knowing wasn't proving. And in criminal justice, you needed proof beyond reasonable doubt.

Marion Gales walked out of CMPD headquarters three days later, his parole violation sentence served. Morrison watched him leave through the window of his office.

The man who killed Kim Thomas was free. And there was nothing Morrison could do about it.

---

## CHAPTER 5: THE CIVIL VERDICT

Ed Friedland's lawyer had an idea that sounded crazy. That's what Ed thought when Barry Goldman first pitched it in his office overlooking downtown Charlotte.

"We sue him."

Ed stared at him. "Sue who?"

"Marion Gales. Wrongful death. Civil court."

"They can't even charge him criminally. How are we supposed to—"

"Different burden of proof," Goldman interrupted. He was in his fifties, gray at the temples, with the kind of confidence that came from winning cases nobody thought he could win. "Criminal court requires proof beyond reasonable doubt. That's a high bar. Ninety-five percent certain, maybe higher. But civil court? Preponderance of evidence. Fifty-one percent likely. We don't have to prove he did it beyond all doubt. We just have to prove it's more likely than not."

Ed was quiet for a long moment. It had been seven years since Kim's death. Seven years of police investigations, false accusations, DNA tests that went nowhere. Seven years of watching his daughter grow up without her mother while the man who killed her walked free.

"Will it work?" Ed asked.

"I don't know," Goldman said honestly. "But it's the only shot we have."

They filed the lawsuit in February 1997. Ed Friedland v. Marion Anthony Gales. Wrongful death. Damages: $8.6 million.

The number wasn't arbitrary. Goldman had calculated lost wages, lost companionship, pain and suffering, the cost of raising their daughter alone. You couldn't put a price on Kim's life. But the law required you to try.

Marion Gales was served with the lawsuit at his apartment in Grier Heights. According to the process server, Gales read the papers, laughed, and threw them in the trash.

"Tell that doctor he ain't getting shit from me," Gales said.

He was probably right. Marion Gales had no assets. No house, no car, no bank account with more than a few hundred dollars. Even if they won, collecting the judgment would be nearly impossible.

But that wasn't the point. The point was justice. The point was a jury saying out loud what the police couldn't prove: Marion Gales killed Kim Thomas.

---

The trial began on a Monday morning in October 1997.

Mecklenburg County Superior Court, civil division. Judge Patricia Crawford presiding. The courtroom was older, wood-paneled, with high ceilings and uncomfortable benches that creaked when you shifted your weight. The jury box held twelve citizens of Charlotte, summoned from their regular lives to decide whether a man was responsible for a woman's death.

Ed sat at the plaintiff's table with Barry Goldman and two associates. He wore a suit he'd bought for the occasion. Seven years since Kim's death, and this was the closest he'd come to a trial. His own trial, the one where he'd been charged with murder, had never happened. The DA dropped charges before it reached a courtroom. No evidence. No case.

This time, there was evidence. And there was a case.

Marion Gales sat at the defense table with a court-appointed attorney who looked like he'd rather be anywhere else. Gales wore an orange jumpsuit—he'd been picked up on a drug charge three weeks before trial and was being held without bond. He slouched in his chair, arms crossed, looking bored.

Barry Goldman stood for his opening statement.

"On July 27, 1990, Kim Thomas Friedland was murdered in her home. She was thirty-two years old. She was a mother, a wife, a women's rights activist who fought for equality and justice. She was handcuffed behind her back and her throat was slashed. Her ten-month-old daughter was in a crib in the next room, unaware that her mother had just been taken from her."

The jury was listening. Ed could see it in their faces. Horror. Sympathy. Anger.

"The Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police Department investigated this murder for seven years. They collected evidence. They interviewed witnesses. They tested DNA. And every piece of evidence pointed to one man: Marion Anthony Gales."

Goldman turned and pointed at Gales. The defendant didn't flinch.

"Marion Gales lived a five-and-a-half-minute walk from Kim Thomas' home. He had a history of violence against women stretching back to age sixteen. In 1974, he shot a woman on Churchill Road—the same street where Kim Thomas lived and died. In 1985, a woman accused him of rape. In July 1990, the same month Kim was murdered, Marion Gales beat his own sister so badly she required medical attention."

Goldman paused, letting that sink in.

"On July 27, 1990, Marion Gales was seen in the area of Churchill Road around the time of Kim's murder. When police finally questioned him in 1995, he denied ever seeing Kim Thomas. But the evidence tells a different story. The evidence says Marion Gales killed Kim Thomas. And that's what we're here to prove."

The defense attorney stood. His opening statement was shorter.

"The police couldn't charge my client because they have no evidence. They have suspicions. They have theories. They have a man with a criminal record who lived in the same neighborhood. That's not evidence. That's prejudice. The burden of proof in this case is still on the plaintiff. They have to prove it's more likely than not that Marion Gales is responsible. And they can't. Because he isn't."

He sat down. Ed glanced at Goldman, who gave him a small nod. This was going to be a fight.

---

The trial lasted six days.

Barry Goldman called twenty-three witnesses. Crime scene investigators who explained the forensics. Neighbors who testified to seeing Marion Gales in the area. The detective who interviewed Gales in 1995. Expert witnesses who explained DNA technology and why the evidence couldn't be processed in 1995 but might be someday.

They called Annie Roseboro, Marion's sister. She was forty now, graying at the temples, with the kind of tired eyes that came from a lifetime of violence. She testified about July 14, 1990—the day her brother beat her.

"Did you tell the police what happened?" Goldman asked.

"No."

"Why not?"

"I was scared. Marion said he'd kill me if I pressed charges."

"Did you believe him?"

Annie looked at her brother across the courtroom. Marion stared back, expressionless. "Yes."

Goldman showed her the domestic violence report filed by the responding officer. "This report says you refused medical treatment. Is that true?"

"I went to the hospital the next day. I had three broken ribs and a concussion."

The jury shifted in their seats. Ed watched one woman—late fifties, grandmother type—look at Marion Gales with something close to hatred.

The defense attorney tried to poke holes in the case. He pointed out that the neighbor who claimed to see Gales in the area couldn't say for certain what day it was. He argued that proximity wasn't proof—half the neighborhood lived within a ten-minute walk. He emphasized that the DNA evidence was inconclusive, that no murder weapon had been found, that Marion Gales had denied involvement.

"Innocent until proven guilty," the defense attorney said in his closing argument. "That's the standard in criminal court, and it should be the standard here. You can't convict a man based on his past. You can't convict a man because he lived in the wrong neighborhood. You can't convict a man because the police need someone to blame."

Barry Goldman's closing argument was different.

"This isn't about innocence or guilt. This is about responsibility. Kim Thomas is dead. Someone killed her. Someone handcuffed her, cut her throat, left her to bleed out on the floor while her baby cried in the next room. Someone did that. The question isn't whether Marion Gales is a bad person—we all know he is. The question is: Did he do this?"

Goldman walked the jury through the evidence again. The geography. The timeline. The history of violence. The interview where Gales denied ever seeing Kim Thomas, even though he lived five minutes away and had committed violence on her street before.

"Ask yourselves: Is it more likely than not that Marion Gales killed Kim Thomas? Not beyond all doubt. Not with one hundred percent certainty. But more likely than not. Fifty-one percent. Preponderance of evidence. That's the standard. And the evidence meets it."

He paused, looking at each juror in turn.

"Kim Thomas can't speak for herself. She can't tell you what happened that morning. She can't tell you who knocked on her door, who she opened it for, who handcuffed her and killed her. But the evidence can speak for her. And it says Marion Gales is responsible."

The jury deliberated for eleven hours.

Ed spent that time in a conference room down the hall from the courtroom, unable to eat or sleep or think about anything except what would happen if they lost. Seven years of grief, seven years of investigation, seven years of waiting—all of it coming down to twelve strangers in a room deciding whether his wife's death meant anything.

At 4:17 PM on the second day of deliberations, the bailiff knocked on the door.

"We have a verdict."

---

Ed's hands shook as he walked back into the courtroom. The jury filed in slowly, avoiding eye contact with both tables. Judge Crawford took her seat.

"Has the jury reached a verdict?"

The foreman stood. He was a man in his sixties, gray hair, wearing a tie that looked uncomfortable. "We have, Your Honor."

"In the case of Friedland v. Gales, on the charge of wrongful death, how do you find?"

The foreman looked down at the paper in his hands.

"We find the defendant, Marion Anthony Gales, liable for the death of Kim Thomas Friedland."

Ed felt his breath leave his body. Liable. They'd said it. A jury had said it out loud.

"And what damages do you award?" Judge Crawford asked.

"Eight point six million dollars."

The courtroom erupted. Reporters scrambled for the doors. Family members wept. Ed sat frozen, unable to process what had just happened.

Marion Gales stood slowly, his face unreadable. His attorney put a hand on his shoulder, but Gales shrugged it off. He turned and looked directly at Ed.

Then he smiled.

It was a small smile, barely visible, but it said everything. *You think you won? I'll never pay a dime. I'll never see the inside of a prison for this. You got your verdict, but I'm still walking out of here.*

And he was right.

---

The judgment was filed three days later. Ed Friedland v. Marion Anthony Gales. Liable. $8.6 million in damages.

Barry Goldman filed the paperwork to collect. He put liens on any property Gales might acquire, garnishments on any wages he might earn, holds on any bank accounts in his name.

Marion Gales had nothing. No house, no car, no job, no assets. The judgment was a piece of paper, and nothing more.

Two weeks after the verdict, Gales was released from jail on the drug charge. Time served. He walked out of Mecklenburg County Jail wearing the same clothes he'd worn at trial.

Ed watched the news coverage from his living room. A reporter shoved a microphone in Gales' face.

"What do you have to say about the verdict?"

"I didn't kill that woman," Gales said. "The jury got it wrong."

"Do you plan to appeal?"

Gales laughed. "With what money?"

He walked away, free. A jury had said he was responsible for Kim Thomas' death. An $8.6 million judgment said he owed. But criminal charges required a higher standard of proof, and the DNA technology wasn't there yet.

Marion Gales went back to his life. Ed went back to his.

---

The years passed slowly.

1998. 1999. 2000. Each anniversary of Kim's death felt like reopening a wound that had never fully healed. Ed marked the dates quietly—private memorials, flowers at her grave, phone calls with her parents who were aging now, worn down by grief that had no resolution.

Marion Gales kept getting arrested. Drug possession. Assault. Parole violations. In and out of the prison system, never gone for long, always coming back. Every time Ed heard about another arrest, he felt a flash of hope. Maybe this time. Maybe this time they'll charge him.

They never did.

The DNA evidence sat in the CMPD crime lab, preserved in a temperature-controlled room, waiting for technology to catch up. Every few years, a new detective would call Ed. They'd re-tested the evidence. Still inconclusive. Still degraded. Still insufficient for criminal charges.

"But the technology is improving," they always said. "Someday."

Someday.

Ed's daughter grew up. Graduated high school. Went to college. Built a life that Kim would never see. Ed remarried—a good woman who understood that part of his heart would always belong to his first wife. They didn't talk about the case often. There was nothing new to say.

But in 2008, something changed.

The CMPD crime lab got a new director. Matthew Mathis. Young, ambitious, with a background in cutting-edge forensic technology. One of his first initiatives was to review every cold case with DNA evidence, using the latest testing methods.

Kim Thomas' case went to the front of the line.

The new tests came back in September 2008. Ed got the call from Detective Morrison, who'd never stopped working the case even after he retired.

"The DNA definitively excludes you," Morrison said. "I know you were already cleared, but this is absolute. No question. Zero chance you were involved."

Ed had known that for eighteen years. But hearing it again, backed by science that didn't exist in 1990, felt like an exoneration he hadn't known he needed.

"What about Gales?"

"Still working on it. The technology is better, but we're not there yet."

Not yet. Always "not yet."

---

The breakthrough came in 2022, though Ed didn't know it at the time.

The CMPD crime lab acquired probabilistic genotyping software. A new technology that used complex mathematical algorithms and biological modeling to analyze DNA samples that were previously unusable. Degraded samples. Mixed samples. Trace amounts from touch DNA.

The kind of evidence that had been sitting in the Kim Thomas file for thirty-two years.

Director Matthew Mathis personally selected the case for re-analysis. The rug from beside Kim's body. The handcuffs. The swabs taken from surfaces in the room. All of it re-tested using software that hadn't existed when the evidence was collected.

The analysis took three years. Multiple rounds of testing. Cross-checking. Verification by outside labs. The science was new enough that it had to be bulletproof before they could bring charges.

In February 2026, the final report was completed.

Marion Anthony Gales' DNA. On the rug beside Kim Thomas' body. Statistical probability: quadrillions to one.

Detective Morrison, retired for fifteen years but still on the department's contact list, got the call first. Then they called Ed.

"We're arresting him," the current detective said. "Tomorrow morning. Thirty-five years late, but we're doing it."

Ed sat down slowly in his kitchen chair. His wife—his second wife, the woman who'd helped him rebuild a life after Kim—put her hand on his shoulder.

"When?" Ed asked.

"Tomorrow. We're sending the Violent Criminal Apprehension Team to pick him up at six AM."

Ed hung up and stared at the calendar on the wall. February 18, 2026. Thirty-five years, six months, and twenty-two days since Kim died.

---

Marion Anthony Gales was sixty-three years old when they arrested him.

He was living in a small apartment in east Charlotte, alone, surviving on disability payments and whatever money he could make doing odd jobs that didn't require background checks. His body had aged badly—decades of drugs and violence and prison had left him with bad knees, worse lungs, and a face that looked older than his years.

The VCAP team knocked on his door at 6:04 AM on February 19, 2026.

Gales opened the door in boxers and an undershirt, squinting against the early morning light.

"Marion Anthony Gales?"

"Yeah?"

"You're under arrest for the murder of Kim Thomas Friedland."

They read him his rights while they cuffed him. Gales didn't resist. He stood there in his doorway, hands behind his back, looking confused.

"This is about that lady from 1990?"

"Yes sir."

"I already beat that case. You can't try me twice."

"You were never tried," the detective said. "You were found liable in civil court, but never charged criminally. Until now."

They walked him to the car. Neighbors watched from their windows. The news crews wouldn't arrive for another hour, but cell phone videos were already starting to circulate online.

Marion Gales sat in the back of the police car, handcuffed, the same way Kim Thomas had been handcuffed thirty-five years earlier.

This time, the cuffs stayed on.

---

The press conference was held at noon on February 20, 2026.

CMPD headquarters, briefing room, packed with reporters from local and national media. The Kim Thomas case had been a legend in Charlotte true crime circles for decades—the cold case that DNA couldn't solve, the civil verdict that meant nothing, the killer who walked free.

Deputy Chief Ryan Butler stood at the podium.

"This morning, our Violent Criminal Apprehension Team located and arrested Marion Anthony Gales based on work done by our cold case homicide detectives and our crime laboratory. Mr. Gales has been charged with first-degree murder in the death of Kim Thomas Friedland, who was killed in her home on July 27, 1990."

The room erupted with questions. Butler held up a hand.

"The breakthrough came from new DNA analysis technology called probabilistic genotyping. This software allowed our lab to analyze degraded evidence that was collected at the crime scene thirty-five years ago but couldn't be processed with the technology available at the time."

Crime Lab Director Matthew Mathis stepped to the microphone.

"We're now able to develop DNA profiles from evidence that we couldn't develop profiles from twenty years ago," Mathis explained. "In this case, we were able to analyze trace DNA from the crime scene and match it definitively to the suspect. The statistical probability is high enough to meet the threshold for criminal prosecution."

A reporter called out: "Why did it take thirty-five years?"

"Because the technology didn't exist," Mathis said simply. "We preserved the evidence properly. We continued testing as new methods became available. We never stopped working this case. And finally, science caught up."

Ed Friedland declined to attend the press conference. He issued a statement through Barry Goldman, his attorney:

"For thirty-five years, my family has waited for justice. We are grateful to the Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police Department and the crime lab for their persistence and dedication. While this arrest cannot bring Kim back, it is a measure of closure that we have desperately needed. Kim was a mother, an activist, and a force for good in this world. She deserved better than to die the way she did. And she deserved better than to wait thirty-five years for her killer to face justice."

Marion Gales was arraigned on February 21, 2026. He pleaded not guilty. Bond was denied. The trial was scheduled for late 2026, though legal experts predicted delays and appeals that could push it into 2027 or beyond.

In his cell at Mecklenburg County Jail, Marion Gales told a cellmate that the whole thing was bullshit. That he'd never touched Kim Thomas. That the DNA was contaminated. That they were framing him because they needed someone to blame.

The cellmate reported the conversation to a guard, who included it in a report that would eventually be used at trial.

---

Ed Friedland stood at Kim's grave on July 27, 2026. Thirty-six years to the day since her death. His daughter stood beside him—thirty-six years old now, the same age her mother never reached.

Flowers covered the headstone. Not just from family, but from strangers who'd followed the case, who'd read about the arrest, who wanted to honor a woman they'd never met.

"Do you think it's enough?" his daughter asked.

Ed didn't answer right away. Was it enough? Thirty-six years of waiting. A civil verdict that meant nothing. A criminal arrest that came three decades too late. Marion Gales would likely spend the rest of his life in prison, but how much life did he have left? He was sixty-three, in poor health, with nothing to lose.

Kim had died at thirty-two. She'd missed her daughter's childhood, her daughter's graduation, her daughter's wedding. She'd missed thirty-six years of life because a violent man who lived five minutes away had decided to take it from her.

"No," Ed said finally. "It's not enough. But it's what we have."

They stood there for a long time, father and daughter, at the grave of the woman who connected them. The woman whose murder had defined both their lives in ways neither of them had asked for.

Marion Gales had been found liable for Kim Thomas' murder. A jury said he did it. An $8.6 million judgment said he owed. But he walked free, because DNA technology wasn't advanced enough to prove it beyond reasonable doubt.

Not yet.

Thirty-five years later, "not yet" had finally become "now."

Justice had arrived. Late, incomplete, and inadequate. But it had arrived.

Kim Thomas could finally rest.
