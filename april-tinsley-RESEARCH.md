# The Murder of April Tinsley: A 30-Year Cold Case Solved by DNA Genealogy

## CASE SUMMARY

On April 1, 1988, eight-year-old April Marie Tinsley was abducted while walking to retrieve her umbrella from a 
friend's house in Fort Wayne, Indiana. Three days later, on April 4, 1988, a jogger discovered her body in a ditch 
along County Road 68 in Spencerville, approximately 20 miles from her home. She had been sexually assaulted and 
strangled to death. The case went cold for decades, haunted by the killer's taunting messages: a barn graffiti in 
1990, and disturbing notes left on girls' bicycles with used condoms in 2004 and 2009. The killer wrote, "I kill 8 
year old April M Tinsley, did you find her other shoe haha I will kill agin." On July 15, 2018, investigators 
arrested John D. Miller, 59, of Grabill, Indiana, after genetic genealogy narrowed suspects to two brothers. When 
confronted by police, Miller immediately said "April Tinsley" and confessed to the crime. He pleaded guilty on 
December 7, 2018, and was sentenced to 80 years in prison (50 years for murder, 30 years for child molestation) on 
December 21, 2018. Miller died of natural causes on September 4, 2025, at age 66, while serving his sentence.

## VICTIM PROFILE

### Early Life & Family Background

April Marie Tinsley was born on March 18, 1980, in Fort Wayne, Indiana, to parents Janet and Michael Tinsley. She 
lived with her mother, father, and infant younger brother (Paul Tinsley) at 300 West Williams Street in Fort Wayne's 
south side. April was described by her mother as "a fun, loving person, she was just the perfect daughter Janet 
always wanted." The family attended Faith United Methodist Church, where April sang in the children's choir.

Fort Wayne in the late 1980s was a mid-sized Midwestern city where children routinely played outside unsupervised, 
walking between friends' houses in neighborhoods where everyone knew each other. April's disappearance shattered 
that sense of safety for the entire community.

### Education & Personality

April was a second-grade student at Fairfield Elementary School in Fort Wayne. According to family and school 
records, she was well-liked at school, described as a sweet, friendly girl who got along well with classmates and 
teachers. She was an active child who enjoyed playing outside with friends and participated in her church choir.

### Life Before the Crime

On April 1, 1988—Good Friday—schools dismissed early. April ate lunch at home and asked her mother for permission to 
play with two friends who lived just a few blocks away. It was an ordinary spring day. April and her two friends 
moved between houses, playing as children do. When the group moved to another friend's house, April realized she had 
left her umbrella behind and decided to walk back alone to retrieve it. It was a walk of only three blocks through a 
familiar neighborhood. That short walk would be the last time anyone saw April alive.

### Family's Decades-Long Ordeal

April's mother, Janet Tinsley, spent 30 years waiting for justice. In interviews following the arrest, she said: 
"Right now I'm numb. I can't believe it's finally here." She had feared an arrest would never come. On the 30th 
anniversary of April's death in 2018 (months before Miller's arrest), Janet revealed what haunted her most: "The 
main question I would ask is why. What made her stand out the most? That'd be the main question I would ask him over 
and over and over again—why?"

Janet's sister, Teresa Tinsley (April's aunt), told reporters after the arrest: "If it wouldn't have been for DNA, I 
don't think we wouldn't have gotten as far as we have." The family initially pushed prosecutors to seek the death 
penalty but ultimately accepted the plea agreement.

According to court testimony, the children in the Tinsley family—April's younger brother Paul and numerous cousins—
were "extremely sheltered and protected" following April's murder. The family struggled with health issues and stress 
for three decades while the case remained unsolved.

## PERPETRATOR PROFILE

### Early Life & Family Background

John D. Miller was born circa 1959 [exact date not publicly available]. Public records provide minimal information 
about his childhood or family background. By 1988, when he committed the murder, Miller was 29 years old. He lived 
in or near Grabill, Indiana, a small town adjacent to Fort Wayne with a population of approximately 1,000 residents.

Miller worked for Eagle-Picher Plastics (later R3 Composites), a fiberglass factory in Grabill, throughout the 
1970s, 1980s, and 1990s. This gave him stable employment and deep roots in the area.

### Lifestyle & Behavior

Neighbors at the Grabill Mobile Home Park (Lot #4 on Main Street), where Miller lived at the time of his arrest in 
2018, described him as "someone who kept to himself and never said hi to anyone," according to WANE-TV and WISH-TV 
reporting. Multiple neighbors characterized Miller as "secluded and often angry" (Wikipedia citing Fort Wayne Police 
records). He had no significant social connections that investigators discovered.

Miller lived alone in a trailer. His brother also lived in the Grabill area, which became crucial to the genetic 
genealogy investigation that eventually identified him.

### Psychological Profile

No formal psychiatric evaluation of Miller was made public prior to his arrest. However, behavioral evidence 
suggests several concerning patterns:

**Compulsion to Taunt:** Miller's decision to leave multiple taunting messages over decades—first in 1990, then 
again in 2004, and 2009—reveals a psychological need for attention and control. Forensic psychologists note that 
offenders who taunt police often experience:
- Need for dominance and power over investigators
- Narcissistic tendencies requiring acknowledgment of their crimes
- Thrill-seeking behavior that extends beyond the initial offense
- Possible guilt or unconscious desire to be caught (evidenced by leaving DNA-containing condoms with notes)

**The 16-Year Gap:** Miller's silence from 1990 to 2004, followed by renewed taunting, raises questions. Possible 
explanations include:
- External life stressors triggering renewed compulsion
- Anniversary effects (2004 was 16 years after the crime)
- Frustration that the case had gone cold and he wasn't receiving attention
- Deteriorating impulse control as he aged

**Immediate Confession:** When confronted by police on July 15, 2018, Miller's first words were "April Tinsley." 
This immediate recognition suggests:
- He had been expecting this moment for 30 years
- Possible relief at finally being caught (some serial offenders report this)
- He knew DNA evidence was insurmountable

Detective Brian Martin stated after sentencing: "For the 59-year-old, [80 years] is essentially life in prison. We 
have one less monster on the street. My end game was to get John Miller off the streets forever and we did that."

### Criminal History

No prior criminal record for John D. Miller was publicly reported. He had no arrests, no sex offender registry 
entries, and no known history of violence before April Tinsley's murder. This made him virtually invisible to 
traditional investigative methods for three decades.

## THE CRIME

### Date, Time & Location

**Date:** April 1, 1988 (Good Friday)  
**Time:** Approximately 3:00 PM (abduction)  
**Location (Abduction):** 300 block of West Williams Street, Fort Wayne, Indiana—residential neighborhood on city's 
south side  
**Location (Murder):** John Miller's trailer in Grabill, Indiana (per his confession)  
**Location (Body Disposal):** Ditch along County Road 68 near Spencerville, DeKalb County, Indiana—approximately 20 
miles northeast of Fort Wayne

### The Abduction

April Tinsley, her umbrella in hand, was walking alone the three blocks back to her friend's house to retrieve the 
item she'd left behind. She was last seen alive in the 300 block of West Williams Street around 3:00 PM. 

Based on Miller's confession, he abducted her from the street in Fort Wayne, though the exact method of abduction 
was not detailed in public records. A witness later reported seeing a composite sketch suspect, and another motorist 
reported seeing a blue pickup truck near the body disposal site, though it's unclear if this was Miller's vehicle.

Miller transported April approximately 15 miles to his trailer in Grabill.

### The Murder

According to Miller's confession given to Fort Wayne Police on July 15, 2018 (per probable cause affidavit and court 
records):

1. Miller took April to his trailer in Grabill
2. He sexually assaulted her
3. He strangled her to death with his bare hands
4. He then sexually assaulted her deceased body (necrophilia)
5. He disposed of her body in a rural ditch the following day

Miller's prepared statement read in court on December 7, 2018, admitted he "abducted 8-year-old April Tinsley, had 
sexual intercourse with her and strangled her with his bare hands."

### Physical Evidence

**From the Crime Scene (1988):**
- April's body found in ditch on County Road 68, wrapped or near a plastic bag
- One of her shoes was missing (referenced in 1990 taunt: "did you find her other shoe")
- Sex toy found in shopping bag near body
- DNA recovered from April's underwear—semen from unknown male perpetrator
- Autopsy findings (see below)

**From Taunting Messages (1990, 2004, 2009):**
- **1990 barn message:** Written in crayon/pencil on barn door. Crayons found nearby. No DNA recovered from this 
message
- **2004 notes:** Four handwritten notes on lined yellow paper, placed in plastic bags. Three bags contained used 
condoms with semen. Some bags contained Polaroid photographs showing nude male lower torso
- **DNA from 2004 condoms:** Matched DNA from April's 1988 underwear, conclusively linking taunts to killer

**From Trash Surveillance (2018):**
- Three used condoms collected from Miller's trash on July 9, 2018
- DNA from these condoms matched 1988 crime scene DNA and 2004 taunt DNA
- This evidence led to Miller's arrest

### The Scene

April's body was discovered by a jogger on April 4, 1988, in a ditch along County Road 68 near Spencerville, a rural 
area in DeKalb County approximately 20 miles northeast of Fort Wayne. The location was isolated—a roadside ditch 
with fields nearby. The barn where the 1990 taunting message appeared was "not far from where her body was found" 
(per FBI statement), suggesting Miller had geographic familiarity with the disposal area.

The Grabill Mobile Home Park where Miller lived was also near this rural area, creating a geographic triangle: Fort 
Wayne (abduction) → Grabill (murder) → Spencerville (disposal).

### Cause of Death

**Official Cause:** Asphyxiation (strangulation)

**Autopsy Findings** (per probable cause affidavit and TIME magazine reporting citing medical examiner):
- Cause of death: Asphyxiation
- Mechanism: Manual strangulation (Miller admitted strangling her "with his bare hands")
- Evidence of sexual assault
- Body had been deceased for approximately 1-2 days when discovered on April 4 (consistent with April 1 abduction)

The autopsy report "suggested she had been raped and then strangled to death" (Wikipedia citing official records). 
Forensic examination found male DNA on April's underwear, which was preserved and eventually matched to John Miller 
30 years later.

## THE INVESTIGATION

### Initial Response (April 1988)

When April failed to return home on April 1, 1988, her mother Janet Tinsley reported her missing to Fort Wayne 
Police at approximately 6:00 PM. Officers arrived at the Tinsley home and began searching the neighborhood. An 
immediate search was launched, but April was not found that night.

For three agonizing days, the community searched for April. Volunteers scoured the area, hoping she had simply 
gotten lost or run away. That hope ended on April 4 when a jogger found her body 20 miles away.

### Early Investigation & False Leads

Fort Wayne Police immediately launched a homicide investigation. Key early actions included:

- **April 26, 1988:** Police sent DNA samples from April's underwear and five suspects to a private laboratory in 
Germantown, Maryland, for DNA profiling. However, DNA technology in 1988 was primitive compared to modern standards. 
The results were inconclusive and could not identify a specific perpetrator.

- **Composite Sketch:** Police released a composite sketch of a suspect based on witness accounts of someone seen in 
the area during April's abduction. This sketch circulated for years but never led to a conclusive identification.

- **Early Suspect:** Authorities identified a 34-year-old suspect who was charged with child molestation in a 
separate case. He was investigated for April's murder but was acquitted of the unrelated molestation charges the 
following month and cleared in April's case.

The investigation stalled. With no CODIS database (established nationally in 1998), no familial DNA searching, and 
limited forensic technology, police could only preserve the DNA evidence and hope for a future breakthrough.

### Key Investigators

- **Detective Brian Martin** (Fort Wayne Police Department): Lead detective who in May 2018 made the critical 
decision to send DNA samples to Parabon NanoLabs for genetic genealogy testing. Martin was instrumental in the 
final investigation that led to Miller's arrest.

- **Fort Wayne Police Chief Rusty York** (as of 2006): Made public appeals for information during cold case reviews

- **CeCe Moore** (Genetic Genealogist, Parabon NanoLabs): Lead genetic genealogist who performed the familial DNA 
work that identified the two Miller brothers as suspects

On October 26, 2018, the Indiana State Police honored three Fort Wayne investigators for their work in finally 
identifying Miller.

### The Taunting Messages: A Break and a Curse

**1990 Barn Message:**

In May 1990, two years after the murder, police were called to a barn in St. Joseph Township (near where April's 
body had been found). Someone had scrawled a chilling message in crayon:

> "I kill 8 year old April Marie Tisley [sic], did you find the other shoe haha I will kill agin [sic]"

This confirmed the killer was still in the area and actively thinking about the crime. The misspellings and mocking 
tone ("haha") demonstrated both limited education and sadistic pleasure. However, no usable DNA was recovered from 
this message.

**2004 Bicycle Notes:**

In spring 2004—16 years after the murder—the killer struck again, but this time he left evidence that would 
eventually seal his fate. Four locations in Fort Wayne received taunting notes. Three were left on girls' bicycles 
in their yards; one was left in a mailbox. The notes, written on lined yellow paper, read:

> "Hi Honey I Been watching you I am the same person that kinapped an Rape an kill Aproil Tinsely [sic] you are my 
next vitem [sic]"

The notes were placed in plastic bags along with:
- Used condoms containing semen
- Polaroid photographs showing a nude male's lower body

**Critical Error:** By including used condoms, the killer provided DNA evidence directly. Police tested the semen 
and confirmed it matched the 1988 DNA from April's underwear. This was the break investigators needed—but without a 
match in any criminal database, they still couldn't identify the perpetrator.

**2009 Messages:**

Additional taunting messages appeared in 2009. The FBI joined the investigation, publicly calling the case "highly 
solvable" given the DNA evidence. Special Agent Todd Bockelman told CNN: "We have DNA, we have the notes, we have 
the condoms. We're confident this will be solved."

Yet it would take another nine years.

### Breakthrough Moment: Genetic Genealogy (2018)

The case was revolutionized by the April 2018 arrest of the Golden State Killer in California using genetic 
genealogy—a technique combining DNA analysis with traditional genealogical research through public ancestry 
databases.

**May 11, 2018:** Just two weeks after the Golden State Killer arrest made headlines, Fort Wayne Police Detective 
Brian Martin contacted Parabon NanoLabs in Virginia. Parabon specialized in:
- DNA phenotyping (creating suspect composite images from DNA)
- Genetic genealogy (identifying suspects through familial DNA matches)

**The Process:**

1. **DNA Upload:** Parabon uploaded the suspect's DNA profile (from the 1988 crime scene and 2004 condoms) to 
GEDMatch, a free public genealogy database where people upload their DNA results to find relatives.

2. **Family Tree Construction:** Genetic genealogist CeCe Moore, lead researcher at Parabon, found distant relatives 
of the suspect in the database—second and third cousins who had uploaded their DNA for ancestry research, unaware 
their genetic information would help solve a murder.

3. **Narrowing the Pool:** Moore built out family trees from these distant matches, working backward through 
generations to find common ancestors. She then worked forward, identifying all descendants of those ancestors who 
would be the right age, sex, and geographic location to be the suspect.

4. **Final Two Suspects:** On July 2, 2018—just six weeks after Detective Martin contacted Parabon—CeCe Moore 
narrowed the suspect pool to two brothers living in the Grabill, Indiana area: John D. Miller and his brother.

**Trash Surveillance:**

Fort Wayne detectives began surveillance of John Miller's residence at Lot #4, Grabill Mobile Home Park. On July 9, 
2018, investigators collected Miller's trash from the curb—a legal search that doesn't require a warrant.

Inside the trash: three used condoms.

**DNA Match:**

On July 12, 2018, the Indiana State Police crime lab confirmed: the DNA from Miller's trash-collected condoms was a 
match to:
- The 1988 DNA from April Tinsley's underwear
- The 2004 DNA from the taunting notes' condoms
- The 2009 taunting evidence

After 30 years, police had their killer.

### The Confrontation and Confession

On July 15, 2018, Fort Wayne Police detectives approached John Miller at his Grabill home. According to the probable 
cause affidavit:

**Detective:** "Do you know why we're here?"

**Miller:** "April Tinsley."

He knew. He had been waiting.

During the subsequent interview, Miller confessed to:
- Abducting April from Fort Wayne on April 1, 1988
- Taking her to his trailer in Grabill
- Sexually assaulting her
- Strangling her to death with his bare hands
- Sexually assaulting her deceased body
- Disposing of her remains in a ditch the following day

He did not explicitly confess to leaving the taunting messages in the recorded interview, but the DNA evidence 
linking him to the 2004 and 2009 notes was irrefutable.

### Mistakes or Controversies

**Limited Initial DNA Technology (1988):** Police preserved DNA evidence from 1988, but testing technology at the 
time could not generate a usable profile for comparison. This was not investigator error—the technology simply 
didn't exist yet. By preserving the evidence carefully, investigators enabled the eventual 2018 breakthrough.

**No CODIS Hit:** Miller had no prior criminal record, so his DNA was never entered into CODIS (the FBI's Combined 
DNA Index System). Traditional DNA database searching—which only works if the suspect's DNA is already in the 
system—failed. This case demonstrated the power of genetic genealogy to identify "invisible" perpetrators with no 
criminal history.

**Privacy Concerns:** The use of GEDMatch raised privacy concerns. People who uploaded their DNA to find relatives 
did not consent to police using their genetic information to solve crimes. However, GEDMatch's terms of service 
(later updated) allowed law enforcement use, and courts have consistently ruled this technique legal. In Miller's 
case, the technique worked exactly as intended: a child murderer who had evaded justice for 30 years was finally 
caught.

## PSYCHOLOGY & MOTIVE ANALYSIS

### Established Motive

The prosecution presented no complex motive beyond sexual gratification and opportunity. Miller was a pedophile and 
sexual sadist who saw an unattended child and acted on his impulses. 

**Evidence of Premeditation:**
- Miller did not know April personally (no relationship established)
- The abduction appears opportunistic—April was alone, vulnerable, and in the wrong place at the wrong time
- However, Miller had the means to abduct, transport, and conceal: a vehicle, a private trailer, and knowledge of 
rural disposal sites

**Evidence of Sexual Sadism:**
- Post-mortem sexual assault (necrophilia) indicates severe paraphilia beyond "ordinary" sexual assault
- The taunting messages demonstrate pleasure derived from prolonging fear in the community and police
- Targeting young girls in 2004 taunting notes suggests ongoing predatory thoughts

### Psychological Factors

**Paraphilias:**
Miller's crimes demonstrate multiple paraphilic disorders:
- **Pedophilia:** Sexual attraction to prepubescent children
- **Necrophilia:** Sexual attraction to corpses (admitted post-mortem sexual assault)
- **Sexual Sadism Disorder:** Deriving pleasure from causing suffering (evidenced by taunting and choice of victim)

No forensic psychologist was appointed to evaluate Miller pre-trial because he pleaded guilty, avoiding the need for 
expert testimony. However, clinical literature on offenders with similar profiles suggests:

**Narcissistic Traits:**
- Need for attention and recognition (taunting messages)
- Grandiosity (mocking police: "did you find her other shoe haha")
- Lack of empathy (31 years of silence about victim's family suffering)

**Impulse Control Deficits:**
- The 2004 and 2009 taunts were high-risk behaviors that served no logical purpose
- By including used condoms with DNA, Miller essentially provided the evidence that would convict him
- This suggests compulsion overriding rational self-preservation

**The "Waiting" Phenomenon:**

Miller's first words when confronted—"April Tinsley"—followed by immediate, detailed confession, suggest he had been 
psychologically preparing for this moment for three decades. Some offenders report relief when finally caught, 
describing their crimes as a burden they couldn't escape. Whether Miller felt guilt, relief, or simply inevitability 
is unknown; he never gave interviews or made public statements beyond his court confession.

### The Taunting: Why?

**Why Start Taunting Two Years Later (1990)?**

Possible psychological explanations:
1. **Anniversary Effect:** Two years provided enough distance to feel safe, but the anniversary may have triggered 
emotional needs
2. **Frustration with Lack of Recognition:** The case had gone cold; Miller may have felt "forgotten"
3. **Testing Boundaries:** Taunting allowed him to re-engage with the crime without committing another murder
4. **Deteriorating Impulse Control:** Initial success may have emboldened him

**Why Wait 14 More Years (1990-2004)?**

This remains one of the case's greatest mysteries. Theories include:
1. **Life Stressors:** Unknown events in 2003-2004 may have triggered renewed compulsion (job loss, relationship 
issues, health problems)
2. **16th Anniversary:** 2004 marked 16 years since the murder—a psychological milestone
3. **Media Coverage:** Cold case reviews or anniversary coverage may have reignited his need for attention
4. **Escalation Fantasy:** He may have been contemplating another abduction and used the taunts as a form of 
"practice"

**Why Include DNA Evidence (2004)?**

This is perhaps the most irrational aspect of Miller's behavior:
- He had successfully evaded capture for 16 years
- DNA technology had advanced significantly since 1988
- By including used condoms, he guaranteed police could link the taunts to the original crime
- He provided fresh DNA that could be tested with modern technology

Explanations:
1. **Sexual Compulsion:** The condoms/photos may have had sexual significance to Miller, overriding rational caution
2. **Unconscious Desire to be Caught:** Psychodynamic theory suggests some criminals self-sabotage due to guilt
3. **Narcissistic Invincibility:** Miller may have believed he was too smart to be caught, even with DNA
4. **Ignorance of DNA Technology:** Miller may not have understood that genetic genealogy would eventually make him 
identifiable

### Alternative Theories

**Defense Theory:**
Miller's guilty plea meant no defense theory was presented at trial. However, hypothetical defenses might have 
included:
- Diminished capacity due to mental illness
- Intoxication at time of crime
- False confession under interrogation pressure

None of these were pursued, and Miller's detailed confession matched the physical evidence perfectly.

**Unsolved Questions:**
- Did Miller commit other crimes? (No evidence found, but not ruled out)
- Why did he specifically target April? (Appears random/opportunistic)
- What triggered the 2004 and 2009 taunting episodes? (Unknown; Miller never explained)

## AFTERMATH & CULTURAL IMPACT

### Media Coverage and Public Reaction

The April Tinsley case became Fort Wayne's most notorious unsolved murder, haunting the community for 30 years. 
Local media covered every development, anniversary, and cold case review. When Miller was arrested in July 2018, the 
story received national and international attention:

- **CNN, BBC, NBC News, CBS News, ABC News, TIME Magazine** covered the arrest and genetic genealogy breakthrough
- **True Crime Podcasts:** Casefile True Crime Podcast produced Episode 282 on the case
- **Television:** The case was featured on "Cold Case Files" (2025 episode: "The Evil Among Us")

The arrest came just two months after the Golden State Killer case, making April Tinsley's case part of the larger 
narrative about genetic genealogy revolutionizing cold case investigations.

### Community Impact

**Immediate Aftermath (1988):**
- **April 20, 1988:** Ninety Fort Wayne residents formed APRIL (Associated Parents Regional Independent League / 
Abduction Prevention Reconnaissance and Information League), a volunteer organization to help solve missing children 
cases and prevent future abductions
- Parents became more protective; the era of children walking alone in Fort Wayne neighborhoods largely ended
- The taunting messages in 1990, 2004, and 2009 terrorized the community each time, particularly parents of young 
girls whose bicycles received the notes

**April's Garden Memorial:**
- **2015:** Construction began on "April's Garden," a memorial dedicated to April at the corner of Hoagland and 
Masterson Avenues on Fort Wayne's south side (near where she lived)
- The garden became a community gathering place for remembrance
- **July 28, 2018:** Memorial walk held following Miller's arrest
- **September 6, 2025:** Following Miller's death, community held balloon release at April's Garden to honor April's 
memory

**Fairfield Elementary School:**
- A memorial to April was installed at Fairfield Elementary School, where she was a second-grader
- Candlelight vigils have been held at the school on anniversaries

### Changes to Law or Policy

**Genetic Genealogy Acceptance:**
The April Tinsley case, along with the Golden State Killer case, helped establish genetic genealogy as a legitimate 
and legal investigative tool. While the technique raised privacy concerns, its success in solving decades-old child 
murders made it difficult to oppose.

**GEDMatch Policy Changes:**
Following law enforcement use, GEDMatch updated its terms of service to:
- Explicitly inform users that their data might be used by law enforcement
- Offer opt-in/opt-out options for law enforcement matching
- Require law enforcement to follow specific protocols

**Indiana DNA Preservation:**
The case demonstrated the critical importance of preserving DNA evidence even when current technology cannot process 
it. Fort Wayne Police's decision to preserve April's underwear in 1988—despite inconclusive initial testing—made the 
2018 breakthrough possible.

### Victim Advocacy

Janet Tinsley became an inadvertent spokesperson for families of long-unsolved murder victims. Her 30-year wait for 
justice illustrated both the trauma of unsolved cases and the hope that new technology could provide closure.

The Tinsley family's story influenced:
- Support groups for families of murdered children
- Advocacy for cold case units and funding
- Public awareness of genetic genealogy's potential

### Lasting Legacy

April Tinsley's murder and its eventual resolution represents:

1. **The Power of Persistence:** Fort Wayne Police never gave up, preserving evidence for 30 years
2. **Technological Hope:** Families of cold case victims now know that unsolved cases can be solved, even decades later
3. **Community Memory:** Fort Wayne continues to honor April through memorials, vigils, and education
4. **The End of Perfect Crimes:** Genetic genealogy means that criminals can no longer assume they'll evade justice 
simply by having no prior record

As of 2025, Parabon NanoLabs reports using genetic genealogy to assist in hundreds of cold cases, with more than 
half generating viable leads. April Tinsley's case was one of the early successes that proved the technique's value.
