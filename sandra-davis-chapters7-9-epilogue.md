## CHAPTER 7: THE MATCH

For forty years, Sandra Kaye Davis's case file sat in the Georgia Bureau of Investigation's records. The evidence—blood samples, rape kit materials, clothing fibers—remained preserved in a temperature-controlled vault. Every few years, someone would pull the file. Run the tests again. Compare the DNA profile against databases that grew larger by the day.

Every few years, the answer came back the same: *No match.*

But the GBI Cold Case Unit never stopped trying.

DNA technology evolved slowly at first, then exponentially. What required a blood sample the size of a quarter in 1990 could be done with a speck invisible to the naked eye by 2020. Degraded samples that once yielded nothing suddenly became readable. Databases that held thousands of profiles now held millions.

In 2024, forty years after Sandra's murder, the GBI assigned a new team to review the case. They used the latest extraction methods on the 1984 rape kit—technology that could pull DNA from samples that had sat in storage for decades. They amplified genetic markers that earlier tests couldn't detect. They built a profile more complete than any previous analysis.

And then they ran it through every database they had access to.

This time, something was different.

---

The investigation into Freddie Lee Granger Jr. began quietly. When DNA analysis produces a potential match, investigators don't rush to make arrests. They build cases. They verify. They dig.

Granger was sixty-eight years old in 2025. He'd been living in Ware County for decades—possibly his entire life. Public records showed an address not far from where Sandra's body had been found in 1984. He'd been twenty-two years old that September. The same age as his victim.

The GBI began assembling everything they could find on Granger. Criminal history, if it existed. Employment records. Known associates. Places he'd lived. Anyone who might remember him from 1984.

Georgia's CODIS database—the Combined DNA Index System—had been operational since the late 1990s, collecting DNA profiles from convicted offenders. If Granger had been arrested for a serious crime after that point, his DNA would be on file. But there was no indication that's how the match occurred.

More likely, the breakthrough came from advances in analyzing the original 1984 evidence. Modern DNA testing can recover profiles from samples that seemed useless decades ago. The rape kit preserved from Sandra's case had sat in storage for forty-two years. In that time, the science of reading genetic material had transformed completely.

The GBI didn't announce their work. Cold cases are solved quietly, painstakingly, with months of investigation before anyone learns a suspect's name.

---

In late 2025 or early 2026, the evidence became irrefutable.

The DNA profile from Sandra's rape kit matched Freddie Lee Granger Jr. The statistical probability of error was vanishingly small—one in trillions. The kind of match that holds up in court. The kind that ends cold cases.

Investigators began preparing the arrest. They coordinated with the Waycross Police Department, the Ware County Sheriff's Office, and Georgia's Department of Community Supervision. They reviewed everything one more time: the 1984 crime scene photos, the autopsy report, the witness statements (few though they were), the chain of custody for every piece of evidence.

They consulted with prosecutors in the Waycross Judicial Circuit District Attorney's Office. What charges would the evidence support? Rape—absolutely. Aggravated assault—yes. Murder—malice murder and felony murder, the latter because Sandra died during the commission of another felony.

In Georgia, malice murder carries a sentence of life in prison or death. Felony murder, the same. Granger was sixty-eight years old. He'd been free for forty-two years while Sandra's family waited for answers.

Now, finally, that freedom was about to end.

---

Granger's background, as far as public records reveal, remains mostly opaque. The GBI has not disclosed whether he had a prior criminal history. If he did, it likely wasn't serious enough to land his DNA in the system before the match was made. Or perhaps he was simply never caught.

There are three main ways cold cases get solved through DNA:

**Direct database match.** A person commits a crime, gets arrested, has their DNA taken and entered into CODIS. Years later, that profile matches evidence from an old case. This works when the suspect has been arrested for something else.

**Genetic genealogy.** Investigators upload DNA from the crime scene to public genealogy databases like GEDmatch. They build family trees, narrow down potential relatives, then identify a suspect. This method famously caught the Golden State Killer in 2018 and has since solved hundreds of cold cases.

**Re-analysis of old evidence.** Better technology allows DNA to be extracted and profiled from evidence that was previously unusable. Then that profile is compared against existing databases—or used to obtain a direct sample from a suspect once identified.

For Granger, the exact method hasn't been made public. But the GBI's press release emphasized "continued DNA testing," suggesting the breakthrough came from improved analysis of the original 1984 evidence rather than genetic genealogy or a new arrest.

However it happened, the result was the same: After forty-two years, the man who killed Sandra Kaye Davis had a name.

---

Waycross is a small city. In 2026, its population was around 14,000—even smaller than it had been in 1984. The kind of place where people remember major crimes for generations.

Sandra's murder had never been forgotten. Older residents recalled the shock of that September day in 1984. Younger ones heard the story from parents and grandparents: *A young woman, just twenty-two, found strangled beside a home on Kollock Street. The killer was never caught.*

For decades, the case was a wound that never healed. A reminder that sometimes, evil goes unpunished.

Now, in early 2026, detectives knew they were about to reopen that wound—but this time, with the promise of closure.

Granger had been living among them for decades. He'd walked the same streets as Sandra's family. He'd gone to work, come home, lived his life while Sandra's family marked anniversaries and birthdays she'd never see. He'd been *right there*.

Building the arrest case took time. Detectives wanted to ensure that when they took Granger into custody, the case was airtight. Defense attorneys would challenge the DNA—it was forty-two years old, after all. They'd question the chain of custody, the testing methods, the statistical analysis. Prosecutors needed to be ready.

In March 2026, everything was in place.

---

The decision was made: Freddie Lee Granger Jr. would be arrested on March 31, 2026—a Monday.

Forty-two years and seven months after Sandra's murder.

Forty-two years during which Granger, if guilty, had been free. He'd aged from twenty-two to sixty-eight. He'd lived an entire adult life. Sandra, frozen at twenty-two in death, had been denied all of it.

The GBI prepared its press release. The Waycross Police Department readied its officers. The Ware County Sheriff's Office coordinated logistics. The arrest would be swift, professional, and final.

In Waycross, a small town that had waited four decades for justice, the wait was almost over.

## CHAPTER 8: THE ARREST

March 31, 2026, started like any other Monday in Ware County, Georgia. Then the news broke.

Shortly after Freddie Lee Granger Jr. was taken into custody, the Georgia Bureau of Investigation issued a press release:

**"GBI Announces Arrest in the 1984 Rape and Murder of Sandra Kaye Davis in Ware County."**

The headline was stark, clinical. But for those who'd waited forty-two years, it was earth-shattering.

---

The arrest itself was coordinated among multiple agencies: the GBI, Waycross Police Department, Ware County Sheriff's Office, and Georgia's Department of Community Supervision. Cold case arrests are rarely dramatic—no car chases, no standoffs. Most of the time, officers simply knock on a door, present a warrant, and take the suspect into custody.

Granger was arrested at his residence in Ware County. He was sixty-eight years old, well past the age when most people pose a flight risk or physical threat. He'd been living in the area for years—possibly decades. There's no indication he tried to flee or resist.

He was processed quickly. Fingerprints. Mugshot. Paperwork. Then he was transported to the Ware County Jail, where he was booked on five felony charges:

- **One count of rape** — for the sexual assault of Sandra Kaye Davis
- **One count of aggravated assault** — for the violent attack preceding her death
- **One count of malice murder** — intentional killing with malice aforethought
- **Two counts of felony murder** — death occurring during the commission of rape and aggravated assault

Under Georgia law, malice murder and felony murder are treated separately, even when they arise from the same act. Prosecutors charge both to give juries options and to ensure the most serious consequences if convicted.

The penalties for these crimes are severe. Malice murder carries a sentence of life in prison without the possibility of parole—or death. Felony murder, the same. Rape and aggravated assault add additional decades.

In practice, given Granger's age, death penalty prosecution is unlikely. Juries are hesitant to sentence elderly defendants to death, and the appeals process can take twenty years or more. Life without parole is the most probable outcome if he's convicted.

But first, there would be a trial.

---

The GBI's press release was brief and factual:

*"On September 1, 1984, 22-year-old Sandra Kaye Davis was found strangled to death beside a home on Kollock Street in Waycross. The Waycross Police Department requested the GBI's assistance, and the investigation began immediately. Through continued DNA testing over the years, Freddie Lee Granger Jr., age 68, of Ware County, was identified as a suspect. On March 31, 2026, Granger was arrested and charged with one count of rape, one count of aggravated assault, one count of malice murder, and two counts of felony murder. He is currently in the Ware County Jail. The investigation is active and ongoing."*

Those five sentences represented forty-two years of work. Hundreds of hours of detective time. Decades of DNA testing. Generations of investigators who'd inherited the case and refused to let it die.

*Continued DNA testing over the years.*

That phrase carried weight. It meant that every time technology improved, someone had gone back to Sandra's case. Every time a new method became available, someone had run the tests again. The case had never been closed, never been forgotten.

And finally, the science had caught up.

---

Within hours, local news outlets began reporting the arrest.

**WJCL (Savannah):** *"GBI announces arrest more than 40 years after Waycross woman found strangled."*

**WALB (Albany, Georgia):** *"GBI arrests man in 1984 rape and murder of South Ga. woman."*

**News4JAX (Jacksonville):** *"Cold case solved: DNA links Ware County man to 1984 murder of young woman."*

The story spread through southeastern Georgia and northern Florida—the media markets closest to Waycross. National outlets picked it up briefly, a wire service story about another cold case solved by DNA. But for the people of Waycross, this wasn't just another headline. This was *their* cold case. *Their* unsolved murder. The one that had haunted their town for more than four decades.

The news traveled fast in a small community. People who remembered Sandra—or who'd heard the story from parents or grandparents—learned that the case had finally been solved. Some felt relief. Others felt anger. Why had it taken so long? How had Granger lived among them for forty-two years?

For Sandra's family, the emotions were complicated.

---

Little has been publicly disclosed about Sandra's surviving family. After forty-two years, some relatives may no longer be alive. Parents who were middle-aged in 1984 would be in their eighties or nineties—or gone. Siblings, if Sandra had any, would now be in their sixties or seventies.

The GBI's press release didn't name family members, and news coverage has so far respected their privacy. But the impact is easy to imagine.

For forty-two years, they'd lived without answers. They'd buried Sandra not knowing who killed her. They'd marked anniversaries—her birthday, the day she died—with the knowledge that somewhere, her killer was free.

They'd watched as other cold cases were solved. The Golden State Killer, caught in 2018 after forty years. Countless others solved by DNA in the 2010s and 2020s. Each one was a reminder: *Maybe ours will be next.*

And then, on March 31, 2026, it was.

The phone call from investigators. The explanation: *We have a match. We've made an arrest.* The tears—relief, grief, rage, exhaustion.

Justice, after forty-two years. But justice delayed so long it feels almost like cruelty.

---

In the days following Granger's arrest, more details began to emerge—though not many.

The GBI confirmed that the case file would be forwarded to the Waycross Judicial Circuit District Attorney's Office for prosecution. The DA's office, in turn, would begin preparing for trial. That process can take months or even years in a case this old and this complex.

Granger's arraignment would come first—a court appearance where charges are formally read and a plea is entered. Then pretrial motions: defense attorneys challenging the DNA evidence, the chain of custody, the testing methods. Prosecutors filing responses, presenting expert witnesses, building their case.

If the case goes to trial (and it probably will, given the severity of the charges), a jury will hear testimony about DNA science, 1984 crime scene procedures, and the decades-long investigation. They'll see photos of Sandra, young and smiling, frozen in time at twenty-two. They'll see photos of Granger, now sixty-eight, old enough to be a grandfather.

And they'll be asked to decide: Does the DNA evidence, forty-two years old, prove beyond a reasonable doubt that this man committed rape and murder?

The science says yes. Statistics put the probability of a false match at one in trillions. But defense attorneys will argue. They'll raise doubts about degraded samples, contamination, storage conditions.

Still, DNA evidence has an extraordinary track record in court. When properly handled and analyzed, it's nearly irrefutable.

Granger's conviction is likely. The only question is when—and what sentence he'll receive.

---

Public reaction to the arrest has been muted but significant.

In Waycross, people are processing the news. Some wonder if they knew Granger. Did they see him at the grocery store? Pass him on the street? Sit near him at church? The idea that a murderer could live quietly among them for decades is deeply unsettling.

Others focus on the triumph of the investigation. The GBI's persistence. The fact that justice, however delayed, finally arrived.

On social media, true crime communities have taken notice. The case is fresh—only one day old as a public story. Reddit threads dissect the details. YouTube channels prep videos. Podcast hosts reach out to Georgia journalists for information.

But the case is still developing. Granger hasn't entered a plea. A trial date hasn't been set. The full story—the *why* and the *how*—hasn't been told yet.

For now, there are only the facts: Sandra Kaye Davis was murdered in 1984. Forty-two years later, Freddie Lee Granger Jr. was arrested. DNA evidence linked him to the crime.

Everything else is still ahead.

## CHAPTER 9: JUSTICE AFTER 42 YEARS

Forty-two years is longer than most people spend building a career. It's longer than many marriages last. It's more than half of the average American lifespan.

Forty-two years ago, the internet didn't exist for public use. Cell phones were the size of bricks and cost thousands of dollars. DNA testing was in its infancy. The Soviet Union still existed. Ronald Reagan was president.

Forty-two years ago, Sandra Kaye Davis was murdered. And for forty-two years, her family waited.

Now, in April 2026, they finally have an answer.

---

The arrest of Freddie Lee Granger Jr. represents more than just the resolution of one cold case. It's a testament to persistence, technology, and the refusal to let time erase the truth.

The Georgia Bureau of Investigation never closed Sandra's case. Every few years, as DNA technology improved, investigators revisited the evidence. They tested, re-tested, and tested again. They waited for science to catch up to justice.

And it did.

In the 1980s, DNA testing required large blood samples and could only establish general matches. By the 1990s, PCR (polymerase chain reaction) allowed smaller samples to be amplified and analyzed. In the 2000s, databases like CODIS began linking old evidence to new suspects. In the 2010s, touch DNA and trace analysis made it possible to pull profiles from the smallest biological samples. In the 2020s, advanced extraction methods revived cases that had seemed hopeless.

Sandra's case benefited from every one of these advances. The rape kit collected in 1984—carefully stored and preserved—became the key to solving her murder forty-two years later.

This is what modern forensic science can do. It can reach back through decades and pull the truth out of the past.

---

The news of Granger's arrest reverberated through Waycross.

For older residents, Sandra's murder was a memory they'd carried for decades. The young woman found strangled beside a home on Kollock Street. The unsolved case that became part of the town's history. The reminder that sometimes, killers go free.

Now, they had closure. The case was solved. The DNA matched. Justice—slow, grinding, four decades delayed—had finally arrived.

For younger residents, the case was a revelation. Many hadn't known about Sandra's murder. It had happened before they were born, and cold cases don't always stay in the public consciousness. But now, learning the story—and learning it had been solved—they felt a connection to the past.

And they felt something else: relief that science works. That persistence matters. That even after forty-two years, the truth can come out.

---

The community's reaction was largely one of gratitude toward law enforcement.

The GBI had kept the case alive for four decades. Detectives who'd worked on it in 1984 had retired or passed away, but new investigators picked up the work. The case file was never archived and forgotten. It was active, ongoing, waiting.

The Waycross Police Department, too, deserved credit. They'd requested the GBI's help in 1984 and had continued to support the investigation over the years. Small-town police departments don't always have the resources for long-term cold case work, but they'd never given up.

And then there was the technology itself—CODIS, modern DNA testing, forensic labs capable of extracting profiles from decades-old samples.

All of it came together on March 31, 2026, when Freddie Lee Granger Jr. was arrested.

For Sandra's family, the arrest was both relief and agony.

Relief, because they finally knew. For forty-two years, they'd wondered: *Who killed Sandra? Why? Would he ever be caught?* Now, they had a name. They had answers—or at least, the beginning of answers.

But also agony, because forty-two years is too long.

Some family members may have died without seeing this day. Parents who buried their daughter in 1984 and spent the rest of their lives wondering. Siblings who grew old waiting for justice. Friends who moved away, started families, built lives—but never forgot the young woman whose life was stolen.

And there's the cruelty of the timeline itself: Sandra was twenty-two when she died. Granger—if guilty—has now lived forty-two years beyond that. He's had more than four decades of freedom while Sandra's family grieved. He's aged into his late sixties while Sandra remained forever twenty-two.

Justice delayed is justice denied, the saying goes. But in cold cases, delayed justice is often the only justice possible.

---

Sandra Kaye Davis's case is now the longest cold case in this series.

Longer than Sharon Johnson's sixteen-year wait. Longer than Sherri Rasmussen's twenty-three years. Longer than Kim Thomas's twenty-eight years. Even longer than Reesa Fletcher's thirty-two years.

Forty-two years from crime to arrest.

It's a staggering length of time. But it's not unprecedented. The Golden State Killer was arrested in 2018, forty years after his last known attack. In 2021, DNA solved the 1972 murder of Rita Curran—forty-nine years later. In 2022, a Pennsylvania man was arrested for a 1975 murder—forty-seven years after the crime.

DNA technology has made these cases solvable. And as technology continues to improve, even older cases may be solved.

There are still thousands of cold cases in the United States. Murders from the 1960s, 70s, and 80s, sitting unsolved. Families waiting, just as Sandra's did.

Sandra's case proves that the wait isn't hopeless.

---

The next phase of the case will be the trial.

Freddie Lee Granger Jr. is currently in the Ware County Jail, awaiting court proceedings. He'll be arraigned, where he'll enter a plea. If he pleads not guilty (and he likely will—the charges carry life sentences), the case will proceed to trial.

That trial will be complex. The crime occurred in 1984, meaning witnesses may have died or their memories faded. Physical evidence is forty-two years old. The defense will challenge the DNA, the testing methods, the chain of custody.

But the prosecution has science on its side. DNA evidence, when properly handled, is extraordinarily reliable. The statistical probability of a false match is negligible.

A conviction is likely. And with it, a sentence of life in prison.

Granger is sixty-eight years old. If convicted, he'll likely die in prison. There will be no forty-two more years of freedom.

For Sandra's family, that will be some measure of justice. Not closure, exactly—nothing can bring Sandra back—but accountability. The person who took her life will finally face consequences.

The case also carries a broader message: Cold cases are not hopeless.

For families waiting for answers, Sandra's story is proof that persistence and technology can eventually deliver justice. Cases that seemed impossible to solve in 1984 are solvable in 2026.

For law enforcement, it's a reminder to preserve evidence carefully and to revisit old cases as technology improves. What couldn't be tested in 1990 might be testable in 2020. What couldn't be matched in 2010 might match in 2030.

And for would-be criminals, it's a warning: DNA doesn't forget. Time doesn't erase evidence. Even if you evade justice for decades, science might still catch you.

But justice arrived.

## EPILOGUE

The case is no longer cold. The investigation that spanned four decades, outlasted multiple generations of detectives, and waited for science to evolve—it's finally over.

Freddie Lee Granger Jr. sits in the Ware County Jail, charged with rape, aggravated assault, and murder. The trial is months away. The full story—the details of what happened on September 1, 1984, and how Granger evaded justice for so long—will be told in a courtroom.

But the outcome is no longer in doubt. DNA evidence has given Sandra a voice she lost forty-two years ago.

---

Sandra Kaye Davis was twenty-two years old when her life was taken. She'd barely begun adulthood. There were so many things she'd never get to do: travel, build a career, fall in love, start a family, grow old.

Science and persistence can bring justice.

And no matter how much time passes, the truth doesn't disappear.
