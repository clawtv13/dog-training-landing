### 6. SHOW vs. TELL: **6.0/10** (C)

**Definition:** Dramatization vs. exposition, concrete vs. abstract, active vs. passive

**ASSESSMENT:**

This is the manuscript's weakest craft element. The writing is **heavily expository** with minimal dramatization.

**SHOW vs. TELL RATIO ESTIMATE:**
- **Telling (exposition):** 85%
- **Showing (scenes):** 15%

**BREAKDOWN:**

**TELLING (Exposition-Heavy Sections):**

❌ **Example 1: Chapter 1**
> "Sandra was one of them. Born in Waycross, raised in Waycross, still in Waycross at twenty-two. Whether that was by choice or circumstance, no one can say now. But she was there, part of the fabric of a small southern town where everyone knew everyone and nothing much changed year to year."

*This is ALL telling. We're told Sandra was "part of the fabric" but not shown how.*

**BETTER (Show):**
> "Sandra waved to Mrs. Henderson every morning on her way to work, the same wave Sandra's mother had given Mrs. Henderson for thirty years. Small towns worked like that."

❌ **Example 2: Chapter 4**
> "The first year was the hardest. September 1, 1985 marked exactly one year since Sandra Kaye Davis was found strangled beside a home on Kollock Street."

*Summary/telling rather than scene.*

**BETTER (Show):**
> "David stood at Sandra's grave on September 1, 1985. One year. The flowers he'd brought last week were already wilted in the Georgia heat. He added the new ones anyway—daisies, like she'd always preferred. Or had she? God, it was already getting hard to remember."

❌ **Example 3: DNA Explanation**
> "DNA testing in the mid-1980s required large samples and often took months to process. Even when results came back, they weren't always conclusive."

*Pure exposition. Necessary but undramatized.*

**SHOWING (Dramatized Scenes):**

✅ **Example 1: Prologue Opening**
> "The morning of September 1, 1984, arrived quiet in Waycross, Georgia. Sunrise painted the Spanish moss gold. Church bells would ring soon—it was Saturday, and the small city of twenty-two thousand souls would ease into Labor Day weekend the way southern towns always had: slow, familiar, safe."

*This SHOWS. Sensory detail, scene-setting, atmosphere.*

✅ **Example 2: The Arrest**
> "Granger answered in a bathrobe, blinking in the porch light, disoriented. Reed held up the arrest warrant. 'Freddie Lee Granger Jr., you're under arrest for the rape and murder of Sandra Kaye Davis on September 1, 1984.'"

*Concrete action, dialogue, visual detail.*

✅ **Example 3: David's Resignation**
> "'Detective, I appreciate what you're trying to do,' David said. 'But my parents died waiting. Two of my sisters died waiting. I'm the last one left in Waycross who remembers Sandra the way she was. I've made peace with never knowing.'"

*Dialogue dramatizing emotion rather than stating "David was exhausted."*

**SENSORY DETAIL AUDIT:**

**Sight:** Adequate (crime scene descriptions, Georgia setting)  
**Sound:** Minimal (church bells mentioned, cicadas once, mostly absent)  
**Smell:** Almost none (missed opportunity: "the September heat," "swamp humidity")  
**Taste:** None  
**Touch:** Minimal (heat mentioned but not felt)

**FILTER WORDS:**
The manuscript occasionally uses distancing filter words:

⚠️ "Detectives *felt* the pressure"  
→ BETTER: "The pressure bore down on every detective."

⚠️ "David *realized* he might never know"  
→ BETTER: Show the realization through action/dialogue

**PASSIVE VOICE:**
Generally well-controlled, but some examples:

⚠️ "Sandra was killed" (repeated phrase)  
→ BETTER: Mix in active alternatives: "Someone strangled Sandra" / "The killer left her beside..."

**VERDICT:** The manuscript is exposition-heavy, which is partially unavoidable given:
1. Breaking news format (limited information to dramatize)
2. 42-year timespan (hard to show decades of waiting)
3. Focus on procedural investigation (inherently expository)

However, more scenes could be constructed:
- Reconstruct Sandra's last day with "might have" framing
- Show family vigils/anniversaries
- Dramatize detective frustration during failed DNA tests
- Show Granger's arrest in real-time scene

**GRADING CONTEXT:**  
For a traditional narrative: **4/10 (Weak)**  
For breaking-news true crime: **6/10 (Acceptable)**

### 2. **Exposition-Heavy / Show vs. Tell Imbalance**
**Severity:** MODERATE (partially format-driven)

**Problem:**  
Approximately 85% of the manuscript is exposition (telling) rather than dramatized scenes (showing). This makes the reading experience feel like a Wikipedia article or news report rather than immersive narrative nonfiction.

**Examples:**

❌ **Telling:**
> "The investigation entered a frustrating phase where every lead seemed promising until it wasn't."

✅ **Showing:**
> "Detective Huxford circled the address on the tip sheet—the third anonymous call this week claiming to know who killed Sandra. He drove to the address, knocked on the door, and learned the caller was a paranoid schizophrenic who 'knew' who killed JFK too. He crossed it off the list."

**Impact on Reader:**  
The manuscript reads more like a report than a story. It's informative but not immersive.

**Solution:**
- Reconstruct more scenes (even with "might have" framing)
- Show family vigils
- Dramatize detective frustration during DNA failures
- Show Granger's arrest in real-time scene (currently summarized)
- Add sensory detail (heat, sounds, smells of Georgia)

**Example Reconstruction:**
> **Current (Tell):**
> "Sandra woke up that morning. Ate breakfast, maybe. Got dressed."

### 3. **"42 Years" Repetition Overload**
**Severity:** LOW (but noticeable)

**Problem:**  
The phrase "forty-two years" (and variants) appears **67 times** in 16,082 words. That's once every 240 words.

While the 42-year timeline is the *central* theme of the case, the repetition becomes a verbal tic. Examples:

- "Forty-two years after Sandra's death"
- "Forty-two years later"
- "Forty-two years of waiting"
- "Forty-two years, six months, and thirty days"
- "For forty-two years, Sandra's family waited"

**Impact on Reader:**  
By the end, the phrase loses impact through overuse. It becomes expected rather than powerful.

**Solution:**  
Vary the phrasing:
- "More than four decades"
- "Since 1984" (use the year more)
- "After such a long wait"
- "Over half Sandra's life, then double it"
- "Long enough for a child to grow up, have children of their own"

Use "42 years" strategically for impact (Prologue, Epilogue, key emotional beats) and vary elsewhere.
