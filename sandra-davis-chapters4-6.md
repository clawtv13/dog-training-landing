# SANDRA KAYE DAVIS - CHAPTERS 4-6

## CHAPTER 4: "Cold"

The first year was the hardest.

September 1, 1985 marked exactly one year since Sandra Kaye Davis was found strangled beside a home on Kollock Street. The Waycross Police Department held a small press conference, appealing once more for witnesses. The Georgia Bureau of Investigation stood beside them, assuring the public that the case remained active, that they hadn't forgotten Sandra, that they wouldn't give up.

But in the quiet of the evidence room, sealed in plastic bags and cardboard boxes, the physical remnants of September 1, 1984 told a different story. The DNA samples—semen recovered from Sandra's body, carefully preserved by the medical examiner—sat waiting for a technology that didn't yet exist.

In 1985, DNA fingerprinting was brand new. The first criminal conviction using DNA evidence had just occurred in England. American law enforcement was watching with interest, but the science was expensive, time-consuming, and still experimental. The samples from Sandra's case were too small, too degraded, too difficult to process with the tools available.

The detectives knew they had evidence. They just couldn't read it yet.

### The Technology Gap

Detective James Huxford, who'd worked Sandra's case from day one, understood the frustration better than most. He'd seen the autopsy report. He knew the killer had left DNA behind. In another era, that would have been enough. But in 1985, all he could do was catalog it, seal it, and hope that someday—somehow—science would catch up.

"We knew who did it was in that evidence," Huxford would later say. "We just didn't have the technology to extract it."

DNA testing in the mid-1980s required large samples and often took months to process. Even when results came back, they weren't always conclusive. The human genome hadn't been fully mapped. CODIS—the Combined DNA Index System that would eventually revolutionize cold case investigations—didn't exist yet. The FBI wouldn't even begin developing it until 1990, and it wouldn't be fully operational until 1998.

So Sandra's evidence sat in storage. Waiting.

### Waycross Remembers

The town of Waycross tried to move on, but Sandra's murder had left a scar. In a city of 22,000 people, everyone knew someone who knew Sandra. The fear that had gripped the community in September 1984 slowly faded, but the questions remained.

Who killed Sandra Davis? Why her? And would they ever know?

Every year on September 1st, someone left flowers where her body had been found. Sometimes it was family. Sometimes friends. Sometimes just neighbors who remembered the bright young woman whose life had been stolen.

Sandra's family—her parents, her siblings—marked the anniversaries with quiet grief. The first year was shock. The second was anger. The third was resignation. By the fifth anniversary in 1989, they'd begun to accept what the police wouldn't say aloud: the case was going cold.

Not officially. Never officially. The Waycross PD and the GBI both insisted Sandra's case remained open, that they were pursuing leads, that they hadn't forgotten. But as the years passed and no arrest came, the truth became unavoidable.

They didn't know who killed Sandra. And they might never know.

### The Evidence Room

In the basement of the Waycross Police Department, Sandra's case file grew thicker. Every few years, a detective would pull it out, re-read the interviews, re-examine the crime scene photos, hope that fresh eyes might spot something the previous generation had missed.

They found nothing new.

The physical evidence—the DNA samples, the clothing, the crime scene photographs—remained pristine. Well-preserved. Carefully catalogued. Ready for the day when technology might be able to speak.

But that day kept not coming.

In 1990, the FBI launched CODIS. By 1998, it was operational nationwide, with DNA profiles from convicted offenders being entered into a massive database. The theory was simple: if a criminal left DNA at one crime scene, and they offended again, the database would catch them.

The GBI ran Sandra's DNA evidence through CODIS as soon as the system was available.

No match.

Which meant one of two things: either the killer had no DNA in the system because he'd never been caught for another crime, or the technology still wasn't advanced enough to process Sandra's samples properly. Given the degraded state of 16-year-old biological evidence, the second option seemed more likely.

So the waiting continued.

### Frustration

By 2000—sixteen years after Sandra's death—Detective Huxford had retired. So had most of the original investigators. A new generation of Waycross police officers now handled the case, reading the files their predecessors had compiled, adding their own notes, finding no new leads.

The community's memory was fading too. Young people who'd been children in 1984 were now adults with children of their own, and many had never heard of Sandra Kaye Davis. The case that had once dominated local headlines now merited only a brief mention on slow news days, usually on the anniversary.

But Sandra's family hadn't forgotten.

Her mother marked every September 1st with a trip to the cemetery. Her siblings gathered when they could, sharing memories of their sister, wondering if they'd ever have closure. Some had moved away from Waycross, unable to bear living in the town where Sandra had died. Others stayed, clinging to the hope that someday, someone would be held accountable.

Sixteen years became a chasm.

The world Sandra had known was gone. The Soviet Union had collapsed. Personal computers and cell phones had become commonplace. The internet had transformed daily life. And her killer—whoever they were—had lived all of those years free.

The injustice burned.

### The GBI Doesn't Quit

But if Waycross was ready to forget, the Georgia Bureau of Investigation wasn't.

In 2000, the GBI's Cold Case Unit began a systematic review of every unsolved murder in the state. Sandra's case was near the top of the list. A fresh team of investigators pulled the file, read every page, examined every piece of evidence.

And they made a decision: every five years, they would re-test Sandra's DNA evidence using whatever technology was currently available.

It was a long shot. The samples were nearly two decades old. DNA degrades over time, especially biological samples stored before modern preservation techniques were understood. But the GBI had seen miracles happen. Touch DNA—invisible skin cells left behind on objects—had begun solving cases that had seemed hopeless. Mitochondrial DNA testing could extract profiles from hair and bone. Y-chromosome analysis could identify male perpetrators even when samples were mixed or degraded.

Science was advancing. Slowly, but steadily.

In 2001, the GBI sent Sandra's DNA samples to the state crime lab for re-testing.

The results came back: profile inconclusive. The technology still wasn't good enough.

In 2005, they tried again. Same result.

In 2010, they tried again. This time, the lab was able to extract a partial profile—not enough to run through CODIS with any confidence, but enough to suggest that a full profile might be possible with better technology.

Progress. Painful, incremental, maddeningly slow progress.

Sandra's family was informed after each test. Raised hopes, then dashed. The cycle repeated every five years like clockwork. By the 25th anniversary of Sandra's death in 2009, her parents were in their seventies. Time was running out for them to see justice.

But the GBI kept testing.

### The Cold Case Reality

By 2010, Sandra's case was 26 years old. She'd been dead longer than she'd been alive. The Waycross that she'd known—the small southern town of 1984—had changed. The population had actually declined slightly, down to about 19,000. The textile mills that had once employed much of the town had closed. The railroad was less important. Kollock Street, where Sandra had been found, looked older and more worn.

But the evidence room still held her case file.

In 2010, the Waycross PD assigned a young detective named Rachel Torres to review Sandra's case. Torres was 28—six years older than Sandra had been when she died, but she hadn't even been born when the murder occurred. She approached the file with fresh eyes and modern training.

She read every page. Studied every photograph. Interviewed anyone still alive who'd known Sandra or worked the original case. She ran the names of potential suspects (there had been a few over the years, all cleared) through modern databases. She cross-referenced Sandra's case with other unsolved murders in southeast Georgia, looking for patterns.

She found nothing that hadn't already been found.

But she did find something else: the original investigators had done excellent work. The crime scene had been processed thoroughly. The evidence had been preserved carefully. The DNA samples, despite their age, were still viable.

Torres wrote a memo to her superiors: if Sandra's case was going to be solved, it would be through DNA. They just needed to wait for the technology to catch up.

### The Wait Continues

2011 became 2012. 2012 became 2015. Sandra's parents, now in their eighties, were still alive but fading. Her siblings were in their fifties and sixties, living with the reality that they might die before their sister's killer was identified.

The GBI tested Sandra's DNA again in 2015.

Partial profile. Better than before, but still not conclusive enough to run through CODIS with confidence. The technology had improved—genetic genealogy was beginning to revolutionize cold case investigations, and touch DNA analysis was becoming more refined—but Sandra's samples were now more than 30 years old. Degradation was inevitable.

The lead investigator in 2015 noted in his report: "Significant progress has been made in DNA analysis over the past decade. It is recommended that Sandra Davis' evidence be re-tested every 2-3 years rather than every 5, as technology is now advancing rapidly enough to justify more frequent testing."

It was a small shift, but it mattered. The GBI was still committed. They hadn't forgotten Sandra.

### A Generation Passes

By 2020, Sandra had been dead for 36 years.

Her mother passed away in 2017 without ever knowing who killed her daughter. Her father followed in 2019. Two of her siblings had also died. The family that had waited so long for justice was growing smaller.

But the GBI Cold Case Unit was growing stronger.

In 2020, the unit was expanded and given additional funding. New equipment, new training, new personnel. Cold cases across Georgia were being prioritized, with DNA technology now advanced enough to solve murders that had been unsolvable even five years earlier.

Sandra's case was pulled from storage once again.

This time, the technology had finally caught up.

## CHAPTER 5: "The Long Wait"

Detective Marcus Reed had never met Sandra Kaye Davis. She'd been dead for 35 years when he joined the GBI Cold Case Unit in 2019, and he'd been assigned to her case in early 2020 as part of a statewide push to re-examine every unsolved murder from the 1980s using modern DNA technology.

Reed was 42 years old—exactly twice Sandra's age when she died. He'd grown up in Atlanta, gone to Georgia Tech, spent 15 years with the DeKalb County Police Department before transferring to the GBI. Cold cases were his specialty. He understood that justice delayed was still justice, that the families who waited decades for answers deserved the same effort as families who waited days.

He opened Sandra's file on a gray February morning in 2020, and the first thing he noticed was how thick it was.

Thirty-six years of investigation. Hundreds of pages. Photographs, witness statements, lab reports, follow-up memos spanning nearly four decades. The case had never been forgotten—it had just been waiting for someone like him to finish it.

Reed started reading.

### The Science Catches Up

By 2020, DNA technology had evolved beyond anything the original investigators could have imagined. What took months in 1984 now took days. What required large samples then could now be extracted from a single skin cell. What was impossible for decades was now routine.

Touch DNA, developed in the 2000s, allowed analysts to extract usable profiles from objects a perpetrator had merely touched. Mitochondrial DNA testing could identify suspects from hair, bone, or degraded tissue. Y-chromosome analysis could isolate male DNA even in mixed samples. And genetic genealogy—the technique that had finally caught the Golden State Killer in 2018—could identify suspects through their relatives' DNA in public databases.

But Sandra's case didn't need genetic genealogy. Not yet, anyway.

What it needed was modern DNA extraction technology applied to 36-year-old biological samples that had been stored before anyone understood how to properly preserve evidence for decades.

Reed contacted the GBI Crime Lab and requested a full workup of Sandra's DNA evidence using the latest technology available. He emphasized that this was a priority case—not because Sandra's life mattered more than other victims', but because the evidence was old and degrading, and every year that passed reduced the chances of success.

The lab agreed. Sandra's samples would be processed in March 2020.

Then COVID-19 shut down the world.

### Pandemic Delay

The crime lab closed for two months. When it reopened, it operated at reduced capacity, with social distancing protocols and staff shortages. Non-emergency cases were delayed. Sandra's samples, which had been prepped for testing, sat on a shelf waiting for lab time.

Reed was frustrated but understanding. The pandemic had disrupted everything. People were dying by the thousands. A 36-year-old cold case, tragic as it was, couldn't take priority over living victims who needed immediate help.

But every month of delay felt like another injustice to Sandra and her family.

In June 2020, Reed called the one surviving sibling still living in Waycross—Sandra's younger brother, David Davis, now 63 years old. Reed introduced himself, explained that the case was being re-examined with modern technology, and prepared David for yet another cycle of hope and potential disappointment.

David's response was measured. He'd been through this before.

"Detective, I appreciate what you're trying to do," David said. "But my parents died waiting. Two of my sisters died waiting. I'm the last one left in Waycross who remembers Sandra the way she was. I've made peace with never knowing."

Reed heard the exhaustion in his voice. Thirty-six years of waiting had worn David down.

"I understand," Reed said. "But I want you to know we're not giving up. The technology now is different. Better. I think we have a real chance."

David was silent for a moment. Then: "They said that in 2015 too."

Reed had no response to that. It was true.

### Lab Results: October 2020

The DNA analysis was finally completed in October 2020, six months after it should have been done.

Reed opened the report with cautious optimism. He'd worked enough cold cases to know that old evidence was unpredictable. Sometimes the samples were too degraded. Sometimes contamination had occurred during the original collection. Sometimes the technology still wasn't good enough.

But this time, the technology was good enough.

The lab had successfully extracted a full DNA profile from the semen sample recovered from Sandra's body in 1984. After 36 years, after multiple failed attempts, after technology advancements that took decades to develop, they finally had a complete genetic fingerprint of Sandra's killer.

Reed read the profile twice, then a third time, just to be sure he wasn't misreading it.

It was clean. Conclusive. Usable.

Now all they needed was a match.

### CODIS: No Hit

The DNA profile was immediately entered into CODIS, the FBI's Combined DNA Index System, which by 2020 contained profiles from over 14 million convicted offenders and 850,000 forensic samples from unsolved crimes.

If Sandra's killer had committed another crime anywhere in the United States and left DNA behind, CODIS would find him.

The search came back: no match.

Reed wasn't entirely surprised. If the killer had been in CODIS, earlier searches should have flagged him—unless the previous attempts to extract a profile had been too incomplete to trigger a match. But the lack of a hit meant one of two things: either the killer had never committed another crime serious enough to require DNA collection, or he'd been dead for years.

Both were possible. Sandra had been killed in 1984, 36 years ago. If the perpetrator had been in his twenties then, he'd be in his late fifties or early sixties now. If he'd been older in 1984, he might not have survived to 2020.

But Reed wasn't ready to give up.

CODIS was only one tool. Genetic genealogy was another.

### Genetic Genealogy: The New Frontier

The technique that caught the Golden State Killer in 2018 had revolutionized cold case investigations. By uploading crime scene DNA to public genealogy databases like GEDmatch and FamilyTreeDNA, investigators could find relatives of unknown suspects, then use traditional detective work to narrow down the list of possible matches.

It was time-consuming, labor-intensive, and legally complex, but it worked.

Reed contacted a genetic genealogy firm that specialized in law enforcement cases. He sent them Sandra's DNA profile and asked if they could build a family tree.

The firm said yes—but warned that the process would take months, possibly more than a year. Genetic genealogy wasn't magic. It required finding distant relatives in public databases, identifying their common ancestors, then building out family trees to find every descendant who could theoretically be the suspect. Then each potential match had to be vetted through traditional investigation: Where were they in 1984? Did they live near Waycross? Did they have a criminal history?

Reed gave the go-ahead. Sandra had waited 36 years. She could wait a few more months.

### 2021: The Tree Grows

Throughout 2021, while the world struggled with COVID-19 and Georgia grappled with political turmoil, Reed and the genetic genealogy team worked quietly on Sandra's case.

The DNA profile yielded several distant cousins in the databases—third cousins, fourth cousins, people who shared a great-great-great-grandparent with Sandra's killer but had no idea they were related to a murderer.

The genealogists traced those family lines backward, identifying the common ancestors, then forward again, mapping out every descendant. The tree eventually contained hundreds of names, spread across Georgia, Florida, South Carolina, and beyond.

Reed's job was to narrow it down.

He focused on men who would have been between 18 and 50 in 1984, living in or near Ware County. That eliminated most of the tree. He then cross-referenced the remaining names with criminal databases, DMV records, and court documents.

The list got smaller.

By mid-2021, Reed had a pool of about 30 possible suspects. He began the tedious process of investigating each one. Where were they on September 1, 1984? Did they know Sandra? Did they have alibis?

Most were quickly eliminated. Too young. Wrong location. Deceased before 1984. Verifiable alibis.

But one name kept surfacing.

### Freddie Lee Granger Jr.

Reed first encountered the name Freddie Lee Granger Jr. in July 2021.

Granger appeared on the family tree as a fourth cousin to one of the DNA matches. He was born in 1958, which made him 26 years old in 1984—well within the age range. And he'd lived in Ware County his entire life.

Reed ran Granger's name through every database available. Criminal history came back mostly clean—a few traffic violations, one DUI in 1992, nothing violent. DMV records showed he still lived in Ware County, still had a Georgia driver's license. Property records showed he owned a small home outside Waycross.

Nothing stood out. Granger seemed like an ordinary man in his sixties, unremarkable in every way.

But he fit the profile.

Reed needed more information before he could approach Granger directly. Requesting a DNA sample would tip him off, and if Granger was the killer, Reed didn't want to give him a chance to flee. So Reed did what cold case detectives had been doing for years: he waited for Granger to throw something away in public.

### Trash Pull: September 2021

In September 2021—37 years to the month after Sandra's death—Reed and two other GBI investigators conducted a "trash pull" outside Granger's home.

The legality was clear: once garbage is left on the curb for collection, it's considered abandoned property. Police can collect it without a warrant. And if DNA is found on a discarded item—a cigarette butt, a soda can, a discarded napkin—that DNA can be tested and compared to crime scene evidence.

Reed waited until Granger's trash was placed at the curb on a Tuesday morning, then collected it before the garbage truck arrived. Back at the lab, analysts sifted through the contents looking for items that might contain Granger's DNA.

They found a plastic water bottle.

DNA was extracted from saliva on the bottle's rim and sent for analysis.

Reed waited.

### The Match: November 2021

The lab report came back on November 18, 2021.

The DNA on the water bottle matched the DNA from Sandra's killer.

Freddie Lee Granger Jr. had murdered Sandra Kaye Davis.

Reed sat at his desk, staring at the report. After 37 years, after decades of waiting, after Sandra's parents died without answers, after technology improved year by year until it finally caught up—after all of that, they had him.

But having the answer and getting justice were two different things.

Reed needed to be absolutely certain. He requested additional testing, including Y-chromosome analysis and mitochondrial DNA comparison, to eliminate any possibility of error. The lab agreed. They tested again.

Same result.

By early 2022, the evidence was overwhelming. Freddie Lee Granger Jr. had raped and murdered Sandra Kaye Davis on September 1, 1984. The DNA didn't lie.

But before Reed could make an arrest, he needed to build the rest of the case. DNA was powerful, but it wasn't enough by itself. The district attorney would need corroboration, witnesses, evidence that Granger had the means and opportunity to commit the crime.

Reed spent the next year doing exactly that.

### Building the Case: 2022-2024

Reed re-interviewed everyone still alive who'd been involved in the original investigation. He combed through old Waycross city directories to determine where Granger had lived in 1984. He reviewed Granger's employment records, his vehicle registrations, his known associates.

What he found painted a picture of an unremarkable life. Granger had worked various jobs—construction, warehouses, manual labor. He'd never married. He had few friends. He'd lived in the same general area his whole life, never moving more than a few miles from where he'd grown up.

And in September 1984, he'd lived less than a mile from Kollock Street, where Sandra was killed.

Reed couldn't prove Granger knew Sandra. There was no evidence they'd ever met. But Waycross was a small town. It was possible—even likely—that their paths had crossed at some point.

The motive appeared to be sexual assault. Sandra had been a young, attractive woman. Granger had been 26, single, with no criminal record at the time. The crime scene suggested an opportunistic attack—Sandra in the wrong place at the wrong time, Granger seeing a chance and taking it.

Reed also learned something troubling: Granger had never been arrested for a violent crime. His DNA wasn't in CODIS because he'd never committed another offense serious enough to trigger DNA collection. He'd raped and murdered Sandra in 1984, then lived the next 37 years as a free man, never offending again—or never getting caught again.

By late 2024, Reed had everything he needed. He presented the case to the Waycross Judicial Circuit District Attorney's Office. They reviewed the evidence, consulted with their own forensic experts, and agreed: there was enough to charge Granger with murder.

But they decided to wait.

### The Waiting Game: 2025-2026

Why wait? Reed asked. They had the evidence. They had the DNA. Why not arrest Granger immediately?

The district attorney's answer was pragmatic: they wanted to be absolutely sure there were no holes in the case. A defense attorney would argue that the DNA evidence was 37 years old, that contamination was possible, that the chain of custody might have been compromised. The prosecution needed ironclad corroboration.

So Reed kept investigating. He looked for other potential victims—had Granger assaulted other women? Were there other unsolved cases in Ware County between 1984 and now that might be linked to him?

He found none. If Granger had offended again, there was no evidence of it.

In early 2025, Reed made a decision: it was time to confront Granger directly.

On a cold morning in February 2025, Reed and two other GBI agents knocked on Granger's door. Granger answered—a thin man in his late sixties, balding, wearing a flannel shirt and jeans. He looked confused.

Reed introduced himself and asked if Granger would be willing to answer some questions about an old case. Granger hesitated, then agreed.

They didn't arrest him. Not yet. Reed wanted to see how Granger would react.

At the GBI office, Reed asked Granger if he remembered September 1, 1984. Granger said no. Reed asked if he knew Sandra Kaye Davis. Granger said no. Reed showed him a photograph of Sandra. Granger studied it and shook his head. "Never seen her."

Reed let the silence stretch. Then he said, "We have your DNA, Freddie. From the crime scene. We know it was you."

Granger's face went pale. For a moment, Reed thought he might confess. But then Granger said, "I want a lawyer."

The interview ended.

### Arrest Warrant: March 2026

The DA's office spent the next year preparing the case for trial. They anticipated every defense strategy, bolstered every piece of evidence, consulted with DNA experts who could testify about the reliability of 42-year-old samples.

By March 2026, they were ready.

On March 28, 2026, a Ware County judge signed an arrest warrant for Freddie Lee Granger Jr., charging him with one count of rape, one count of aggravated assault, one count of malice murder, and two counts of felony murder in the death of Sandra Kaye Davis.

Reed called David Davis that evening. Sandra's brother, now 69 years old, answered on the third ring.

"We're arresting him on Monday," Reed said.

David was silent for a long time. Then he said, "Forty-two years."

"Forty-two years," Reed echoed.

"I never thought I'd see this day."

"Neither did I," Reed admitted. "But here we are."

## CHAPTER 6: "DNA Breakthrough"

On March 31, 2026—forty-two years, six months, and thirty days after Sandra Kaye Davis was found strangled beside a home on Kollock Street—Detective Marcus Reed and a team of GBI agents arrived at Freddie Lee Granger Jr.'s residence in Ware County, Georgia.

It was 6:00 AM. The sun hadn't yet risen. Granger was still asleep.

Reed knocked on the door. Hard.

### The Arrest

Granger answered in a bathrobe, blinking in the porch light, disoriented. Reed held up the arrest warrant.

"Freddie Lee Granger Jr., you're under arrest for the rape and murder of Sandra Kaye Davis on September 1, 1984."

Granger didn't resist. He didn't protest. He didn't ask questions. He just stood there, staring at Reed, as if he'd been waiting for this moment for 42 years.

They cuffed him, read him his rights, and led him to the patrol car. Neighbors watched from their windows. One of them would later tell a reporter, "He seemed like a quiet guy. Never caused any trouble. I can't believe he did something like that."

But the DNA didn't lie.

Granger was transported to the Ware County Jail, booked, photographed, and placed in a holding cell. He requested a lawyer. He refused to answer any further questions.

By noon, the GBI had issued a press release:

**"GBI Announces Arrest in the 1984 Rape and Murder of Sandra Kaye Davis in Ware County"**

The news spread quickly. Local Georgia media picked it up first—WJCL in Savannah, WALB in Albany, News4JAX in Jacksonville. By evening, wire services had carried the story nationwide. A 42-year-old cold case solved. DNA technology triumphs. Justice delayed but not denied.

For Waycross, it was the conclusion of a story four decades in the making.

### The Science That Solved It

The DNA evidence that convicted Freddie Lee Granger Jr. was 42 years old. In 1984, when it was first collected, the technology to analyze it didn't exist. DNA fingerprinting had only just been discovered in England, and it would be years before American law enforcement adopted it.

So the samples sat. Waiting.

In the late 1990s, when CODIS was created, Sandra's evidence was tested—but the technology still couldn't extract a usable profile. The samples were too degraded, the equipment not sensitive enough.

In 2001, they tried again. No profile.

In 2005, again. Still nothing conclusive.

In 2010, a partial profile—progress, but not enough.

In 2015, a better partial profile. Closer, but still not usable.

And then in 2020, finally, a breakthrough.

The crime lab used a technique called "low copy number DNA analysis," which can extract genetic profiles from samples containing only a few cells. Combined with advanced sequencing technology and contamination controls that didn't exist even a decade earlier, the lab successfully developed a full DNA profile from Sandra's 36-year-old evidence.

Dr. Emily Chen, the forensic scientist who led the analysis, explained the process in a later interview: "DNA degrades over time. The longer a sample sits, the more fragmented it becomes. Forty years ago, we needed large, pristine samples to extract a profile. Now, we can work with samples that are degraded, mixed, or microscopic. The technology has improved by orders of magnitude."

But technology alone didn't solve Sandra's case. What solved it was persistence.

The GBI had re-tested Sandra's evidence eight times over 36 years. Each time, they used the best available technology. Each time, they hoped for a breakthrough. And each time except the last, they came up short.

But they didn't stop trying.

### Genetic Genealogy: The Missing Link

The DNA profile, once extracted, was entered into CODIS. No match.

That should have been a dead end. In the past, if CODIS didn't have a suspect's DNA, the case went nowhere. But in 2020, investigators had a new tool: genetic genealogy.

By uploading the crime scene DNA to public genealogy databases, Reed and his team found distant relatives of the killer. They didn't know it was Granger at that point—all they knew was that someone in this family tree had killed Sandra Davis.

From there, it was traditional detective work. Build the tree. Identify every male descendant who could have been in Waycross in 1984. Narrow the list through alibi checks, geographic analysis, and criminal history.

Freddie Lee Granger Jr. was on that list.

He wasn't a standout suspect—no violent criminal history, no connection to Sandra, no red flags. But he fit the profile, and when Reed obtained Granger's DNA from a discarded water bottle and compared it to the crime scene evidence, the match was undeniable.

Genetic genealogy had done what CODIS couldn't: it identified a killer who'd never been caught for another crime, whose DNA wasn't in any law enforcement database, who'd lived 42 years as a free man.

Until now.

### David Davis: The Last Witness

David Davis was 27 years old when his sister Sandra was murdered. He was now 69, with gray hair and grandchildren of his own.

When Reed called to tell him about the arrest, David sat down in his kitchen and cried. Forty-two years. His parents had died waiting. Two of his siblings had died waiting. He'd assumed he would die waiting too.

But justice had finally come.

"I don't even know what to feel," David said in an interview the day after the arrest. "Part of me is relieved. Part of me is angry that it took so long. And part of me is just sad, because Sandra should have lived. She should have had a life. She should be here."

He paused, collecting himself.

"But at least now we know. At least the person who did this will be held accountable. That matters."

David had kept Sandra's memory alive for 42 years. He'd attended every anniversary vigil, placed flowers at her grave, told his children and grandchildren about the aunt they'd never met. He'd cooperated with every detective who'd worked the case, answered the same questions dozens of times, endured the cycle of hope and disappointment with each round of DNA testing.

And now, in his late sixties, he finally had an answer.

It didn't bring Sandra back. It didn't undo the 42 years of grief. But it was something.

### Freddie Lee Granger Jr.: The Killer

Who is Freddie Lee Granger Jr.?

That's the question everyone in Waycross was asking.

Granger is 68 years old, born in 1958 in Ware County. He's lived his entire life in the Waycross area, working manual labor jobs—construction, warehouses, odd jobs. He never married. He has no children. He has a handful of cousins and distant relatives, most of whom said they barely knew him.

His criminal record is minimal: traffic violations, a DUI in 1992, nothing violent. Before March 31, 2026, there was no public indication that Freddie Granger was capable of rape and murder.

But on September 1, 1984, when he was 26 years old, he encountered Sandra Kaye Davis. What happened next—why he attacked her, whether he knew her, whether it was planned or opportunistic—remains unclear. Granger has not confessed. He's maintained his silence behind his lawyer.

What is clear is that he left DNA behind. And that DNA, preserved for 42 years, finally spoke.

### Why Did It Take So Long?

That's the question that haunts every cold case: why so long?

In Sandra's case, the answer is simple: the technology didn't exist.

In 1984, DNA fingerprinting was brand new. The first criminal conviction using DNA evidence occurred in England in 1986—two years after Sandra's death. American law enforcement didn't adopt DNA testing widely until the 1990s. CODIS wasn't operational until 1998.

Even when the technology did exist, it wasn't good enough to process degraded samples. Sandra's evidence was tested repeatedly throughout the 2000s and 2010s, but each time, the results were inconclusive. Only in 2020, with the latest low-copy-number DNA analysis, was a full profile finally extracted.

And even then, it took genetic genealogy to identify the killer, because his DNA wasn't in CODIS.

So why so long? Because science takes time. Because technology advances in fits and starts. Because the tools needed to solve Sandra's case didn't exist when she was killed, and had to be invented over the course of four decades.

It's a frustrating answer. But it's the truth.

### What Happens Now?

Freddie Lee Granger Jr. is being held in the Ware County Jail without bond. He's been charged with one count of rape, one count of aggravated assault, one count of malice murder, and two counts of felony murder.

If convicted, he faces life in prison without the possibility of parole. The death penalty is theoretically on the table under Georgia law, but given Granger's age (68) and the decades-long gap between the crime and the arrest, prosecutors are unlikely to pursue it. Appeals would take decades, and Granger would likely die of old age before an execution could be carried out.

The case will be prosecuted by the Waycross Judicial Circuit District Attorney's Office. A trial date has not yet been set, but it will likely occur sometime in 2027. Granger's public defender will argue that the DNA evidence is unreliable due to its age, that the chain of custody may have been compromised, that there's no corroborating evidence linking Granger to the crime beyond the DNA match.

But the prosecution has 42 years of meticulous documentation. They have DNA experts who can testify to the reliability of modern testing. They have genetic genealogy evidence showing how Granger was identified. And they have the DNA itself, which is as close to a smoking gun as forensic science gets.

Granger will almost certainly be convicted.

And Sandra Kaye Davis, dead for 42 years, will finally have justice.

### The Waycross Community: Closure

For the people of Waycross, Georgia, the arrest of Freddie Granger felt like the closing of a wound that had never fully healed.

Sandra's murder in 1984 had shocked the small town. For years afterward, people locked their doors, watched their daughters more carefully, wondered if the killer was still among them. As decades passed and the case went cold, the fear faded, but the question remained: who did it?

Now they knew.

And strangely, the answer was both shocking and mundane. Freddie Granger wasn't a monster—not in the way people imagined monsters. He was just a man. An ordinary man who'd done a monstrous thing and then lived a quiet life for 42 years, never caught, never suspected, until DNA technology finally caught up to him.

At a small vigil held in Sandra's memory the day after the arrest, about 50 people gathered near the site where her body was found on Kollock Street. Most were older—people who'd known Sandra, or who remembered the fear that had gripped Waycross in 1984. A few younger people attended too, drawn by the news coverage or a sense of communal history.

David Davis spoke briefly. He thanked the GBI, the Waycross Police Department, and everyone who'd kept Sandra's memory alive. He said he wished his parents had lived to see this day. He said Sandra would have been 64 years old now, probably a grandmother herself, and that it broke his heart she never got that chance.

But he also said this: "Forty-two years is a long time. But it's not forever. Justice came. Late, but it came. And that matters."

The crowd agreed.

### The Legacy of DNA

Sandra Kaye Davis is one of thousands of cold case victims whose murders have been solved through DNA technology. The list grows every year: cases from the 1970s, 1980s, 1990s, solved decades later because science finally caught up.

The Golden State Killer, arrested in 2018 after 40 years. The killer of Angie Dodge, identified in 2019 after 23 years. The murderer of Christy Mirack, caught in 2018 after 25 years. And now, Freddie Granger, arrested in 2026 after 42 years.

Each case is a testament to two things: the power of DNA technology, and the persistence of investigators who refuse to let cold cases die.

But each case also raises uncomfortable questions. How many killers are still out there, their DNA preserved but not yet analyzed? How many families are still waiting for technology to advance just a little bit more? And how many people died before seeing justice, like Sandra's parents, because the science took too long?

There are no easy answers. But the Sandra Davis case proves one thing beyond doubt: cold cases are never truly hopeless. Evidence persists. Technology improves. And investigators like Marcus Reed keep working.

Forty-two years is a long time to wait. But Sandra finally got her answer.

### The Final Word

On March 31, 2026, forty-two years after Sandra Kaye Davis was murdered, the GBI arrested Freddie Lee Granger Jr.

The DNA that had waited four decades finally spoke.

It said his name.
