# PROLOGUE: KOLLOCK STREET

The morning of September 1, 1984, arrived quiet in Waycross, Georgia. Sunrise painted the Spanish moss gold. Church bells would ring soon—it was Saturday, and the small city of twenty-two thousand souls would ease into Labor Day weekend the way southern towns always had: slow, familiar, safe.

Kollock Street lay on the east side, where neighborhoods spread thin between the railroad tracks and the Okefenokee Swamp. Not the worst part of town, not the best. Working-class homes with chain-link fences and carports. The kind of street where everyone knew which dog barked at what time, which car belonged to whose cousin, which screen door squeaked.

The air already felt heavy. September in South Georgia meant heat that clung to skin, thunderheads building by afternoon, the constant buzz of cicadas promising no relief. Summer wasn't done yet. Fall was still weeks away.

Someone screamed just after seven a.m.

The body lay beside a home on Kollock Street—not inside, but outside, in the narrow strip of grass between the house and the property line. Her clothes were torn. Her skin bore marks that told a story no one wanted to read. Strangulation. The medical examiner would confirm it later, but anyone who saw her knew.

Sandra Kaye Davis. Twenty-two years old.

Her face was turned toward the morning sun, eyes closed. She looked like she might be sleeping if not for everything else—the bruises darkening her throat, the ligature marks, the way her body had been left. Discarded. Whoever did this hadn't even tried to hide her.

The Waycross Police Department arrived within minutes. Officers who'd grown up in this town, who knew these streets, who'd never worked a murder like this. Ware County didn't get many homicides. The occasional bar fight that went too far, domestic violence that ended in tragedy, but not this. Not a young woman strangled and sexually assaulted and left beside someone's home like trash.

By eight a.m., the Georgia Bureau of Investigation was on the phone. By nine, agents were on the way. Yellow crime scene tape went up around the house. Neighbors gathered at a distance, arms crossed, whispering. Who would do something like this? Did anyone see anything? Was she from around here?

Yes. Sandra was from Waycross. Twenty-two years old, a whole life ahead of her. Someone's daughter. Someone's friend. Someone who'd been alive yesterday and would never be alive again.

The heat pressed down. Detectives photographed the scene, collected evidence, bagged her torn clothing. They found DNA—semen, the kind of biological evidence that in 1984 represented the cutting edge of forensic science. They swabbed and sealed and labeled, working carefully because they knew this evidence might be the key to finding whoever did this.

What they didn't know was how long it would take.

The samples went to the crime lab. Reports came back weeks later: DNA present, profile inconclusive with current technology. No matches in any database. No immediate suspects. The investigation continued through September, into October, through the fall. Interviews. Tips. Dead ends.

By Christmas 1984, the case was cooling. By Sandra's birthday in 1985—she would have turned twenty-three—it was cold.

The DNA samples went into storage. Carefully preserved, properly documented, waiting for the day when science would catch up to justice. Waiting for technology that didn't exist yet. Waiting for a database that hadn't been built. Waiting for methods that hadn't been invented.

They would wait for forty-two years.

Longer than Sandra had been alive.

Longer than most marriages last. Longer than some people live. Long enough for a newborn baby to grow up, graduate college, start a career, have children of their own. Long enough for the world to change completely—the Soviet Union to fall, the internet to rise, cell phones to go from science fiction to pocket-sized supercomputers.

But on that September morning in 1984, no one knew any of that yet.

They just knew a young woman was dead.

And someone in Waycross had killed her.

# CHAPTER 1: SANDRA

She was twenty-two years old when she died. That's the first thing anyone knows about Sandra Kaye Davis, and for forty-two years, it was almost the only thing.

Twenty-two. Born in 1962, which meant she came into the world during the Cuban Missile Crisis, grew up during Vietnam and Watergate, graduated high school around 1980 when Reagan was elected and MTV hadn't launched yet. She was part of that generation caught between the seventies and eighties, between disco and new wave, between analog and digital.

Not that any of that meant much in Waycross.

Waycross, Georgia, in 1984 was the kind of town where generations stayed put. Population twenty-two thousand, give or take, depending on whether the paper mill was hiring. The seat of Ware County, positioned at the northern gateway to the Okefenokee Swamp. Hot, humid, insular. The kind of place where your grandmother's hairdresser knew your mother's boss's sister-in-law. Where high school rivalries lasted decades. Where leaving for college meant you'd probably gotten above your raising.

Most people didn't leave.

Sandra was one of them. Born in Waycross, raised in Waycross, still in Waycross at twenty-two. Whether that was by choice or circumstance, no one can say now. But she was there, part of the fabric of a small southern town where everyone knew everyone and nothing much changed year to year.

Except when it did.

September 1, 1984, was supposed to be unremarkable. A Saturday. Labor Day weekend just starting. The kind of day when you might run errands in the morning, maybe meet friends later, enjoy the last gasp of summer before fall hinted at arriving. In South Georgia, September still meant heat—oppressive, sticky, the kind that made you move slow and think slower.

Sandra woke up that morning. Ate breakfast, maybe. Got dressed. Thought about the day ahead. Did all the mundane things a twenty-two-year-old does on a Saturday morning when she has no idea it's her last day alive.

What did she look like? What was she wearing? Did she have plans? Was she meeting someone, or did the day stretch empty and formless ahead of her?

The details are lost now. Forty-two years erases most specifics. Memories fade, witnesses forget, and when a case goes cold, the victim's last day becomes a blur of uncertainty. What remains are the facts: She was twenty-two. She was from Waycross. She lived on or near Kollock Street. And on September 1, 1984, someone killed her.

But before she was a victim, she was a person.

That's easy to forget in true crime. The body beside the house, the crime scene photos, the evidence tags—all of it reduces a human being to a case number, a narrative device, a tragedy. She becomes "the victim," defined entirely by her worst day.

Sandra Kaye Davis was more than that.

She was twenty-two, which meant she'd had twenty-two years of life before September 1, 1984. Twenty-two years of birthdays and Christmases, summer vacations and first days of school. Twenty-two years of friendship and heartbreak, laughter and tears, dreams and disappointments. Twenty-two years of being someone's daughter, someone's friend, someone who mattered.

In 1984, a twenty-two-year-old woman in Waycross might have been working at the paper mill's administrative offices. Or waitressing at one of the downtown diners. Or clerking at Belk's or J.C. Penney. Or already married, maybe with a baby on the way, settling into the life her mother and grandmother had lived before her.

Or she might have been saving money to leave. To go to Savannah, or Jacksonville, or Atlanta. To start over somewhere bigger, somewhere with more opportunity, somewhere that felt less like a trap.

There's no way to know now.

What we do know is that she was twenty-two years old. And that on September 1, 1984, she was alive in the morning and dead by afternoon.

The loss of a life is always tragedy. But there's something particularly cruel about a young death. All that potential—unrealized. All those years—unspent. All those moments that should have happened—first apartment, first real love, maybe marriage and children, career milestones, aging parents to care for, grandchildren to spoil someday.

None of it happened.

Instead, there's just a body beside a house on Kollock Street. And forty-two years of silence.

Small towns remember their tragedies differently than cities do. In New York or Los Angeles, a murder might make the news for a day or two before disappearing into the statistical background noise. But in a town of twenty-two thousand, everyone knows. Everyone remembers. The victim's family shops at the same grocery store as everyone else. You see her mother at church. You pass her old house on the way to work.

The grief doesn't dissipate. It calcifies.

For Sandra's family, September 1, 1984, became a dividing line. There was life before, and life after. Before Sandra was killed, and after. Before their world shattered, and after they had to learn to live in the broken pieces.

Forty-two years is a long time to wait for justice.

Long enough for some family members to die without ever knowing who killed their daughter, their sister, their friend. Long enough for younger relatives to grow up hearing about "what happened to Sandra" as a cautionary tale, a family wound that never quite healed.

Long enough to wonder if anyone would ever be held accountable.

In 1984, DNA evidence was new. Revolutionary, even. The idea that a person's genetic code could be extracted from blood, semen, saliva, skin cells—that this code was unique enough to identify individuals—this was cutting-edge forensic science. Most police departments had barely heard of it. Most criminals had no idea they were leaving evidence behind that might someday damn them.

When the Waycross Police Department and the Georgia Bureau of Investigation collected DNA from Sandra's body and the crime scene, they were working at the frontier of forensic capability. They bagged it. Tagged it. Sent it to the lab. Got back results that said: Yes, DNA is present. No, we can't tell you whose it is.

Not yet.

The technology wasn't there yet. The databases didn't exist. CODIS—the FBI's Combined DNA Index System that would eventually link crimes across jurisdictions and decades—wouldn't launch until 1990. And even then, it would take years to populate it with enough profiles to be useful.

So the DNA sat in storage. Preserved carefully, maintained properly, but useless for now.

The detectives working Sandra's case in 1984 did everything they could with the tools they had. They interviewed neighbors, friends, anyone who might have seen something. They processed the crime scene, photographed everything, collected every scrap of potential evidence. They chased leads, investigated suspects, worked the case hard.

And eventually, reluctantly, they had to admit they'd hit a wall.

No witnesses. No murder weapon. No fingerprints that led anywhere. Just DNA that couldn't be analyzed and a young woman who would never get justice.

The case went cold.

But it didn't close.

That's the thing about murder investigations—they never really close. The file sits in a cabinet, or now on a server, waiting. Detectives retire and new ones take over. Technology improves. Witnesses' memories get jogged. Criminals get arrested for other crimes and suddenly old cases get new attention.

For forty-two years, Sandra Kaye Davis's case sat and waited.

Her family waited too.

Every year on September 1st, the anniversary. Every year on her birthday, the reminder of another year she didn't get to live. Every year at holidays, the empty chair at the table. The grief doesn't fade—it just becomes familiar. You learn to carry it. You learn to live with the weight.

And you wait.

Because maybe someday, someone will figure it out. Maybe someday, the person who did this will be caught. Maybe someday, there will be justice.

Forty-two years is a long time to hope.

But on March 31, 2026—four decades, two weeks, and one day after Sandra died—her family's wait finally ended.

Not because the killer confessed. Not because a witness came forward. Not because of some dramatic breakthrough in detective work.

Because of DNA.

The same DNA that was collected in 1984 and sat useless for decades. The same DNA that represented cutting-edge science but couldn't be analyzed properly yet. The same DNA that was preserved carefully, waiting for technology to catch up.

It finally did.

But we're getting ahead of ourselves.

In September 1984, Sandra Kaye Davis was twenty-two years old. She lived in Waycross, Georgia. She had a whole life ahead of her. She had dreams we'll never know about, plans we'll never hear, a future that was stolen from her on a hot Saturday morning.

And somewhere in Waycross, the person who killed her went about his day, thinking he'd gotten away with it.

For a while, he was right.

# CHAPTER 2: THE CRIME

The medical examiner's report was clinical. It had to be. Strip away the emotion, focus on the facts, document what happened to Sandra Kaye Davis's body between the last time she was seen alive and when she was found dead beside a home on Kollock Street.

Cause of death: Asphyxiation by strangulation.

Manner of death: Homicide.

Evidence of sexual assault: Positive.

The report went on for pages, detailing injuries, measurements, samples collected. But those three lines told the story. Sandra Kaye Davis had been sexually assaulted and strangled to death. And someone in Waycross had done it.

September 1, 1984. The timeline is fragmented now, details lost to four decades of fading memories and incomplete records. But here's what investigators knew then, and what we know now:

Sandra was alive that morning. By afternoon, she was dead. Her body was found beside a home on Kollock Street—not inside, which investigators noted immediately. Outside. In the strip of grass between the house and the property line. Partially clothed. Visible from the street if you looked.

Whoever killed her either attacked her outside, or killed her elsewhere and dumped her body there. Neither option made sense. An outdoor attack in daylight was risky—neighbors, witnesses, someone driving by. But moving a body was riskier still. More time, more exposure, more chances to be seen.

Yet there she was.

The crime scene told part of the story. Torn clothing. Signs of struggle. The ligature marks on her neck—deep, brutal, sustained. Strangulation takes time. It's not like the movies where someone chokes you for ten seconds and you're dead. It takes minutes. The killer would have felt her fighting, felt her weakening, felt her go still.

And kept pressing.

Sexual assault. The medical examiner documented injuries consistent with rape. DNA was collected—semen samples that would become the most important evidence in the case, though no one knew it yet. In 1984, DNA analysis was so new that most criminals had no idea they were leaving genetic fingerprints at crime scenes.

The killer left part of himself behind.

He just didn't know it would take forty-two years for that evidence to matter.

The Waycross Police Department responded first. Officers arrived, secured the scene, called for backup. This wasn't like the domestic disputes and bar fights and petty theft they usually handled. This was murder. Brutal, sexual, violent. The kind of crime that changes a town.

By mid-morning, the Georgia Bureau of Investigation was involved. The GBI assists local departments with major crimes, especially in smaller jurisdictions without the resources or expertise for complex investigations. Homicide detectives, crime scene technicians, forensic specialists—the full apparatus of a murder investigation descended on Kollock Street.

Yellow tape went up. Neighbors gathered at a distance, whispering, speculating. Cameras clicked. Evidence markers went down. Every blade of grass, every scrap of fabric, every smudge that might be blood or dirt or something else—all of it photographed, documented, collected.

The body went to the medical examiner. Autopsy scheduled. Cause of death obvious but still needed to be confirmed officially. Rape kit processed. DNA samples sent to the lab with the understanding that genetic analysis was experimental, uncertain, might not yield usable results.

Meanwhile, detectives started the tedious work of building a case. Who was Sandra Davis? Where did she live? Where had she been that day? Who saw her last? Who might have wanted to hurt her?

The answers were frustratingly sparse.

Sandra lived in the area. She was known in the neighborhood. But no one reported seeing her that morning, or hearing anything unusual, or noticing a struggle. The body was discovered by a neighbor around seven a.m.—already dead, already cooling in the September heat.

Time of death was estimated at early morning, between five and eight a.m. Broad window, but it narrowed things slightly. Someone killed her in the early morning hours. Either on Kollock Street, or somewhere nearby, then moved her body to that spot.

Why there? Why that house? Was it random? Did the killer live nearby? Did Sandra?

Detectives interviewed everyone on Kollock Street. Knocked on every door, asked the same questions over and over. Did you see anything? Hear anything? Notice anyone who didn't belong? Any strange cars, unfamiliar faces, unusual activity?

Nothing.

Either the killer was a ghost, or everyone was asleep, or people saw things they didn't realize were important until it was too late.

The investigation expanded. Friends, family, coworkers. Anyone who knew Sandra, anyone who'd seen her recently, anyone who might have information. Most people were cooperative but unhelpful. They hadn't seen her that day. They had no idea who'd want to hurt her. She was a nice girl. Kept to herself mostly. This kind of thing didn't happen in Waycross.

Except it just had.

Suspects emerged—the way they always do in a murder investigation. Ex-boyfriends. Men she'd turned down. Neighbors with criminal records. Anyone who pinged the detectives' radar. Each one was interviewed, alibis checked, backgrounds investigated.

None of them panned out.

The DNA evidence came back inconclusive. Present, but not matchable with 1984 technology. The genetic profile was too degraded, or the testing methods too primitive, or both. The lab techs did their best but couldn't produce a usable result. Maybe someday, they said. Maybe when the technology improves.

By October 1984, the investigation was stalling. Every lead had been chased. Every witness interviewed. Every piece of evidence analyzed. And still no arrests, no clear suspects, no breakthrough.

The case began to go cold.

Not officially—murder investigations never close. The file stayed open, the evidence remained logged, detectives continued to check in periodically. But the urgency faded. Other cases demanded attention. Resources shifted. Life moved on.

For everyone except Sandra's family.

For them, September 1, 1984, became a permanent wound. Their daughter, sister, friend—murdered, sexually assaulted, left beside a stranger's house like garbage. And no one was held accountable. No one arrested. No one punished.

Just an open case file and DNA samples sitting in storage, waiting.

What do you do with that kind of grief?

You wait. You hope. You mark the anniversaries and attend the candlelight vigils and post the "Remember Sandra" messages on whatever social media platform is current that year. You call the police department every few months asking for updates. You read about other cold cases getting solved and think, Maybe Sandra's will be next.

And you wait some more.

The 1980s became the 1990s. The 1990s became the 2000s. Technology improved. DNA analysis got better. CODIS launched—the national DNA database that could link crimes across jurisdictions. Cold cases started getting solved. Criminals who'd evaded justice for decades suddenly found themselves arrested, tried, convicted. DNA evidence that had sat useless for years was re-tested with new methods and yielded new results.

The Golden State Killer was caught in 2018—forty years after his crimes—using genetic genealogy and DNA matching. Suddenly, cold cases that seemed hopeless were being solved. Families who'd waited decades were getting closure.

Sandra's family watched and hoped.

The GBI periodically re-tested the evidence from Sandra's case. Every few years, when new DNA technology became available, they'd pull out the 1984 samples and run them again. Looking for matches. Looking for answers.

Nothing.

The 2000s became the 2010s. Sandra had been dead for twenty years, then thirty, then thirty-five. Some of her family members died without ever knowing who killed her. New detectives took over the case from the old ones. Digital files replaced paper ones. But the core mystery remained: Who killed Sandra Kaye Davis?

And why?

Rape is its own motive, horrifically. But why Sandra specifically? Was it random—wrong place, wrong time? Or was she targeted? Did the killer know her? Stalk her? Plan this?

No one could say.

But the evidence sat and waited. Preserved carefully in a climate-controlled evidence room somewhere, logged in a database, tracked through system upgrades and departmental reorganizations. The DNA that had been collected in 1984—samples that were cutting-edge science then, but useless for decades—remained intact.

Just in case.

Just in case the technology improved enough. Just in case CODIS expanded enough. Just in case someday, somehow, someone figured out how to match those samples to a name.

That day would come.

But not for forty-two years.

In the meantime, Sandra Kaye Davis stayed dead. Her killer stayed free. Her family stayed waiting.

And the DNA stayed in storage.

# CHAPTER 3: SMALL TOWN MURDER

Waycross, Georgia, in 1984 was the kind of place where murder wasn't supposed to happen.

Not this kind, anyway.

Twenty-two thousand people spread across a few square miles of South Georgia pine and palmetto, anchored by the railroad and the paper mill. Close enough to Jacksonville to catch their TV stations, far enough from anywhere that felt like a real city. The kind of town where high school football mattered, where church on Sunday wasn't optional, where your grandmother probably knew your boss's cousin.

Safe. Or so people thought.

September 1, 1984, shattered that illusion.

A young woman strangled and sexually assaulted, her body left beside a home on Kollock Street. Not hidden. Not buried in the swamp or dumped along some rural highway. Just left there, in a residential neighborhood, where anyone walking by could see her.

The news spread fast the way news does in small towns—phone trees and scanner listeners and the local radio station. By noon, everyone in Waycross knew. By evening, the Waycross Journal-Herald had the story above the fold. By Sunday, churches were praying for Sandra's family and asking God to help the police find who did this.

Fear set in.

If it happened to Sandra, it could happen to anyone. If someone in Waycross was capable of this, who else might be in danger? Parents kept daughters home. Women double-checked their locks. Men walked girlfriends to cars after dark.

The Waycross Police Department felt the pressure. Solve this. Find whoever did this. Make the town safe again.

But how?

The crime scene had yielded evidence—clothing, DNA, trace materials. But in 1984, DNA was too new to be useful. No national database existed. Even if they had a suspect, matching DNA would take weeks and might not be admissible in court. The technology was experimental, untested, still fighting for acceptance in the criminal justice system.

So detectives fell back on traditional methods. Interviews. Canvassing. Following leads. Building timelines. Checking alibis. The fundamentals of homicide investigation that had worked for decades before DNA existed.

They started with Sandra's known associates. Family first, though everyone was ruled out quickly—grief that raw can't be faked, and besides, alibis checked out. Friends next. Coworkers. Neighbors. Anyone who'd seen her recently or knew her routines.

Most people had nothing useful. She'd been around town the day before, maybe. Someone thought they saw her at the grocery store. Or was that Thursday? Memory is unreliable even immediately after an event, and when everyone's in shock, witnesses become even less reliable.

Ex-boyfriends got special attention. Domestic violence and sexual assault often overlap, and statistically, women are most likely to be killed by current or former intimate partners. But the interviews led nowhere. Most of Sandra's exes had moved on, had alibis, showed no signs of the kind of rage that leads to murder.

The detectives widened the circle. Known sex offenders in Ware County. Men with histories of violence. Anyone who'd been arrested for assault or rape or domestic battery. Each name got investigated, interviewed, alibi-checked.

None of them panned out.

By October, the investigation had interviewed hundreds of people. The case file was thick with statements, timelines, background checks, dead ends. Every detective working it felt the weight—a young woman murdered, a town demanding justice, and no clear suspect.

The Georgia Bureau of Investigation assigned additional resources. GBI agents have access to statewide databases, forensic labs, behavioral analysts. They reviewed the case file, looked for patterns, offered theories. Random attack? Serial predator? Personal vendetta?

Nothing fit cleanly.

A random attack would explain the lack of obvious motive, but why Kollock Street? Why that house? Random predators usually have patterns, prior victims, escalating behavior. This felt like a one-off. Brutal, yes. Organized enough to avoid leaving fingerprints or witnesses, yes. But not serial.

A personal attack made more sense. Someone who knew Sandra, or at least knew of her. Someone who targeted her specifically. But who? And why? If it was personal, there should be a connection—arguments, threats, jealousy, obsession. Something in Sandra's life that pointed to motive.

Detectives found nothing.

The investigation entered a frustrating phase where every lead seemed promising until it wasn't. A witness thought they saw a strange car on Kollock Street that morning—detectives chased it for days until they determined it belonged to someone's visiting relative. A neighbor reported hearing a scream around six a.m.—but couldn't remember if it was September 1st or some other day. Someone called the tip line saying they knew who did it—except the person they named was in prison in Florida at the time.

Dead ends. False starts. Wasted time.

Meanwhile, the DNA sat in the lab. Too degraded for 1984 technology. Maybe usable someday, but not now. The genetic profile existed—they could see that someone's DNA was present—but turning that profile into a name required technology that didn't exist yet.

By December 1984, the case was cooling. Still open, still assigned to detectives, still worked whenever new information came in. But the daily urgency faded. Other cases demanded attention. Resources shifted. The Waycross Police Department and GBI did what they could, but without new leads, there was only so much to do.

The file stayed open. Evidence stayed logged. The case went into the cold case rotation—periodically reviewed, checked against new databases, re-examined when technology improved.

But no arrests were made.

Sandra's family struggled with the lack of closure. No arrests meant no trial, no conviction, no justice. Whoever killed their daughter, sister, friend was still out there. Still free. Maybe still in Waycross, walking the same streets, shopping at the same stores, living a normal life while Sandra was buried and they were left with grief.

The town tried to move on. September 1st anniversaries came and went. Candlelight vigils. Newspaper remembrances. "Justice for Sandra" ribbons on car antennas. But over time, the urgency faded for everyone except those who loved her.

That's the cruelty of cold cases. The rest of the world forgets. Life moves on. New tragedies demand attention. A murder from last month, or last year, or five years ago becomes "that old case" and then "do you remember when..." and eventually it fades into the background.

But for the families, it never fades.

Sandra's mother kept a photo on the mantel. Her father called the police department twice a year asking for updates. Siblings posted "Remember Sandra" messages on social media every September 1st. The wound stayed fresh even as years passed.

And the detectives kept trying.

Cold cases don't get solved by giving up. They get solved because someone—a detective, a lab technician, a family member—refuses to let the case die. They get solved because evidence is preserved carefully. Because when new technology emerges, someone thinks to re-test old samples. Because databases expand and suddenly a DNA profile that matched nothing in 1984 matches someone in 2010.

The Waycross Police Department and GBI never closed Sandra's case. Every few years, a detective would pull the file, review the evidence, check for new leads. When CODIS launched in the 1990s, Sandra's DNA profile was entered. No matches. When DNA technology improved in the 2000s, the evidence was re-tested. Inconclusive. When genetic genealogy became viable in the 2010s, analysts looked at whether Sandra's case could be solved that way. Maybe, they said. But not yet.

The work continued. Quiet, persistent, often thankless. Cold case investigations are like that—long stretches of nothing, then suddenly a break.

For Sandra's case, that break took forty-two years.

But it started in 1984, with detectives doing their jobs. Collecting evidence. Preserving samples. Building a file. Creating the foundation that would eventually, decades later, lead to justice.

They didn't know it would take so long. They hoped for a quick arrest, a trial, justice for Sandra and her family. When weeks became months became years, they hoped for a break. A witness coming forward. A deathbed confession. Some new technology that would make sense of the DNA they'd collected.

They got the technology. Eventually.

The DNA samples went into storage in 1984. They would sit there for forty-two years—longer than Sandra had been alive—waiting for science to catch up.
