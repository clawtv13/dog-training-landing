# PROLOGUE: The Girl in the Ditch

The farmer saw the umbrella first.

Bright yellow, decorated with cartoon characters, it lay half-hidden in tall grass beside County Road 68. His truck rolled past before the wrongness of it hit him. Fifty yards down, his foot found the brake. Kids don't abandon their umbrellas. Not willingly.

He turned back.

Morning pressed gray against the Indiana fields—that particular April shade where winter refuses to release its grip. Mist hung in the ditches like frozen breath. County Road 68 was the kind of road you took to avoid people, to get work done without traffic getting in the way. Miles of corn stubble and fence posts.

The umbrella lay ten feet from the road's edge. As he approached, he saw more: a shoe. Small. Pink. The kind a little girl would wear.

His throat closed.

Another step revealed her.

She lay in the ditch, partially hidden by brown grass, her small body still against the earth. A red windbreaker stood out against the dull morning. One bare foot. The other still wore its mate—carefully tied white laces on pink canvas.

He didn't go closer. Didn't need to. The angle of her neck, the unnatural stillness—he understood.

His hands shook as he reached for the CB radio.

---

By the time police arrived, word had begun spreading through DeKalb County's small communities. Spencerville. Grabill. The rural townships where everyone knew everyone, where children still played outside unsupervised because nothing bad ever happened here.

Fort Wayne Police had been searching since Friday. Three days. Seventy-two hours that felt like years to her mother, who'd reported her missing when she didn't come home for dinner. The little girl had only been walking three blocks to retrieve an umbrella she'd left behind.

They'd found the umbrella. And they'd found her.

April Marie Tinsley. Eight years old. Second grade at Fairfield Elementary. Brown hair, brown eyes, four feet tall, sixty pounds. She sang in the church choir. Had a baby brother too young to understand why his sister wasn't coming back.

The crime scene unit worked through the morning—methodical, silent. Photographs. Measurements. Evidence collection: fibers, soil samples, and something else. Something that would sit in a police locker for thirty years, waiting for technology to catch up with justice.

The jogger who'd found her—a local farmer checking fence lines—would never shake the image. Decades later, that yellow umbrella still appeared in his dreams. Cartoon characters grinning at a gray sky. Cheerfulness beside horror.

---

In Fort Wayne, April's mother sat surrounded by police officers and family, waiting for news she already knew. A mother knows. She'd known Friday night. Knew through Saturday's searching, Sunday's prayers. Knew when detectives climbed her front steps that Monday morning.

But knowing doesn't make it real. Doesn't make it bearable.

The detective chose his words carefully—"found" and "passed" instead of "murdered" and "dead." The words didn't matter. Only one word mattered: April. And her daughter's name would never sound the same.

---

The investigation moved fast initially. Tips flooded in. Witnesses reported a blue pickup truck. A composite sketch emerged of a man spotted near the abduction site. DNA evidence collected from April's body—semen recovered from her underwear—represented cutting-edge forensic science in 1988, though the technology couldn't yet identify its source.

Five suspects within weeks. All tested. None matched.

The trail went cold.

Two years later, someone found a message scrawled in crayon on a barn near where April's body had been discovered:

*I kill 8 year old April Marie Tisley did you find the other shoe haha I will kill agin*

Misspellings. Mocking laughter. A promise of more death. The message hung over Fort Wayne like smoke, suffocating hope that this had been an isolated tragedy.

No more children died. But the city's innocence did.

Parents who'd let their children walk to school alone started driving them. Yards once full of playing kids emptied. The neighborhood where April had lived—where doors used to stay unlocked—became a grid of watchful eyes and bolted windows.

Sixteen years passed. Case files gathered dust. April's mother marked birthdays her daughter would never have. The detective who'd worked the case retired, carrying the failure like a stone.

Then, in 2004, the messages started again.

Four houses in Fort Wayne. Four families. Four little girls came home to find notes tucked into plastic bags on their bicycles:

*Hi Honey I Been watching you I am the same person that kinapped an Rape an kill Aproil Tinsely you are my next vitem*

The notes came with Polaroid photographs. Used condoms. DNA that matched the 1988 evidence. The killer was still here. Still watching. Still wanting them to know.

But who? Police had the DNA. The handwriting. The photographs. Everything except a name.

---

On a July morning in 2018—thirty years and three months after April Tinsley disappeared—two Fort Wayne detectives pulled into the Grabill Mobile Home Park. Lot #4. They'd watched the trailer for a week, waiting to be certain.

The man who answered the door was fifty-nine. Gray hair. Glasses. The kind of neighbor who keeps to himself, the kind you'd pass in a store and never notice.

"Do you know why we're here?" the detective asked.

The man stood silent, face unreadable. Then, quietly: "April Tinsley."

He knew. He'd been waiting thirty years for this knock. When they asked what happened, he told them everything. The abduction. The rape. The strangulation. What he'd done to her body afterward. Where he'd left her. Why he'd taunted them.

He told it all without hesitation, without tears, without any sign he understood the decades of grief he'd caused or the childhood he'd stolen. He recited the horror like reading a grocery list—clinical, detached.

Thirty years before anyone knew who had taken April Tinsley.

And when the truth finally came, it proved that monsters don't always look like monsters.

Sometimes they're your neighbor.

# CHAPTER 1: A Normal Friday

## April 1, 1988 — Fort Wayne, Indiana

April Marie Tinsley woke to sunshine streaming through her bedroom window. Eight years old, gap-toothed smile, dark brown hair that her mother later described as "always a little messy, no matter how many times I brushed it." She'd had Mr. Hops, her favorite stuffed rabbit, since she was three—worn thin from being dragged everywhere. Grocery stores, church, sleepovers at Grandma's house.

Good Friday. Schools let out early.

April didn't care about the religious significance. What mattered was the extra hour of freedom, the weekend stretching ahead like an open field. Second grade at Fairfield Elementary meant multiplication tables (she still counted on her fingers when no one looked) and singing in the children's choir.

Mrs. Patterson had handed out pastel eggs filled with candy that morning. April got a yellow one. Yellow was her favorite—not sunshine yellow, more like butter yellow, the soft kind that didn't hurt your eyes. Her umbrella matched: yellow decorated with cartoon characters she'd picked out herself at the Dollar Store on South Calhoun Street. Three weeks of allowance saved.

At home, the Tinsley house on West Williams Street smelled like Friday: tuna casserole baking, faint laundry detergent from clothes hanging on the backyard line. The neighborhood was the kind where everyone knew everyone. Chain-link fences. Kids on bikes without helmets. Unlocked doors.

Baby Paul napped when April got home just after noon. She tiptoed past—waking him meant Mom would be busy for the next hour. No permission to go outside.

"Mom, can I go to Katie's?" Already pulling on sneakers by the front door.

Janet Tinsley looked up from folding laundry. Her daughter vibrated with that specific energy eight-year-olds have on Friday afternoons when weather is perfect and school is finally, blessedly over.

"Which Katie? Bishop or Hobbs?"

"Katie Bishop. Melissa's there too. We're gonna play Barbies."

Janet knew the house. Three blocks. Williams to Lafayette to Hoagland. April had walked it dozens of times.

"Be home by five. Take your umbrella. Weather said rain."

April grabbed the yellow umbrella from the hook, though the sky was clear. Mom always worried about rain. Or strangers. Or cars going too fast. A hundred other things that seemed silly when you were eight and the whole world felt safe.

"Love you!" Already halfway out.

"Love you too, baby."

The screen door slammed.

---

## 3:00 PM

April loved Katie Bishop's house. Katie's mom let them have unlimited juice boxes, and Katie owned the Barbie Dream House—the big one with the working elevator. April only had three Barbies at home, all hand-me-downs from her cousin. Katie had at least ten, plus Ken, plus the pink convertible.

They played for over an hour. Melissa Hobbs arrived around 2:30. Three of them sprawled across Katie's bedroom floor, narrating elaborate soap operas where Barbie was a veterinarian, Ken was a race car driver, and everyone lived in houses with elevators because obviously.

Katie's mom called up the stairs: "Girls, we're going to Melissa's house. You coming, April?"

April looked around. Her umbrella was downstairs.

"I'll catch up. Just gotta grab my umbrella."

They spilled out together—sunlight, laughter, childhood invincibility. Melissa's house was four blocks in the opposite direction. April would grab the umbrella, then cut through the alley to Melissa's. Easy.

"Don't take forever!" Katie called.

April grinned. "I won't!"

She watched her friends disappear around the corner, their voices fading into the afternoon hum: lawnmowers, someone's radio through an open window, a dog barking three yards over.

April turned back. Her umbrella was just inside Katie's front door, propped against the wall where she'd left it. Thirty seconds to grab it.

But Katie's mom had locked the door. April heard the deadbolt click.

No big deal. She'd go home, grab her backup umbrella (plain blue, not as cool but functional), then head to Melissa's. Home was three blocks. She'd done it a hundred times. Could walk it with her eyes closed.

April started down the sidewalk. Williams Street was quiet. Most people still at work. A few cars passed—no one she recognized. Spring air smelled like cut grass and someone grilling burgers at 3 PM.

She kicked a pebble along, counting steps. One-two-three-four. Even numbers were best. Eight was her favorite because that's how old she was. Would nine be her favorite next year? Or would eight always be the best?

Lots to wonder about. Would she be taller than Melissa by fifth grade? Would Mr. Hops eventually fall apart completely, and could Mom sew him back together one more time? What would being a grown-up feel like—having a job, driving a car, not needing permission to visit friends?

Would there be tuna casserole left when Dad got home from work?

April didn't wonder if she was safe. That thought didn't occur to her. Why would it? This was her neighborhood. Her streets. She knew every crack in the sidewalk, every yard with a mean dog, every shortcut.

Three blocks from home.

Three blocks from Mom folding laundry.

Three blocks from baby Paul asleep in his crib.

Three blocks from tuna casserole in the oven.

Three blocks from everything normal and boring and safe.

## What April Loved

Before we continue, you should know a few things about April Marie Tinsley. Not April the victim. Not April the headline. Just April.

She loved singing. Off-key, too loud, but she sang anyway—belting hymns in the children's choir at Faith United Methodist Church every Sunday. "Jesus Loves Me" was her favorite. Short. All the words memorized.

She loved animals. A stray orange tabby with a torn ear roamed the neighborhood. April snuck it lunch scraps: bologna sandwiches, cheese crackers, once an entire hard-boiled egg. Mom said they couldn't afford a pet. April was working on her. Persistence was key.

She loved her family. Dad worked long hours, but when he was home, he'd toss her up until she shrieked with laughter. Mom was strict about bedtime and vegetables but also read stories every night, doing all the voices—scary monsters, brave princesses.

And Paul. Baby Paul, who couldn't do much except cry and sleep and grab April's finger with his tiny fist. April had been an only child for seven years and liked it that way. But now? She couldn't imagine life without him. She'd teach him everything when he got older: bike riding, tree climbing, cookie jar raiding.

She loved her friends. Katie and Melissa, but also Amanda and Sarah and Tommy from down the street. Tag until streetlights came on. Forts from cardboard boxes. Trading stickers and friendship bracelets and secrets.

She loved her neighborhood. Mr. Kowalski's corner store gave her free bubble gum if she swept the front step. The library on Fairfield Avenue—Mrs. Chen knew her name and saved new *Baby-Sitters Club* books. The park with good swings that went high enough to flip your stomach.

She loved normal things. Mac and cheese. Saturday cartoons. Mom's perfume. Rain on the roof. Clean sheets. Sun through backyard trees, all gold and dappled.

April Marie Tinsley loved her life.

No reason not to.

## 3:15 PM

April was halfway home when she saw the car.

Nothing remarkable. Just a car, slowing as it approached. She kept walking. Cars slowed all the time. Looking for addresses. Lost.

The car stopped.

April kept walking. Her mother had taught the rules: Don't talk to strangers. Don't take candy from anyone you don't know. If someone asks for help, find an adult—it's not your job.

But April also lived in a world where neighbors helped neighbors, where adults were generally trustworthy, where nothing bad had ever happened on Williams Street.

She didn't run.

She should have run.

## What Came Next

Details exist about what happened next—recovered from a confession given thirty years later, assembled from forensic evidence, too horrific to include here because this chapter is about April when she was still April. Not yet a case file. Not yet a cold case. Not yet a cautionary tale.

What matters: April Marie Tinsley, eight years old, second grade, gap-toothed smile, lover of yellow umbrellas and stray cats and *Baby-Sitters Club* books, disappeared.

By 6:00 PM, when she hadn't come home, her mother called police.

By midnight, the entire neighborhood was searching.

By morning, hope was fading.

Three days later, a jogger found her body in a ditch twenty miles away.

## Before the End

But that's not this chapter. This chapter is about the before. The normal Friday, the good Friday, the Friday that should have been unremarkable and safe and boring.

This chapter is about an eight-year-old girl who loved her life and had no idea it was about to end.

This chapter is about the moment before everything changed.

This would be the last.

# BOOK #2: MIND CRIMES
## CHAPTER 2: Vanished

Good Friday, April 1, 1988. Fort Wayne, Indiana.

The afternoon sun promised warmth but delivered chill—early spring's false advertising. Eight-year-old April Tinsley didn't mind. She had her umbrella, permission to play, and friends waiting.

April was a tornado of energy and laughter. The kind of kid who sang in church choir without self-consciousness, made friends easily, trusted the world because the world had given her no reason not to. Her mother, Janet, described her as "the perfect daughter"—not pristine perfection, but messy, alive, running-barefoot-through-summer perfection.

That afternoon, April and two friends ping-ponged between houses on Fort Wayne's south side, the way kids did then. No cell phones for check-ins. No GPS trackers sewn into backpacks. Just "be home by dinner" and the understanding that neighborhoods were safe.

Around 3:00 PM, the trio decided to move to another friend's house. April realized she'd left her umbrella behind—three blocks back. A five-minute walk through a neighborhood where everyone knew everyone.

"I'll go get it," April said.

Her friends shrugged. Okay. See you in a minute.

That minute never came.

---

April walked alone down West Williams Street. Houses here were modest, close together, the kind of street where people sat on porches watching the world go by. Someone should have seen her. Maybe someone did.

What we know: somewhere between her friend's house and home—somewhere in those three familiar blocks—April vanished.

No scream heard. No struggle witnessed. Just a little girl with an umbrella, and then nothing.

---

Janet Tinsley first felt worry's cold finger around 6:00 PM.

April should be home. Dinner was ready. Baby Paul was in his high chair. Michael, April's father, was washing up. The table was set for four.

April's chair sat empty.

"She's probably just playing," Michael said. "Lost track of time."

Janet wanted to believe that. But worry coiled in her chest, tightening with each passing minute. She walked to the neighbor's house. Had they seen April?

No. Not since earlier.

She walked to the friend's house where April had gone for the umbrella.

"She left an hour ago," the friend's mother said. "I thought she went home."

The cold finger became a fist.

---

By 6:15, Janet was running—through the neighborhood, calling April's name. Neighbors emerged, seeing panic in Janet's face.

"April! April Marie Tinsley!"

Nothing.

At 6:30, Janet called Fort Wayne Police.

"My daughter is missing."

---

Officers arrived within minutes, fanning out through the neighborhood. Flashlights cut through growing dusk. They knocked on doors, checked backyards and garages and abandoned buildings. They interviewed neighbors, piecing together April's last movements.

A picture emerged: April had walked alone, heading back for her umbrella. Then she'd disappeared.

No one saw a struggle. No one heard a cry. It was as if she'd simply evaporated.

But children don't evaporate. Someone had taken her.

---

By 8:00 PM, the search had grown. Neighbors joined police, armed with flashlights and hope already curdling into dread. They checked every alley, every dumpster, every shadow. Called April's name until voices went hoarse.

Janet stood on her front porch, shaking. Michael held her, but his hands trembled too. Inside, baby Paul cried, oblivious to the nightmare unfolding.

"Where is she?" Janet whispered. "Where is my baby?"

---

The first twenty-four hours in a missing child case are critical. Every cop knows this. Every parent prays they never learn it.

As night fell on Fort Wayne, the search intensified. Volunteers arrived with coffee and grim determination. Fort Wayne Police mobilized every available officer, set up a command center, coordinated search grids, contacted neighboring jurisdictions.

A detective pulled Michael and Janet aside.

"We need to ask you some questions."

Standard procedure. Statistics are brutal: most children are harmed by someone they know. Parents, relatives, family friends. So the detective asked terrible, necessary questions.

"Has April been upset lately?"

"Any arguments at home?"

"Can you think of anyone who might want to hurt her?"

"Do you have any enemies?"

Janet answered mechanically, voice flat with shock. No, no, no. April was happy. April was loved. April had no reason to run and every reason to come home.

The detective believed her. But belief doesn't find missing children.

---

Midnight came and went. April had been missing for nine hours.

Volunteers searched through darkness, flashlight beams bobbing like desperate prayers. They checked abandoned buildings, construction sites, drainage ditches. Walked April's route over and over, as if repetition could reveal what they'd missed.

Nothing.

Fort Wayne is mid-sized—population around 170,000 in 1988. Big enough to have crime, small enough that everyone knew each other. The kind of place where people left doors unlocked, kids walked to school alone, where a child being abducted in daylight was unthinkable.

Until now.

---

Saturday, April 2, 1988. Day two.

Janet Tinsley hadn't slept. How could she? Somewhere her daughter was scared, hurt, alone. Or worse. No—she couldn't think about worse. Had to believe April was alive.

The search resumed at dawn. More volunteers—dozens, then hundreds. Teachers from Fairfield Elementary. Parents who'd seen the news and thought about their own children. Off-duty cops. Strangers who heard "April Tinsley" and felt compelled to help.

They formed search lines, walked through fields and forests and abandoned lots. Checked dumpsters, drainage pipes, anyplace a child's body might be hidden.

Body. The word hung in the air, unspoken but omnipresent.

---

Fort Wayne Police released a statement. Eight-year-old April Tinsley, blonde hair, blue eyes, last seen wearing... The description broadcast on every local news station, printed in every newspaper.

HAVE YOU SEEN THIS GIRL?

Tips flooded in. A girl matching April's description at a gas station. No—someone else. A suspicious van near Williams Street. Investigators chased it down. Dead end. A neighbor remembered seeing a man watching children play. Police interviewed him. Nothing.

False leads. Red herrings. Desperate hope grasping at shadows.

---

Janet stood in the living room, staring at April's school photo on the mantel. That smile. That bright, trusting smile.

"Come home, baby," Janet whispered. "Please come home."

The phone rang constantly. Reporters wanting statements. Friends offering support. Police providing updates that weren't updates. We're doing everything we can. We haven't given up hope.

But after forty-eight hours, hope became harder to hold.

---

Sunday, April 3, 1988. Day three.

Easter Sunday. Churches across Fort Wayne packed. But many pews sat empty—people were out searching instead of celebrating resurrection.

At Faith United Methodist Church, where April sang in the children's choir, the pastor led prayer.

"Lord, bring April home safely. Protect her, wherever she is. Give her family strength. Guide the searchers. Let this story have a happy ending."

Amen. Amen. Please, God, amen.

But God, it seemed, wasn't listening.

---

The search expanded beyond Fort Wayne. Neighboring counties. FBI. Bloodhounds. Helicopters. Divers. All deployed.

The trail led nowhere.

Nothing. Nothing. Nothing.

---

Janet Tinsley appeared on the evening news, her face a mask of barely controlled terror.

"If someone has April, please—please—just let her go. Bring her home. She's just a little girl. She hasn't hurt anyone. Please."

Her voice cracked. The camera lingered on her tears, broadcasting anguish to the entire region.

Michael stood beside her, hand on her shoulder, his face grey with exhaustion and fear. Baby Paul was with relatives—too young to understand why mommy was crying, why strangers filled the house, why everyone kept saying his sister's name.

"April is the perfect daughter," Janet continued, voice barely above a whisper. "She's sweet and kind and she trusts people. She wouldn't have fought if someone told her to come with them. She would have trusted them."

That trust. That beautiful, terrible trust children have before the world teaches them fear.

Someone had exploited that trust. Someone had looked at a little girl with an umbrella and seen opportunity.

---

Monday, April 4, 1988. Day four.

The sun rose on another day of searching. Volunteers assembled at Fort Wayne Police headquarters, receiving grid assignments and encouragement. Don't give up. Keep looking. She's out there.

Twenty miles northeast of Fort Wayne, in Spencerville—a tiny unincorporated community in DeKalb County—a jogger took his morning run along County Road 68.

Rural route. Farm fields. Scattered houses. Quiet. Peaceful. The kind of place where nothing ever happened.

The jogger noticed something in the ditch beside the road. At first he thought it was a discarded doll or bag of trash. He almost kept running.

But something made him stop. Look closer.

Blonde hair. A small hand. A child.

The jogger stumbled backward, hand over his mouth. He ran to the nearest house, pounded on the door.

"Call 911. Now. There's a body. There's a little girl."

## CHAPTER 3: The Search

The call came into Fort Wayne Police at 7:42 AM.

Body found. County Road 68. Female child. Possible match for April Tinsley.

For three days, hundreds had searched. For three days, they'd prayed for a miracle. And now, twenty miles from where she'd vanished, April had been found.

But miracles come in only one form. Bodies come in the other.

---

Detective work is methodical. Careful. By-the-book. But when the call came about the body in Spencerville, detectives didn't walk—they ran.

DeKalb County Sheriff's Department had secured the scene. Yellow tape cordoned off a section of County Road 68. The jogger who'd found April sat in a deputy's cruiser, shaking, still in shock.

The ditch was shallow, unremarkable. The kind of place you'd drive past a thousand times without noticing. But now it would be forever marked as the place where April's story ended.

Investigators approached carefully, cameras clicking, documenting everything. The body was partially concealed, one shoe missing. April's clothing was disheveled but identifiable.

One detective—a veteran who'd seen more death than any human should—felt his throat tighten. He had a daughter April's age.

"It's her," he said quietly. "It's April Tinsley."

---

At Fort Wayne Police, officers prepared to deliver the news no parent should receive.

Janet and Michael Tinsley had been awake for four days straight—sustained by coffee, cigarettes, and rapidly dimming hope that their daughter was still alive. When detectives arrived that morning, Janet knew before they said a word.

"No," she said. Just that. "No."

"Mrs. Tinsley—"

"NO!"

She collapsed. Michael caught her, but he was falling too, folding into himself, his strength gone. The detectives steadied them both, guided them to the couch, said the words that would echo forever.

"We found April. I'm so, so sorry."

Janet's scream could be heard three houses down.

---

The medical examiner's report would come later with its clinical language and autopsy findings. But even without the report, investigators could see what had happened.

April had been sexually assaulted. Strangled. Her death hadn't been quick or painless. Someone had stolen her, violated her, ended her life, and discarded her like trash beside a rural road.

Cause of death: asphyxiation. Manual strangulation. Someone had wrapped their hands around an eight-year-old girl's throat and squeezed until she stopped breathing.

The autopsy would reveal more horrors—evidence of sexual assault, both before and after death. Whoever had taken April hadn't just murdered her. They'd brutalized her in ways that made even hardened detectives want to vomit.

But those details wouldn't be released to the public. The Tinsley family would be spared the full truth, though partial truth was devastating enough.

---

By noon, the news had spread across Fort Wayne.

APRIL TINSLEY FOUND DEAD.

Volunteers who'd searched for three days broke down in the street. At Fairfield Elementary, counselors helped eight-year-olds understand that their friend wasn't coming back. Teachers wept. Parents held their children tighter. Fort Wayne's innocence died that day along with April Tinsley.

---

Fort Wayne Police held a press conference at 3:00 PM.

Police Chief stood at a podium, flanked by FBI agents and county sheriffs. His voice was steady, professional. But his hands gripped the podium white-knuckle tight.

"At approximately 7:30 this morning, the body of April Tinsley was discovered in DeKalb County. The Allen County Coroner has confirmed her identity. This is now a homicide investigation."

Cameras flashed. Reporters shouted questions.

"Do you have any suspects?"

"Was she sexually assaulted?"

"Do you think she was killed where she was found, or elsewhere?"

The Chief raised his hand for silence.

"We are pursuing all leads. We're asking anyone with information—anyone who saw anything unusual on Friday afternoon, anyone who noticed suspicious vehicles or individuals—to contact us immediately. No detail is too small."

He paused, jaw tight.

"Someone in this community knows who did this. Someone saw something. And we will not rest until April's killer is brought to justice."

---

But justice is a long road. Sometimes, thirty years long.

---

Evidence recovered from April's body included DNA—semen found on her underwear. In 1988, DNA profiling was in its infancy. The technology existed, but forensic databases didn't. No CODIS, no national registry of criminal DNA. The genetic material was carefully preserved, catalogued, stored.

Maybe someday, investigators thought. Maybe someday this will matter.

It would. But not for three decades.

---

Fort Wayne Police interviewed hundreds. Friends, neighbors, registered sex offenders, anyone with a history of violence against children. They compiled lists, cross-referenced alibis, followed every lead no matter how slim.

They developed a composite sketch based on witness descriptions of a man seen near where April disappeared. The sketch was distributed to every police department in Indiana, posted in every post office and convenience store.

HAVE YOU SEEN THIS MAN?

Tips flooded in. Every tip investigated. Every one led nowhere.

One suspect rose briefly—a 34-year-old man with a history of child molestation charges. Police questioned him extensively. His alibi held. Physical evidence didn't match. He was cleared.

The investigation stalled. Days became weeks. Weeks became months. Leads dried up. Tips stopped coming.

April Tinsley's murder went cold.

---

On April 8, 1988—one week after April was abducted—her memorial service was held at Faith United Methodist Church.

Packed. Standing room only. Hundreds who'd searched, who'd prayed, who'd hoped against hope that April would come home.

The casket was small. Too small. Yellow roses—April's favorite—rested on top.

Janet Tinsley sat in the front pew, supported by family on either side. She stared straight ahead, face blank. Shock had given way to numbness so deep it felt like drowning.

The pastor spoke about innocence, faith, senseless tragedy. He said April was with Jesus now, safe in His arms. Said her suffering was over.

But what about the suffering of those left behind? What about parents who would never see their daughter grow up? What about the little brother who would never really remember his sister?

The choir sang "Amazing Grace." Janet's lips moved silently, forming words without sound. How could you sing about grace in a world where children were murdered?

---

April was buried at Greenlawn Memorial Park. The gravestone was simple:

APRIL MARIE TINSLEY  
1980 - 1988  
BELOVED DAUGHTER

Eight years. That's all she got. Eight years of sunshine and laughter and church choir and playing with friends. And then darkness.

---

In the weeks following April's funeral, Fort Wayne tried to move on. People returned to work, to school, to ordinary life rhythms. But something had shifted. Parents no longer let children walk alone. Doors once unlocked were now bolted. Strangers were viewed with suspicion.

The monster who'd killed April was still out there. Walking the same streets. Breathing the same air. Maybe watching news coverage, satisfied with the chaos he'd created.

On April 20, 1988, ninety Fort Wayne residents formed a volunteer organization called APRIL—Associated Parents Regional Independent League, also known as Abduction Prevention Reconnaissance and Information League. Their mission: help solve missing children cases, prevent future abductions, keep April's memory alive.

But volunteer organizations and community vigilance don't catch killers. Police work does. Evidence does. And in 1988, the evidence led nowhere.

---

Fort Wayne Police refused to give up. Every few years, they'd reopen the case, reinterview witnesses, reexamine evidence. They sent DNA samples to different labs, hoping new technology might reveal what old technology couldn't.

In 1990, the case made headlines again—for the worst reason.

Someone had written a message on a barn door in St. Joseph Township, not far from where April's body was found. Scrawled in crayon:

"I kill 8 year old April Marie Tisley did you find the other shoe haha I will kill agin"

Atrocious spelling. Mocking tone. Unmistakable content—April's killer was taunting them.

Investigators descended on the barn, dusting for prints, searching for DNA, interviewing everyone within five miles. They found crayons nearby but nothing identifying the writer.

The message proved one thing: the killer was still in the area. Still thinking about April. Still proud of what he'd done.

And still free.

---

Years passed. The case remained open but essentially dead. Janet and Michael Tinsley tried rebuilding their lives. How do you rebuild around a void that size? They had Paul to raise, tried giving him a normal childhood. But April's absence was a constant presence.

On anniversaries of her death, Janet visited the grave, laid fresh flowers, sat for hours just... remembering. April at six, missing front teeth. April at seven, singing off-key but confident. April at eight, walking toward death with an umbrella in her hand.

"Why?" Janet whispered to the gravestone. "Why her? Why my baby?"

The gravestone had no answers.

---

Then, in 2004, sixteen years after April's murder, the killer spoke again.

Four homes in Fort Wayne received disturbing packages. Three were left on girls' bicycles in front yards. One was placed in a mailbox. Each package contained:

- A handwritten note on lined yellow paper
- Used condoms containing semen
- Polaroid photographs showing a nude male lower body
