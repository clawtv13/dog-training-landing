## 🎯 EVALUATION RUBRIC (1-10 Scale)

### 1. STORY COHERENCE: **9/10**

**Assessment:** The narrative follows a clear, logical progression from discovery → investigation → cold case → breakthrough → arrest. Cause-and-effect is strong throughout.

**Strengths:**
- **Perfect chronological flow:** Prologue (1990 discovery) → Chapter 1 (the victims' last night) → Chapter 2-3 (discovery & early investigation) → Chapter 4-5 (case goes cold) → Chapter 6-7 (DNA breakthrough & suspect profile) → Chapter 8-10 (2025-26 arrest & justice)
- **No plot holes:** Every major event is documented and explained
- **Consistent world-building:** Houston in 1990 vs 2026 is clearly differentiated; police procedures, DNA technology evolution, and family experiences all track logically
- **Excellent setup/payoff:** The 1990 anonymous tip (Chapter 4) pays off in Chapter 8; the 1996 DNA collection failure becomes the key turning point

**Minor Weaknesses:**
- One small logic gap: Chapter 5 mentions Parrott was "sentenced to 20 years in 1995, served three," but doesn't explicitly explain *when* those three years were served or how he was free to commit the 1996 assault. This creates mild confusion about his incarceration timeline.
- The "recently placed" DNA upload in 2025/26 is mentioned multiple times but never fully explained—*why* was it only uploaded then? This feels like an important detail that's glossed over.

**Score Justification:** Near-flawless coherence. The narrative never leaves the reader confused about what happened or why. The one point deduction is for the timeline clarity issues above.

Chapter 7 (Parrott's profile) is strong but long—could be tightened by 15%.

**Act III - Chapters 8-10 (75-95%):** ✅ Excellent acceleration. The 2025 tip → DNA match → arrest sequence is propulsive and satisfying.

**Epilogue (95-100%):** ✅ Perfect resolution. Reflective, bittersweet, emotionally complete.

### 4. TONE & VOICE: **10/10**

**Assessment:** Absolutely masterful. The narrative voice is consistent, respectful, restrained, and emotionally powerful throughout. Genre-perfect.

**Tone:**

- Emotional without being manipulative
- Detailed without being gratuitous

This is **exactly** how true crime should be written.

**Examples of Excellent Tone:**

**Restraint in violence (Prologue):**

This conveys brutality without voyeurism. The reader understands the horror without being subjected to graphic detail.

**Empathy for victims (Chapter 1):**

This could easily be cloying or sentimental, but it's not—it's grounded in real human emotion.

No exclamation points. No melodrama. Just facts that speak for themselves. Devastating.

**Authorial Voice:**

- Occasionally employs rhetorical questions ("How was that possible?") to mirror reader's thoughts
- Uses short, punchy sentences for emphasis: "He'd been there all along. Named twice in unrelated cases. Ignored twice."
- Never strays into modern slang or anachronistic language

**Genre Consistency:**
- ✅ Matches true crime documentary tone (Serial, In the Dark, etc.)
- ✅ No sudden shifts to comedic, melodramatic, or preachy
- ✅ Balances police procedural detail with human emotion perfectly

**Score Justification:** Flawless tone and voice. This is publishable-quality true crime writing. No deductions.

### 6. SHOW vs. TELL: **9/10**

**Assessment:** The manuscript predominantly *shows* rather than tells, with strong sensory detail and concrete scenes. Some exposition-heavy sections could be dramatized.

**STRENGTHS:**

**Showing emotion through behavior (Chapter 1):**
> "At Bayou Mama's that Thursday night, they were glued to each other. The club was packed, the music was loud, and the air was thick with smoke and humidity."

✅ We *see* their infatuation through actions, not told "they were in love."

**Showing crime scene horror (Prologue):**
> "Her stomach dropped. Not nylon. Hemp. Old hemp rope, frayed at the edges, darkened with something that wasn't tree sap."

✅ Sensory detail (frayed, darkened) creates dread without stating "she was horrified."

**Showing Parrott's predatory nature (Chapter 7):**
> "When police searched his home, they found: Police uniforms. Multiple badges. Emergency lights. Law enforcement equipment. It was a shrine to control. A costume closet for predation."

✅ The evidence *shows* his obsession; the final lines name it without over-explaining.

**WEAKNESSES (Telling Instead of Showing):**

**Chapter 4 - Emotional summary:**
> "The families marked time in their own ways. Barbara Craig started a scrapbook of newspaper clippings about the case. Garland Atkinson Sr. avoided the west side of Houston entirely."

⚠️ This is mostly telling. Would be stronger as: *"Barbara Craig cut out every article. Houston Chronicle, Channel 11, the weekly neighborhood paper. She pasted them into a three-ring binder, page after page of her daughter's name in 18-point font. By 1995, the binder was four inches thick."*

**Chapter 5 - Repetitive telling:**
> "Months passed. Then a year. Cheryl's family marked her birthday in February 1991. Andy's family did the same. The anniversaries were unbearable."

⚠️ This tells us time passed and it was hard, but doesn't *show* a specific unbearable moment. Consider: *"On Cheryl's first birthday after the murder, Barbara baked a cake out of habit. Chocolate with vanilla frosting. Cheryl's favorite. She set it on the table, lit 23 candles, and realized no one was going to blow them out. She left the cake there until the candles burned down to nothing."*

**Chapter 8 - Abstract motivation:**

⚠️ Telling. Show him solving something (rearranging papers, making connections) or cut the line entirely—his actions already demonstrate this.

Occasional filter words appear but aren't excessive:

**RECOMMENDATIONS:**
- **Chapter 4-5:** Replace 3-4 "time passed" summary paragraphs with specific vignette scenes (one failed lead, one family anniversary, one detective frustration moment)

## 🚩 CRITICAL WEAKNESSES (Top 3)

### 1. **Act II pacing drags in middle chapters (4-5)**
**Problem:** The 16-year span (1990-2007) and then 18-year span (2007-2025) creates structural challenge. Chapter 5 repeatedly tells us "years passed, nothing happened" without enough *specific moments* to break monotony. Reader engagement dips here.

**Impact:** Medium-high. The saggy middle risks losing readers who expect momentum.

**Fix:**
- Add 2-3 micro-vignette scenes in Chapter 5: one false lead that almost worked, one heartbreaking family anniversary moment, one detective rage moment (Belk throwing a file, etc.)
- Cut 300 words from Chapter 7's "Psychology" section to tighten Act II overall
- Consider adding a *false hope* beat at 50% mark (2007 breakthrough) that then deflates to sharpen midpoint

**Example rewrite (Chapter 5):**

❌ **Current (telling):**  
*"Months passed. Then a year. Cheryl's family marked her birthday in February 1991."*

✅ **Suggested (showing):**  
*"February 14, 1991. Barbara Craig baked a chocolate cake with vanilla frosting. Cheryl's favorite. Twenty-three candles. She set it on the table, lit them, and realized: no one was coming. She sat there until the wax pooled on the plate and the flames burned out."*

### 2. **Some exposition-heavy passages tell instead of show**
**Problem:** Several sections summarize emotions or events instead of dramatizing them. Chapter 4's "families marked time in their own ways" paragraph is pure exposition. Chapter 5's anniversary descriptions are repetitive summaries. Chapter 8's "Burrow liked puzzles" tells rather than demonstrates.

**Impact:** Medium. Readers are *told* about grief but don't always *feel* it viscerally in these moments.

**Fix:**
- Replace 4-5 telling paragraphs with dramatized scenes (family dinner where Cheryl's absence is palpable, Belk's last day before retirement, etc.)
- Show Burrow's puzzle-solving mind through his actions (rearranging evidence photos, making timeline charts) rather than stating it

### 3. **Minor timeline/procedural clarity issues**
**Problem:** 
- Parrott's incarceration timeline is confusing—sentenced 20 years in 1995, served 3, but was he in prison during the 1996 assault or out on bond? Chapter 5 says both.
- The "recently placed" DNA in 2025 is mentioned repeatedly but never explained—*why* only then? This feels like crucial missing context.
- Detective Burrow appears in Chapter 8 as the hero but is barely characterized—who is he beyond "fresh eyes"?

**Impact:** Low-medium. Doesn't break narrative but creates friction for attentive readers.

**Fix:**
- Add 2-3 sentences clarifying Parrott's exact custody status in 1995-96 period
- Add 1 paragraph explaining the 2025 DNA upload—staffing change? New DA policy? Backlog clearing initiative?
- Give Burrow a character-establishing paragraph (veteran detective? young go-getter? career cold case guy?) when he's introduced

## 📝 FINAL EDITOR NOTES

### What This Story Does Brilliantly:

This manuscript honors Cheryl Henry and Andy Atkinson's humanity while delivering a propulsive, satisfying narrative about justice delayed but ultimately delivered. The research is meticulous, the tone is respectful, and the emotional beats largely devastate. The structural decision to open with discovery (Prologue), flashback to their last night (Chapter 1), then move forward chronologically works beautifully. The 36-year span never feels rushed or dragged—readers feel the *weight* of time.

Detective Belk's 19-year obsession and Shane Henry's lifelong advocacy give the story emotional anchors that prevent it from becoming just another cold case procedural. The villain (Parrott) is appropriately terrifying *because* he's banal—a forgettable middle-aged man who weaponized authority. The ending doesn't cheat: justice comes, but it's bittersweet. The parents are dead. 36 years are gone. No one wins.

### What Needs Work:

The middle chapters (4-5) need narrative variety to sustain engagement across the "cold case years."
